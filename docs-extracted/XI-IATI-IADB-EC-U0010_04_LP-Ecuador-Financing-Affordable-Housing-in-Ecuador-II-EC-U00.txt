                           I. PROJECT RATIONALE AND DESCRIPTION


          Project highlights. The project provides a US$250 million IDB investment guarantee and
          a financing structure that together are expected to mobilize a total of US$750 million in
          additional private resources to expand the supply of mortgages available to middle-income
          households and thus reduce the quantitative housing deficit in Ecuador. The project will
          follow on the achievements of and build on the innovative financial structure developed by
          an earlier IDB operation in Ecuador (4699/OC-EC) in the housing sector, which has yielded
          significant results in improving access to financing and resource mobilization and has
          delivered sector additionalities. The program seeks to achieve outcomes relating to a
          reduction in the quantitative housing deficit in Ecuador, to direct private resource
          mobilization, to an expansion of the mortgage portfolio for the target population, and to the
          placement of mortgage loans for women.



A.        Project description

1.1       Objectives. The general development objective of the operation is to help reduce
          the quantitative housing deficit in Ecuador. This will be achieved through the
          specific development objective of expanding affordable mortgages for
          middle-income households.5

1.2       Main problem addressed: housing deficit in Ecuador. In Ecuador, the housing
          problem manifests itself in a deficit that in 2022 affected 2.34 million homes—
          equivalent to 46.2% of the occupied private homes in the country. This deficit takes
          shape in two dimensions: the quantitative deficit, which relates to the 743,331
          households that need new housing (substandard dwellings); and the qualitative
          deficit, which affects the 1,595,162 existing homes that need structural
          improvements, additions, or basic services to be adequate (recoverable dwellings).

1.3       Housing deficit by income segment of the population. In both the quantitative
          and qualitative dimensions, the housing deficit is a problem that affects various
          income segments of the Ecuadorian population. As can be seen in Table 1, while
          both types of deficits are more prevalent in the lower income ranges,
          middle-income households (those with monthly incomes between US$700 and
          US$2,500 or between 1.5 unified basic salary (SBU)6 units to 5.3 SBU (see
          technical justification of this range in paragraph 1.17)) also suffer from this
          problem. Accordingly, as of 2024, there were approximately 167,000 households
          in the middle-income range with a qualitative housing deficit.

1.4           The problem of housing affordability in Ecuador. Ecuador is facing a major
              housing affordability problem, which is reflected in a significant gap between the
              cost of housing solutions and the ability to pay of a large segment of the population.
              This situation is compounded by several interrelated factors that limit access to
              decent, adequate housing. According to a recent IDB study,7 more than 80% of
              Ecuadorian households cannot access a standard two-bedroom single-family
              home in the formal market. This gap not only reflects a difference in income and
              prices, but also the lack of access to affordable mortgage financing, which prevents
              households from transforming their potential ability to pay into effective housing
              demand. As a result, a significant proportion of households opt for informal
              solutions, self-builds, or substandard housing, feeding into a total housing deficit
              that affects 46.2% of the country’s households.

1.5           Determinant 1: Shortage of affordable mortgage financing for middle-income
              families in Ecuador. As mentioned above, one of the main determinants of the
              housing deficit in Ecuador is the lack of affordable mortgage financing
              (paragraph 1.4), which affects households in various income brackets
              (paragraph 1.3). Among them, middle-income households face a particular
              constraint in the housing market. Even though most of them meet creditworthiness

7     Optional link 3.[1]

          criteria and have formal access to the financial system, they are unable to access
          formal housing because of the lack of affordable mortgages in terms of rates,
          terms, and downpayments. The country’s mortgage portfolio is barely equivalent
          to 2% of gross domestic product (GDP), compared to a regional average of 15%
          to 20%, which points to the market’s shallowness and limited capacity to serve this
          segment. With rates around 10% to 11% and terms of up to 15 to 20 years, debt
          service under these conditions exceeds the threshold of 40% of household income
          that is considered affordable, while downpayments of up to 30% are far beyond
          the reach most households’ savings capacity. Consequently, although middle-
          income families (equivalent to 34% of all households in Ecuador)8 are creditworthy,
          they face a financial system that does not offer suitable conditions for accessing
          formal housing and are excluded from the available supply of this type of financing.
          The mortgage market is small and costly, with a limited supply of long-term credit
          compared to potential household demand. An array of structural characteristics
          and market failures explain this socially suboptimal equilibrium, including notably
          the following:

          a. Financial system geared toward short-term financing. The Ecuadorian
             financial system is characterized by a high concentration of short-term financing
             and an excess of precautionary liquidity. In 2024, nearly 45% of deposits were
             demand or nontime deposits, while long-term deposits represented only 3% to
             5% of total deposits. This structure reflects financial institutions’ preference for a
             conservative maturity matching, mainly financing assets of similar maturity and
             limiting their exposure to long-term loans, such as mortgages. This configuration
             is linked to the structural constraints of the country’s macrofinancial framework,
             including: (i) the limited availability of long-term resources to improve maturity
             matching in the financial system; and (ii) the absence of a lender of last resort,
             along with the limitations on the Central Bank of Ecuador to expansively utilize
             net international reserves in a crisis. These restrictions stem from the official
             policy of dollarization, which, while having contributed to price stability, has
             lowered the system’s capacity to absorb liquidity shocks and promote long-term
             financing. In addition, factors such as macroeconomic volatility and political
             instability (reflected in an average country risk of more than 1,400 basis points
             over the last five years)9 raise risk premiums and drive a preference for liquidity
             in the financial system. In addition, limited institutional savings and shallow
             capital markets restrict the participation of local institutional investors such as
             pension funds and insurance companies, which are the primary demanders of
             long-term financial instruments.

          b. Shallow capital market.10 As a result, the country lacks strong secondary
             market mechanisms essential for expanding mortgage financing.
             Securitization, which in other systems makes it possible to transfer risk and
             free up liquidity by selling mortgage portfolios to institutional investors, remains

              limited. Between 2016 and 2021, mortgage issues totaled just US$891 million
              (equivalent to about 2,000 homes per year), reflecting a shallow market and an
              investor base with no appetite or capacity to absorb mortgage-backed
              securities. Furthermore, there is no legal framework to issue covered bonds—
              low-risk instruments that in other markets provide a stable and long-term
              source of funding. The absence of these mechanisms forces the financial
              system to operate under an originate-to-hold model, where banks keep the
              loans they extend on their balance sheets, thus limiting their capacity to
              originate new mortgage portfolios.

          c. Limited savings capacity of the population to cover downpayments. The
             private financial system in Ecuador generally requires a downpayment of
             approximately 30% of a property’s value. For Ecuadorian households, meeting
             this requirement is difficult due to their limited savings capacity. According to a
             recent survey by Equifax Ecuador (2025),11 22.6% of the population does not
             set aside savings, and only 14.2% hold annual amounts greater than
             US$4,000, thus highlighting households’ limited capacity to build up their own
             capital. In addition, high rates of informal employment limit middle-income
             households’ access to formal mortgages by making it difficult to verify income
             and build a credit history. In Ecuador, the informal employment rate reached
             68.6% in 2024,12 and regional evidence shows that informal workers tend to
             have restricted access to credit,13 which exacerbates housing finance gaps.
             For middle-income households with preferences for liquidity in volatile
             economies, building up that initial capital is difficult even if the installment-to-
             income ratio is manageable. As highlighted in an IDB study,14 macroeconomic
             volatility and income instability in the region push households to prioritize short-
             term precautionary savings over long-term capital accumulation for investment
             projects such as housing. The high downpayment requirement functions as a
             screening mechanism for banks, thereby ensuring that only applicants with the
             highest savings capacity and capital at risk can gain access to financing,
             effectively shutting out most middle-income households from the market.

1.7          Other determinants not addressed by the operation. Certain determinants of
             the quantitative housing deficit in Ecuador for the target population fall outside the
             scope of this operation yet have an influence on the operation’s expected impacts
             in the long term. On one hand, the high costs of land19 and rising construction costs

          (between 15% to 25% for some inputs between 2021 and 2024),20 which represent
          between 30% and 40% of the final home value, limit the affordability of housing for
          middle-income households. Public efforts are under way in the country to address
          this through, for example, regulatory frameworks for zoning, land use, and
          management, and tax incentives for the construction industry. On the other hand,
          significant red tape slows growth of the housing supply. For example, securing a
          building permit in the country takes more than 130 days.21 This calls for public
          interventions to digitalize and simplify bureaucratic procedures, for example.
          Lastly, insufficient financial market regulation in Ecuador22 creates barriers to
          long-term financial intermediation, thus affecting the system’s ability to provide
          affordable mortgages without public intervention, as one example. To address this,
          the country’s guarantee system regulations were recently revised23 to include
          mechanisms and instruments that can promote home loans.

1.8       Proposed solution. The proposed solution consists of a public home financing
          program that targets the middle-income population24 with a view to lowering the
          barriers to access mortgages and expanding long-term financing. The program will
          replicate and scale the structure (paragraphs 2.2–2.4) tested in the operation that
          preceded it, which has yielded significant value added for the country
          (paragraphs 1.12–1.15), and will be implemented through an investment
          guarantee backing a new sovereign debt issue. The related proceeds will be
          channeled through a public management trust and a mortgage securitization
          mechanism to local financial institutions (banks, mutual entities, and cooperatives).
          This will transfer and diversify risk, free up liquidity on banks’ balance sheets, and
          mobilize private resources to create a mortgage portfolio that will include affordable
          conditions (which will be required in the program Operating Regulations for all
          mortgages) in rates, terms, and downpayments that will be passed on to the final
          beneficiary. In addition to the guarantee, this resource execution framework is an
          additionality delivered by the Bank, making the overall financing structure a high
          value-added proposition. This operation includes key lessons (for further details,
          see paragraph 1.15) learned from the execution of the previous guarantee
          operation in terms of: (i) improvements in the efficiency of the financial structure
          and its mobilization capacity, through the IDB’s experience in structuring guarantee
          operations ; (ii) improvements in the operation’s scope, through adjustments to the
          operating regulations to expand the operation’s impact at the level of the target
          population and geographic areas; and (iii) improvements in the operation’s
          monitoring and reporting through the update of the financing framework supporting
          the Secured Instrument (paragraph 1.29); etc.

1.10      Need for public intervention. The nature and prevalence of market failures and
          the market’s structural characteristics prevent the private financial sector from
          expanding mortgage financing and/or providing affordable terms in the short to
          medium term without some type of public intervention. On one hand, intermediary
          financial institutions cannot control factors that directly influence the scarcity of
          long-term financing for them, such as a shallow capital market, scarce long-term
          resources, exposure to external economic cycles, macroeconomic instability, and
          the lack of sufficient institutional investors (paragraphs 1.5(a), 1.5(b)). Against this
          backdrop, a public intervention that provides long-term financing alternatives (such
          as mortgage securitizations) to intermediary financial institutions helps expand
          mortgage origination (paragraphs 1.12–1.14). On the other hand, the standard
          market terms for mortgages set by the intermediary financial institutions are
          incompatible with the characteristics of demand, such as the limited savings
          capacity of many households, which limit housing affordability (paragraph 1.5(c)).
          In this context, public interventions are necessary that provide financial incentives
          to intermediary financial institutions so that they can originate portfolios at lower
          rates, with longer terms, and with lower downpayments.

1.11      Evidence of the effectiveness of the proposed solution. The available
          evidence supports the relevance of this solution. From a theoretical point of view,
          sovereign guarantees help improve the country’s risk perception, lower financing
          costs, and enable additional resources to be leveraged in markets with access
          restrictions.25 At the empirical level specific to the proposed solution, which
          includes the guarantee instrument plus the additionality of the resource execution
          framework, operation 4699/OC-EC demonstrated the viability of this structure and
          achieved a higher resource mobilization than initially foreseen through the
          established mechanism (paragraph 1.13). In addition, the experience of the
          preceding operation shows the capacity of the instrument and the financing
          structure to build capacity and promote development of the mortgage market
          (paragraphs 1.14, 1.15).

C.    Financing instrument or modality

2.5   The selected instrument is an investment guarantee, which will partially cover the
      Republic of Ecuador’s obligations vis-à-vis the Secured Instrument creditors. The
      investment guarantee will be for up to US$250 million from Ordinary Capital and
      have a maximum term of 25 years and a maximum weighted average life of
      15.25 years. The guarantee will bear the sovereign counterguarantee of the
      Republic of Ecuador. The scale of the identified sector and financial needs
      (paragraphs 1.3, 1.16) justify the need for a solution with resource mobilization
      capacity. The investment guarantee instrument is the most appropriate choice
      compared to other IDB financing modalities, since it combines a greater
      mobilization effect with an efficient use of resources. With a guarantee of up to
      US$250 million, at least US$150 million is expected to be directly mobilized in the
      first stage of the financing structure (paragraph 2.3) and, thanks to the
      US$400 million in financing generated by the guarantee, an additional amount of
      approximately US$600 million is expected to be mobilized in the second stage of
      mortgage securitization (paragraph 2.4), leading to a projected total mobilization
      of US$750 million. This scale of total mobilization would not be possible without
      the mobilization generated by the guarantee instrument in the first stage.

B.        Sustainability

3.4       Sustainability of results and financial sustainability. As long as the country
          faces challenging conditions in its macroeconomic, institutional, and social
          environment, the Bank’s intervention remains justified because: (i) there are no
          mechanisms that enable the private sector to provide adequate solutions without
          public intervention and without compromising its financial and commercial viability
          (paragraph 1.10); and (ii) there is a large-scale and significant development
          problem (paragraph 1.3) affecting the target population, which has long-term
          impacts on the country’s economic development and is not sufficiently addressed.
          The structural and rigid nature of such distortions may mean that such solutions
          are consistently required at different levels of magnitude in financial systems with
          differing levels of development.43 With this in mind, elements can be identified in a
          long-term horizon that favor the sustainability of results and financial sustainability
          of a program such as this. These include consolidating mechanisms, capacities,
          and incentives that remain in the system over time so that necessary interventions
          such as this can contribute, in spite of the persistent magnitude of the
          shortcomings, to leaving credit expansion mechanisms in place so that it continues
          under commercial conditions with less dependence on public intervention. In
          operational terms, while the loans are originated under intermediary financial
          institutions’ risk models with amounts and terms calibrated to the ability to pay in
          order to maintain sustainable installment-to-income ratios, they are expected to
          generate payment histories and data that inform intermediary financial institutions’
          risk models and reduce information asymmetries over time. In financial terms, the
          program promotes the use of long-term financing through mechanisms such as
          mortgage securitization, which is expected to contribute over time to the
          development of secondary markets to gradually move from publicly supported
          resources to market-based solutions. In institutional terms, it is key to continue
          establishing common eligibility, origination, monitoring, and performance protocols
          that standardize the affordable housing mortgage product and promote private
          equity crowd-in.

C.        Environmental and social risks

3.6       In accordance with the Environmental and Social Policy Framework (GN-2965-23),
          the operation is classified as financial intermediation (FI) with a low environmental
          and social risk rating. Based on the findings of the environmental and social due
          diligence, the environmental and social impact of the eligible housing under the
          program is anticipated to be Category C. Potential environmental and social
          impacts associated with the program will be managed through an environmental
          and social management system, which will be based on compliance with the IDB’s
          exclusion list, local law, and additional eligibility and exclusion criteria, such as the
          exclusion of Category A or B housing and housing with a substantial or high
          environmental and social risk rating. The program’s environmental and social
          requirements constitute the environmental and social management system, which
          will be included in the program Operating Regulations.

          footnote 37 of the Proposed Policy for a Flexible Guarantee Instrument for
          Sovereign Guaranteed Operations (GN-2729-2), which states that IDB sovereign
          guarantees will not cover debt acceleration. Accordingly, if the secured debtor
          defaults on its obligations, the disbursements under the IDB guarantee will follow
          the original repayment schedule of the Secured Instrument, regardless of its
          acceleration. Accelerating the IDB guarantee would strengthen the structure of the
          guarantee for the market and substantially improve the financial conditions of the
          Secured Instrument for Ecuador.47 Two risks associated with this waiver have been
          considered: (i) greater financial exposure, i.e. concentrated payments; and
          (ii) distorted incentives, i.e. incentives to trigger the guarantee to secure more

          since the amount of the guarantee operation is not of such a volume so as to affect
          the Bank’s balance sheet. Also considered low, the risk related to distorted
          incentives will be mitigated through a short amortization period to be included in
          the counterguarantee agreement to be entered into by the government and the
          IDB. Any payment made by the IDB under the guarantee will be reimbursed by the
          government within 180 days of the date of payment of the guarantee, although the
          IDB, at its sole discretion, may agree to more flexible payment terms.48

                                    I.   FIDUCIARY CONTEXT

  1. Country and sector context

Maturity level of fiduciary systems.
Financial management fiduciary system:
- Budget, treasury, accounting, and reporting: Central government agencies included in the
  General State Budget use the e-SIGEF integrated financial management system, which
  comprises three subsystems. Based on the 2024 evaluation carried out using the Tool for
  Determining the Level of Development and Use of Public Financial Management Systems
  (GUS), it was concluded that these systems are reliable, aligned with international standards,
  highly developed, and adequate to manage IDB-financed projects. The accounting and
  reporting subsystem identifies and tracks financial transactions, ensures the traceability of
  resources by funding source, and is aligned with International Public Sector Accounting
  Standards. It was recommended that a code be assigned to classify expenditures by
  component and to separate transactions by executing agency in projects with multiple
  executing agencies in the organizational and sequential report. Generated from the budget,
  treasury, and accounting modules, this report is the basis for the preparation of project
  financial statements and facilitates the reconciliation thereof with e-SIGEF information.
- Internal control: Under Ecuador’s constitution, the State Comptroller General’s Office (CGE)
  controls the use of public resources in the state and private sector. It leads the administrative
  control system, which includes internal and external audits and internal control, and issues
  the corresponding regulations. The program will have mechanisms set out in the program
  Operating Regulations that define the governance, planning, monitoring, evaluation, and
  fiduciary processes (financial management and procurement) for its execution. In 2025, the
  GUS guide for this subsystem will be applied to support CGE strengthening.
- External control: Government agencies are subject to control and oversight by the CGE,
  which may audit projects financed by multilateral development banks as per its annual audit
  plan. However, it is not currently eligible to audit Bank-financed projects, so executing
  agencies must contract independent Bank-eligible audit firms for reporting. In 2025, the GUS
  guide for this subsystem will be applied to support CGE strengthening.
Procurement management fiduciary system: Due to the nature of the operation, no procurement
will be managed through any local system or regulation, and the Bank's procurement policies
do not apply.
     - Execution environment. Ecuador faces citizen insecurity and is highly exposed to
        natural hazards (floods, landslides, earthquakes, and volcanic activity). These factors
        impact the housing sector through direct damage to the housing stock and, indirectly,
        through delays in construction, appraisals, and insurance in affected areas. Since this
        is a financial intermediation operation, its direct exposure to these risks is limited; in
        addition, the structural demand for housing sustains the capacity to channel resources
        to the intended beneficiaries.
     - Market complexity. The operation will take place against a backdrop of political
        tensions and fiscal pressures that may affect investor confidence in the macroeconomic
        stability and institutional capacity of the State. At the sector level, the financial system
        perceives fragility in the real estate market due to the limited availability of affordable
        mortgage financing for low-income but creditworthy households, which restricts solvent
        demand. However, the operation mitigates these perceptions through a country-tested
        IDB sovereign guarantee, and by offering resources to intermediary financial institutions

   3. Executing agency capacity and performance level

 During the operation’s design phase, the MEF's institutional capacity analysis was updated to
 validate the executing agency's capacity to guarantee both the operation’s technical quality and
 the quality of the trust's operational supervision. It was also corroborated that the execution
 framework adopted for loan contract 4699/OC-EC is relevant to this operation and that the
 fiduciary management of the resources through the public administration trust will be
 maintained.
 Since the guarantee consists of a contingent fund and not the management of resources or
 procurements by the MEF using IDB funds, no fiduciary risks classified as medium-high or high
 were identified.


   4. Budget cycle and flow of funds

 Programming         The Organic Code of Planning and Public Finances establishes the rules
 and budget          governing the programming, preparation, approval, execution, control,
                     evaluation, and liquidation of the budgets. These rules are applicable to the
                     execution of Bank-financed programs in Ecuador and their application in
                     the public sector is standardized through the e-SIGEF system.
                     If the guarantee is triggered, the secured creditor may request the
                     corresponding disbursement without the need for budget certification or a
                     current investment project, since the contingent liability alone is triggered.
 Flow of funds       If it is necessary to trigger the IDB guarantee, the designated representative
                     for the bond creditors or the bank extending the loan, as appropriate, will
                     directly request the IDB to disburse the resources. The IDB will transfer the
                     requested amount as follows: (i) in the case of bonds, the resources will be
                     deposited in a bank account with the sole purpose of managing the
                     guarantee funds, which will be in the name of the representative of the
                     creditors; and (ii) in the case of a loan, the resources will be transferred
                     directly to the lending bank in the account defined by it for such purpose.
