                                  I.         PROJECT DESCRIPTION

     A.     Project Impact, Outcome, and Outputs

1.      On 24 October 2019, the Asian Development Bank (ADB) approved the Promoting Gender
Equality in Housing Finance Project in Kazakhstan for T38 billion ($100 million equivalent). 1 The
significant demand for housing arising from population and household growth, migration, and
urbanization—coupled with rising housing prices, a national mortgage lending market geared to
higher-income groups, and women’s limited access to affordable mortgage finance compared
with men—constrained the Kazakhstan housing market and the achievement of sustainable
economic growth and decreasing national poverty. As a result, the project was developed to
support women’s access to housing, given that secure housing forms the foundation of women’s
safety, health, and economic stability. The project was designed as a financial intermediation loan
to Otbasy Bank 2 for onlending to low- and medium-income women subborrowers for longer-term,
fixed-rate, local currency-denominated mortgage credit. The loan was guaranteed by the
Government of Kazakhstan.

2.      The program’s expected impact was to promote gender equity and achieve sustainable
economic growth, and the expected outcome was to increase the acquisition and renovation of
housing units by women. This project indirectly contributed to the national poverty reduction and
inclusive growth objectives by enabling Otbasy Bank to provide loans to women subborrowers
and promote rural and regional housing mortgage assistance. The expected outputs of the
program were: (i) the long-term affordable mortgage financing for eligible women subborrowers
made available, (ii) the accessibility of Otbasy Bank’s lending products for eligible women
subborrowers improved, and (iii) the gender inclusiveness of the banking industry’s housing
finance assessed.

                                       II.     PROJECT DESIGN

A.        History

3.       Otbasy Bank Housing Construction Savings Bank Joint Stock Company (JSC) [Otbasy
Bank], originally established as Housing Construction Savings Bank of Kazakhstan JSC, was
founded on 16 April 2003 by Decree of the Government of the Republic of Kazakhstan No. 364.
It was created to enhance the long-term financing of housing construction, and it is the only
institution in Kazakhstan that has developed a housing construction savings (HCS) system. The
HCS system involves taking long-term deposits from individuals who are then granted housing
mortgage loans after accumulating sufficient savings for a certain period. This deposit-taking
model is similar to the Bausparkassen (building society) model that is prominent in Europe,
primarily in Germany. Otbasy Bank is 100% state-owned, and Baiterek National Managing
Holding JSC has been its sole shareholder since 2013. As of 31 December 2023, Otbasy Bank
has 19 regional branches, 18 service centers, and 3 service points in the country; it employs 1,537
staff, of which 975 (63%) are women. It is headquartered in Almaty, and its operations are
supported by an extensive network of 1,132 consulting agents and 125 consulting centers, as
well as online channels through which services are provided remotely. In 2023, the bank also
received investment-grade ratings from international agencies—Baa2 with a positive outlook from

B.        Scope of Operations

4.       Otbasy Bank is a key player in the banking system, ranking fourth in assets at nearly T4
trillion ($8.76 billion) as of 31 December 2023, and is one of the 21 second-tier banks in
Kazakhstan. Its operations focus on the provision of housing savings and loans supported by the
government’s provision of long-term, low-cost funding; annual interest bonuses on depositors’
savings; and partnerships relating to state housing programs. As the only entity in Kazakhstan
implementing the HCS system, Otbasy Bank has dominated the market consistently. It issued
62% of the banking industry’s mortgage loans during 2019–2023 and, as of 31 December 2023,
represents 58% of the country’s outstanding mortgage loan portfolio.

C.        Relationship with ADB and Other Lenders

5.     Otbasy Bank’s partnership with ADB was its first venture of securing funding outside
government resources. The partnership was formalized on 15 November 2019 with the signing of
the loan agreement for a loan of T38 billion, for a term of up to 10 years with bullet repayment.
On 23 November 2020, the project was declared effective following the signing of the guarantee
agreement with Kazakhstan’s Ministry of Finance (MOF) on 23 October 2020.

6.      This project has significantly enhanced access to housing finance for women, marking the
first mortgage program in Asia exclusively targeting women. Additionally, it fostered the growth of
provincial regions since 60% of the project funds were allocated to the purchase or renovation of
houses outside the four major cities. The ADB loan also expanded Otbasy Bank’s product line
and, as observed during the ADB-funded regional workshops (para.13), satisfaction with the
project is high among the beneficiaries, and there were repeated calls for the project’s continuity.

7.       As of 31 December 2023, Otbasy Bank had no loans from international financial
institutions other than ADB. All of its borrowings were from domestic sources, including T63 billion
from MOF. 3 Nonetheless, it collaborates with international financial institutions like the European
Bank for Reconstruction and Development, the International Finance Corporation, the United
Nations (UN), and the World Bank to support the country’s housing market. These partnerships
involve economic, social, and environmental assessments, legal and regulatory reviews, business
development, and improvements in lending technologies. Key contributions are the International
Finance Corporation’s guidance on diversifying products and enhancing customer focus, the
European Bank for Reconstruction and Development’s advisory services and capacity building
for operational efficiency and green standards, the UN’s social and environmental assessments,
and the World Bank’s policy reviews. These collaborations have strengthened Otbasy Bank’s
capacity to deliver products and services while aligning with international standards.

D.        Project Design and Formulation

8.     As of January 2023, Kazakhstan’s housing stock reached 405.2 million square meters,
adding 12% since 2014. Despite this increase, urbanization demand remained high while
affordable quality housing was in low supply. Housing availability averaged 21.4 square meters
in 2017 and increased to 23.4 square meters per resident by the end of 2022. Still, this is below
the UN’s standard of 30 square meters. Most houses are also old: 88.3% of multi-apartment

residential buildings and 66% of individual houses were built before 2000. 4 Moreover, housing
prices have surged by 131% for primary housing and by 227% for secondary housing during
2013–2022, driven by inflation and market expectations. 5

9.     While the mortgage loan portfolio of the banking system, involving Otbasy Bank and a few
commercial banks, grew 11.3% in 2022, high interest rates of more than 20% make mortgages
unaffordable for many. 6 State programs offer some relief, but market-oriented loans remain
expensive. Also, the high demand for state programs has created considerable backlogs, which
are exacerbated each year by the steady rise in the number of citizens requiring housing. For
instance, by the end of 2023, 648,000 people were on the waiting list for state-assisted housing. 7

10.     Since women accounted for 51.16% of Kazakhstan’s population in 2023 (10,250,028 out
of 20,033,842 people), 8 it is estimated that about 2.2–2.8 million women 9 are in need of better
housing. This highlights a significant demand for housing improvements and underscores the
importance of targeted interventions to ensure housing affordability and accessibility for women.
Moreover, the present mortgage lending market in Kazakhstan is mainly geared towards the
higher-income groups. Given the significant gender wage gap of 25.2% in 2022, women find it
harder to obtain mortgage finance than men (although women’s educational levels in Kazakhstan
are on a par or higher than those of men). 10 Women’s wages as a percentage of men’s wages
have risen from 2019 to 2022, currently standing at 78.3%. But significant gender wage gaps
remain across various sectors. 11 Hence, the rationale for the project is rooted in substantial unmet
demand for long-term, fixed-rate local currency mortgage loans, particularly among women in
rural areas.

11.      The soundness of the project design is evident in its targeted approach, focusing primarily
on providing affordable housing finance to middle-income women subborrowers and enhancing
Otbasy Bank’s policies and practices on gender and inclusivity. The formulation process was
comprehensive, involving consultations, surveys, and market assessments to craft eligibility
criteria that matched both the project’s purpose and the intended beneficiaries’ needs. As a result,
the design of Otbasy Bank’s new loan product—Umai Women’s Mortgage Program (Umai)—was
anchored on Kazakhstan’s drive for inclusive economic growth and financial inclusion.

                                   III.         PROJECT IMPLEMENTATION

A.         Lending Policies

14.    Otbasy Bank implements state, sector-specific, and its own housing programs with funds
from deposits and loans from the government, Samruk-Kazyna National Welfare Fund JSC, and
Baiterek National Managing Holding JSC; from republican and local budgets; and from ADB.
Otbasy Bank’s operations primarily consist of taking long-term deposits from individuals who are
then granted mortgage loans once they have accumulated sufficient savings. Its offers three loan
products: interim, preliminary, and housing loans.

15.     The ADB-funded Umai (para. 11) falls under preliminary loans because it caters to the
specific eligibility criteria of the funder. State programs are also categorized thereunder. A
dedicated project management unit at Otbasy Bank was set up to oversee Umai’s implementation.
This unit was responsible for administering the program, providing regular progress reports, and
managing any issues in coordination with ADB. The implementation arrangements, as designed
at approval, were comprehensive and adequate to deliver the project outputs. These
arrangements ensured that processes were streamlined and efficient, enabling timely
disbursements and effective monitoring of project progress.

16.      Umai and Otbasy Bank’s other loans are collateralized by the property being purchased
or renovated and the accumulated savings. Due diligence on the subborrower is conducted to
verify their eligibility, solvency, and capacity to pay the loan. The property is also subjected to the
same due diligence. Policies on loan loss provision, write-off, and recovery measures are likewise
established and strictly implemented. The bank complies with the prudential ceilings and ratios of
National Bank of Kazakhstan (NBK), which are also a covenant under the ADB loan.

B.      Characteristics of Subloans

17.      Each loan application met the criteria established by ADB in accordance with section 4 of
the loan agreement between Otbasy Bank and ADB. The applicants and their applications were
subjected to review and assessments to ensure eligibility and the subborrowers’ solvency. In
particular, each application was checked for compliance with the following criteria: (i) the subloans
to be provided solely to women customers, each acting as the sole subborrower and bearer of
the legal title to a residential housing unit; (ii) the subborrower to have a monthly household
income of no more than T320,000 (as per the loan agreement) and T640,000 (as amended); (iii)
the monthly repayment of the mortgage loan not to exceed 55% of the subborrower’s monthly
income; (iv) the subloans to be used to purchase a housing unit or to renovate an existing house;
(iv) the subloan is for financing no more than 80% of the appraised value of a purchased housing
residential unitor renovation cost or 85% if the subborrower is eligible according to her monthly
household income;(v) the price of a housing unit to be capped at T30 million; and (vi) the loan
amount to be capped at T25.5 million for purchase and at T3.7 million for renovation.

18.     In aggregate, Otbasy Bank also had to comply with the following projected profile of the
ADB-funded loan product: (i) a minimum of 60% of the proceeds to be used to purchase or
renovate a house outside the four largest cities with the lowest poverty rates (Aktau, Almaty,
Atyrau, and Nur-Sultan); and (ii) at least 20% of all mortgage loans financed with the ADB loan to
be directed to low-income subborrowers with a monthly household income of less than T200,000
(original, enforced under tranche 1) and T550,000 (amended, enforced under tranches 2 and 3). 15
Likewise, the pass-on rate to the subborrower had a ceiling of 200 basis points plus cost. Over
the project’s 3.08-year implementation period, Otbasy Bank provided 3,356 subloans under
Umai—thus benefitting 3,248 women who celebrated the purchase of a unit and 108 women who
felt more secure in their renovated homes.

C.       Implementation and Internal Operations of Subprojects

19.     Following ADB’s initial disbursement of T8.4 billion on 16 February 2021, Otbasy Bank
launched Umai officially on 12 April 2021 as part of the ADB project. Umai received an
overwhelming 20,710 applications from the start, manifesting the high demand for this kind of loan
product. However, 68% (14,083) of these applications were rejected at the initial screening
because the applicants’ monthly household income exceeded the ceiling of T320,000. After
further validation, only 109 (0.5%) of the 20,710 applications were found to be compliant with
Umai’s eligibility criteria and were processed. Thus, by 26 October 2021, just 64% (T5.4 billion)
of the T8.4 billion first tranche had been on-lent to a total of 620 eligible subborrowers.

20.       Other developments in Kazakhstan also influenced this performance. In February 2021,
the Government of Kazakhstan approved the partial withdrawal of pension savings from the
Unified Accumulated Pension Fund. It was to be used only for certain purposes, such as housing
(buying, building, or renovating homes; paying off mortgages; or using it as a downpayment or
equity for a mortgage loan); medical expenses; and investment management. Thus, by 27
September 2021, 427,000 Kazakhs had withdrawn part of their pension savings, totaling T1.9
trillion ($4.4 billion). 16 This led to an 11.7% increase in the average price of residential real estate
from January 2021 to September 2021.

21.     The subborrowers’ income profile also changed during the implementation of the program,
since the average wage in the country had steadily increased from T162,954 in the first quarter
of 2019 (at the time of loan processing) to T257,030 in the third quarter of 2021. 17 Recognizing
the impact of these developments, the government also adjusted its own social housing programs
(SHPs), including those that were administered by Otbasy Bank. Eligibility under SHP is linked to
the country’s minimum subsistence allowance, which is updated annually. For example, for the
Bakytty SHP, the beneficiary’s income level must be lower than 3 times the minimum subsistence
allowance. As of 1 January 2021, the minimum subsistence allowance was T34,302—up by 13%
from the 2019 figure. Consequently, the 2021 income threshold of SHP beneficiaries increased
by 10%.

22.     In view of the country’s macroeconomic situation and the slow mobilization of the first
disbursement tranche, and in consideration of Otbasy Bank’s request, ADB amended the loan
agreement to increase the monthly household income threshold from less than T200,000 to less
than T550,000 for low-income subborrowers. For middle-income households, the ceiling was
raised from less than T320,000 to less than T640,000. Following the approval of the amendment,
Otbasy Bank submitted a request to disburse the remaining T29.6 billion under the loan. However,
when the request was finalized in January 2022—a time of heightened political and market
uncertainty in Kazakhstan—there was limited appetite for ADB’s bonds in the domestic market.
Though the amount raised (T14 billion) was less than requested, the ADB bonds were issued at
a yield below similar-maturity Kazakhstan government bonds (before issuance expenses), the
benefits of which were passed on directly to the subborrowers. Otbasy Bank deferred the third-
tranche request because of NBK’s high base rate, which would have also been passed on to the
subborrowers. Eventually, the third tranche of T15.6 billion was disbursed on 27 April 2023. With
only 2 months remaining before the loan closing date of 30 June 2023, a 6-month extension until
31 December 2023 was then approved to allow Otbasy Bank additional time to mobilize the funds.

23.     In December 2023, ADB, after several discussions with Otbasy Bank, approved the
reduction in the targeted number of renovation-related subloans from 2,000 to 100. The targeted
number was found to be ambitious in consideration of Otbasy Bank’s historical performance in
this area: its renovation loans had never exceeded 5% of its loan portfolio and, as of September
2023, represented just 2.7% thereof. Also, the collateral requirements—a combination of savings
(equity) and property collateral—were quite onerous in relation to the loan amount (T3.7 million),
which further diminished the marketability of renovation subloans to clients.

24.      By 31 December 2023, Otbasy Bank had issued 3,661 mortgage loans under the Umai
initiative since its inception in 2021—3,356 financed by ADB and 305 financed by Otbasy Bank
through its own funds. The demand for Umai was high among women seeking their own housing,
particularly in rural areas, resulting in a 66.3% project fund utilization in areas outside the four
main cities of Kazakhstan (Aktau, Almaty, Astana, and Atyrau). Affordable housing finance in
urban areas strengthens the low- and middle-income working class by enabling asset-building,
which reduces the post-retirement financial burden. In rural areas, affordable housing finance
helps build and enhance valuable housing assets, thereby boosting living standards, particularly
for women who are predominantly self-employed in agriculture. This segment, characterized by
low and volatile incomes, also benefits from strong family and community networks that provide
resilience in the event of financial stress. As such, housing finance has significantly contributed
to poverty reduction among the low, middle, and informal income-earning population.

        3.      Lending Operations

27.     Otbasy Bank focuses on mortgage lending to the population of Kazakhstan and is the only
participant in the country’s housing construction savings (HCS) market. The loan portfolio
accounted for 78% or T3.1 trillion ($6.8 billion) of its total assets as of 31 December 2023. Otbasy
Bank uses local funds primarily through its HCS system, which is further augmented by state
bonuses (interest premium) that incentivize savings generation. As of year-end 2023, the bank
was servicing 2.7 million active depositors with total deposits of T2.7 trillion ($5.8 billion). In 2023,
486,265 Kazakhs opened a savings account in Otbasy Bank, averaging about 1,900 new
accounts daily. In the same year, 502,000 Kazakh families, of which almost 280,000 are headed
by a woman, were able to improve their living conditions with a loan from Otbasy Bank. The bank’s
deposit portfolio is substantial, and 58.6% of its total depositors are women. The local currency
raised through these deposits and their long-term characteristics are crucial to the bank’s
operations and financial standing, and its ability to further extend its housing loan products to a
broader customer base. Otbasy Bank also benefits from state-supported programs such as Nurly
Zher, 7-20-25, Baspana Hit, and regional youth housing programs, which often include state
funding or guarantees. The bank groups its loan products into three main categories: interim,
preliminary, and housing (Appendix 3, Table 1).

28.     Other operations—Otbasy Bank’s activities revolve solely around its mortgage lending.

                     V.         EVALUATION OF PROJECT PERFORMANCE

A.      Relevance

40.       The project is rated highly relevant. Affordable quality housing in Kazakhstan was in low
supply. Housing prices in Kazakhstan surged from 2013 to 2022—rising by 131% for primary and
by 227% for secondary housing—driven by inflation and market expectations. State programs
offer some relief, but market-oriented loans remain expensive. Furthermore, women found it
difficult to obtain affordable mortgage finance because of constraints in accessing banks’ financial
services, prolonged and complicated procedures for commercial loan approvals, and excessive
paperwork. In rural areas, women were even more disadvantaged in accessing mortgage
financing since most of them were self-employed and had informal income sources. With lower
income levels, women faced higher collateral requirements to be eligible for a loan. The project
supported the adoption of an innovative mortgage program (Umai) that exclusively targeted
women subborrowers and filled critical gender gaps in the housing finance market. Thanks to the
program, women who were ineligible for loans from the heavily subsidized programs of the
government—and from banks given their bias to subborrowers with a more formal and steady
income source—finally had access to housing finance.

41.     The provision of affordable housing remains a key agenda for the government. In 2020, it
launched the “5-10-20” state housing program, which stands for 5% loan rate, 10% downpayment,
and 20-year loan term. This was followed by the government’s approval of the partial withdrawal
of pension savings for the purpose of acquiring or renovating a home. Improving the housing
infrastructure system is also included in the government’s concept for the development of housing
and communal infrastructure for 2023–2029. 19

42.     The project is fully aligned with ADB’s Strategy 2030 and its operational priorities (OPs)
of addressing remaining poverty and reducing inequalities (OP1), accelerating progress in gender
equality (OP2), and strengthening governance and institutional capacity (OP6). 20 Housing
security is vital for women’s safety, health, and economic stability, as are targeted interventions
for housing finance and gender-responsive policies. The project also aligns with ADB’s country
partnership strategy, 2023–2027 for Kazakhstan, which emphasizes resilient and sustainable
growth with gender equality as a central theme. 21 It also calls for affordable housing finance for
women and youth through a sovereign-guaranteed modality.

43.     The project design and its scope were appropriate for achieving the intended outcome of
giving women access to housing finance. Otbasy Bank, the project’s executing and implementing
agency, is a key player in the housing market of Kazakhstan, particularly in helping citizens
purchase their first homes. It met all of ADB’s financial intermediary eligibility criteria, as outlined
in the Operations Manual. 22 The design and monitoring indicators were also well drafted,
measurable, and directly relevant to achieving the outcome of the project, with specific targets for
loans in rural areas. The project successfully ensured gender inclusivity through the
implementation of the GAP. Although it underwent minor changes during implementation, this
was necessary to adjust to developments in the housing sector (para. 22). Furthermore, ADB
innovatively and befittingly designed the funding features for the project. It was one of the first (1

B.      Effectiveness

44.     The project is rated effective. It successfully facilitated (i) the acquisition and/or renovation

beneficiaries and contributed to significant achievements in mortgage loan issuance. In summary,

annually would translate into 8% annual growth in the lending portfolio to women (Table 1); and
into loans to women subborrowers accounting for 59% of the overall loan portfolio, with 1% annual

a greater proportion of women’s loans in the overall loan portfolio. While outstanding loans to
women (Table 2) more than doubled from T697 billion (2020) to T1.64 trillion (2023), as a
percentage of the overall portfolio (53%) they fell short of the 59% target. This may be partially
explained by the gender gap in wages—women tend to be granted smaller loans than men
because they earn lower wages than men on average. Although Otbasy Banks’s outreach to
women consistently exceeded that to men (in both loans issued and outstanding), men’s higher
income allows them to obtain bigger loans. Despite being funded and supported by the
government, Otbasy Bank strictly employs prudent credit underwriting policies and procedures
that review subborrowers’ cash flow, solvency, and capacity to pay the loan. Nonetheless, Otbasy
Bank substantially delivered since it still accomplished 90% of its target (53% versus 59%).

                 VI.        ISSUES, LESSONS, AND RECOMMENDATIONS

A.      Issues and Lessons

54.     The project encountered two main issues, the originally set income ceiling of subborrowers
                                                                                                15


and the low amount of renovation loans. Both issues highlight the need for ADB to be flexible and
responsive when faced with changing realities so that the project’s funds are ultimately delivered
to the envisaged beneficiaries.

55.    External shocks such as the COVID-19 pandemic and the Russian invasion of Ukraine
are impossible to predict, but it is possible to mitigate adverse socioeconomic effects with better
contingency planning and a predetermined crisis response that supports both the borrower and
the subborrowers.

56.    In addition, Otbasy Bank should enhance its communication and raise awareness among
potential subborrowers, especially the younger ones, of the number of subborrowers it can
accommodate under its lending programs by conducting detailed media campaigns and
community outreach programs. This will help it manage the public’s expectations more effectively.

B.     Recommendations

57.      Integrating gender and climate priorities in future projects. Future iterations could
incorporate green building standards and energy-efficient solutions, address gender inequality,
and promote inclusive growth to significantly improve the quality of life for women and their
families. In response to Kazakhstan’s Strategy for Carbon Neutrality 2060, future projects could
offer financing for energy-efficient housing. Products like energy-efficient home financing and
retrofit loans would promote sustainable practices. These could provide incentives for installing
solar panels, improving insulation, and implementing other eco-friendly improvements in
alignment with national environmental goals. Apart from mortgage and other affordable housing
initiatives, future demand for housing could be nurtured by developing financial literacy programs
for women and vulnerable groups. Targeted financial literacy initiatives would support women and
vulnerable groups, equipping them with the knowledge needed to navigate mortgage options and
financial planning for homeownership.

58.    Mortgage market reforms in Kazakhstan. The mortgage loan market is dominated by
Otbasy Bank, which has issued on average 62% of the banking system’s mortgage loans since
2019. To better develop this market, the government should support the increased participation
of banks. This would generate healthy competition and ultimately benefit potential homeowners
and the economy. Hence, ADB should continue the policy dialogue with the government for
mortgage market reforms to create a realistic and competitive landscape.

59.  Covenants. The current covenants are and remain relevant and sufficient for project
management.

60.     Future monitoring. No further reporting required. But ADB could nonetheless evaluate
further developments in housing, mortgages, and climate financing, and an extension of its
partnership with Otbasy Bank.

61.     Further action or follow-up. Since demand for affordable housing remains high and
given ADB’s focus on climate, which can be integrated in the housing programs, the project team
should explore continued collaboration with Otbasy Bank and other financial institutions that could
lead to realistic competition.

62.    Timing of the project performance evaluation report. The project performance
evaluation report was timely.

                              GENDER ACTION PLAN ACHIEVEMENTS

A.      Introduction

1.       As of 1 January 2024, 10,250,028 women lived in Kazakhstan, constituting 51.16% of the
country’s total population, a 1.5% increase from 2022. 1 Women represent 48.54% of the
economically active population, 48.3% of all employed individuals, and 49.4% of all hired workers.
However, gender disparities persist in employment and wages. The unemployment rate at the
end of 2023 was 4.7%, with a higher rate for women (5.3%) compared to men (4.2%). The
average monthly salary for men was T361,481, whereas for women it was T285,744. The largest
wage gaps are in financial and insurance activities, the mining industry, and professional,
scientific, and technical activities. Women’s salaries surpass men’s only in the education sector.

2.     In terms of financial inclusion, women accounted for 59% of depositor clients and 57% of
borrower clients at the end of 2022. Although men and women are subject to the same mortgage
lending terms, analysis shows that women’s average loan amounts are smaller (T15.8 million vs.
T17 million), and female borrowers tend to be older (44 and 40 years respectively) and more likely
to be co-borrowers (31% and 21% respectively). Women’s initial down payments are also lower
than men’s.

3.      As of January 2023, Kazakhstan's housing stock reached 405.2 million square meters,
growing 12% over the last 10 years. Despite this increase, urbanization demand keeps being high
and affordable quality housing is in low supply. Housing availability averaged 21.4 square meters
in 2017 and even though it increased to 23.4 square meters per resident by the end of 2022, they
are below the UN's standard of 30 square meters. Many homes are old, since 88.3% of multi-
apartment residential buildings and 66% of individual houses were built before 2000. Housing
prices surged from 2013 to 2022, with primary housing rising by 131% and secondary by 227%,
driven by inflation and market expectations. The mortgage market, dominated by Otbasy Bank
with a 66% share of the mortgage market, and a few commercial banks, grew 11.3% in 2022, but
high-interest rates (over 20%) make mortgages unaffordable for many. Since 2005, the
government of Kazakhstan has implemented various initiatives to enhance access to housing,
including rental schemes, savings plans, interest rate subsidies, and support for private housing
developers. State programs offer some relief, but market-oriented loans remain expensive.
Furthermore, high demand to access these programs has created considerable backlogs, which
are exacerbated each year by the steady rise in the number of citizens requiring housing. For
instance, by the end of 2023, 646,000 people were on the waiting list for state-assisted housing.

4.      With women constituting 51.16% of the total population of Kazakhstan (footnote 1), it is
estimated that around 2.2-2.8 million women 2 are in need of better housing. This highlights a
significant demand for housing improvements, particularly among women, underscoring the
importance of targeted interventions to address housing affordability and accessibility for women
in Kazakhstan. Furthermore, the present mortgage lending market in Kazakhstan is mainly geared
toward higher income groups. Given the significant gender pay gap of 25.2% in 2022, women
face lower access to affordable mortgage finance compared to men (although women’s
educational levels in Kazakhstan are on par or higher than those of men). 3

7.        Outcome Indicators. In summary, of the 2 outcome of performance indicators, 1 was

Thus, the target to issue at least 28,000 (Table 1) subloans to women annually translating to a

consistently superseded men (issued and outstanding), the latter’s higher income allows them to

employs prudent credit underwriting policies and procedures which review subborrowers cash

delivered, accomplishing 90% of its 59% target.

8.     Output 1: Long-term affordable mortgage financing for eligible women borrowers
made available. Otbasy Bank provided 3,248 subloans to subborrowers for purchasing housing

9.       The program underwent some changes that were agreed upon between ADB and Otbasy
Bank and reflected on the amended loan agreement to better adapt to the needs of the population.
Initial disbursements to subborrowers were slow. On 12 April 2021, Otbasy Bank officially
launched Umai Women’s Mortgage Program for their customers. However, in June 2021 ADB
received a request by Otbasy Bank to review the net household income ceiling of T200,000, since
Umai received 20,710 applications, but 68% of them were rejected at initial screening due to
having a higher household income. Subsequently, only 109 applications (0.5% of the total) met
the eligibility criteria and validation. As of 26 October 2021, T5.4 billion (64%) of the T8.4 billion
that were disbursed on the first tranche had been disbursed to 620 sub-borrowers. Furthermore,
in February 2021 the government of Kazakhstan approved the partial withdrawal of the pension
savings from the Unified Accumulate Pension Fund. The withdrawals could be used for home
renovations, down payments for a mortgage loan, or to purchase residential units. The
subborrowers income profile also changed during the program’s implementation, since the
average wage in the country had steadily increased from T162,954 in the first quarter of 2019 to
340,556 in the first quarter of 2023. Consequently, ADB in December 2021, in view of the
country’s macroeconomic situation and the slow disbursement, approved a minor change
increase the monthly household income threshold to T550,000 for low-income sub-borrowers and
to T640,000 for middle-income households. In December 2023, another minor change was
approved to lower the renovation subloans from 2,000 to 100. Historically, demand for renovation
loans has been low due to the onerous requirement of collateralizing the housing unit vis-à-vis
the lower loanable amount. It had never exceeded 5% of Otbasy Bank’s loan portfolio and, as of
September 2023, represented just 2.7% thereof.

  D.       Challenges, Lessons Learned and Recommendations

  15.    Given the availability of sex disaggregated data, significant market share and Otbasy
  Bank’s key role in providing housing loans, continued policy dialogue with Otbasy Bank and the
  government is recommended. This can lead to further developing and introducing reforms in the
  mortgage market which can bring forth increased participation of banks and may ultimately benefit
  the potential homeowners and the economy.
