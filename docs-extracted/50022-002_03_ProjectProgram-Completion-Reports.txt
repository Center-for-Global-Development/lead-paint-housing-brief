                         I.       PROGRAM DESIGN AND IMPLEMENTATION

1.      The Affordable Rural Housing Program (ARHP) was the first housing sector program
financed by the Asian Development Bank (ADB) using the results-based lending (RBL) modality.
It was also the first RBL in Uzbekistan and in Central and West Asia. Increasing affordable rural
housing was a high-priority in Uzbekistan’s Reform Program for Structural Transformation of the
Economy for 2015–2019, to achieve inclusive growth and greater diversification of the economy.
The government’s strategy focused on accelerating rural development, increasing rural job and
livelihood opportunities, and improving rural living conditions to provide an attractive alternative
to urban migration.

2.       The program supported a portion of the government’s State Affordable Rural Housing
Program (SARHP), which targeted the construction of 75,000 rural dwelling units in all 13 regions
of the country, using six state-owned commercial banks to provide mortgage loans. The SARHP
aimed to improve the quality of life in rural areas through rural housing construction, employment
generation, and private sector development. Under ADB’s ARHP, three state-owned banks
(“participating commercial banks” [PCBs])–Ipoteka Bank, National Bank of Uzbekistan (NBU),
and Qishloq Qurilish Bank (QQB)–provided loans targeting the construction of at least 29,000
dwelling units in nine regions of the country: Bukhara, Fergana, Karakalpakstan, Kashkadarya,
Khorezm, Namangan, Samarkand, Surkhandarya, and Tashkent.1 The construction was carried
out by private contractors and supervised by Qishloq Qurilish Invest (QQI), the State Design
Institute of Qishloq Qurilish Loyiha, and the Ministry of Construction.

A.       Rationale

3.     Poverty rates in Uzbekistan are higher in rural than in urban areas (16.0% versus 11.9%)
and the rural population continues to grow.2 Rural housing is on average older and often has a
large number of occupants due to multi-family arrangements in single households. In 2022, only
59% of rural housing had direct access to potable water, 27% had sanitary human waste disposal,
37% had modern heating, and 37% had access to natural gas.3

4.      Although more than 69,000 single-family homes were built under the Housing for
Integrated Rural Development Investment Program (HIRDIP) from 2009 to 2016, supported by a
$500 million ADB loan, there remained a pressing need for affordable rural housing.4 To improve
affordability, the dwelling units in the SARHP were smaller houses than those in HIRDIP and
apartments in buildings with between four and nine stories. As in HIRDIP, the government
provided the land at no cost, bore the cost of associated infrastructure (i.e., utilities and streets
and roads), provided income tax exemption for loan repayments, and exempted all goods, works,
and services under the program from customs duties, value added tax, and mandatory
contributions to state trust funds (e.g., social tax).

5.       Using ADB lending, the government provided the PCBs with credit lines at preferential
rates. 5 These low-cost loans to the PCBs enabled them to make loans to homeowners with
duration of 20 years, a 1-year grace period, and an interest rate of less than the refinancing rate
for the first 5 years.6 These measures made the dwelling units affordable for vulnerable low-
income and single-parent families, which were selected (in cases where the demand for ARHP
dwelling units exceeded the supply) in accordance with social prioritization criteria adopted under
the program. In 2021, the cost of a two-room, 42.6 m2 apartment averaged SUM148.3 million
(approximately $14,000), while the cost of a three-room, 52.8 m2 apartment averaged SUM180.2
million (approximately$17,000) (using the average exchange rate for 2021). However, not all
families qualified because they did not have sufficient savings for the down payment (at least
10%), and income to repay the mortgage loan.

6.       In addition to providing homes, HIRDIP and SARHP fulfilled the government’s goal of
stimulating rural employment to increase rural incomes, providing jobs for returning migrants, and
improving the knowledge of unskilled laborers. For every 10,000 houses constructed under the
state rural housing program, an estimated 150,000 jobs were created, including 44,600 in
construction.7 Thus, based on the estimate in paragraph 2, the SARHP provided more than 1
million jobs, including more than 300,000 in construction.

7.      ARHP was aligned with five out of the seven ADB Strategy 2030 operational priorities: i)
addressing remaining poverty and reducing inequalities, ii) accelerating progress in gender
equality, iii) tackling climate change and building climate and disaster resilience, iv) promoting
rural development, and v) strengthening governance and institutional capacity.8 The program was
able to support those operational priorities because of the choice of the RBL modality, which
focuses on the delivery of specific and measurable results. The RBL enabled the program to
support not only the construction of dwelling units, but also on gender equality (by linking a portion
of loan disbursements to progress on lending to women borrowers), climate change (by linking a
portion of loan proceeds to climate change assessments on housing sites),9 and strengthening
governance and institutional capacity (by linking loan disbursements to institutional development
in the banks and the principal construction supervisor QQI, and in procurement reforms).

B.       Program Impact, Outcome, and Output

8.       The design and monitoring framework (DMF) is in Appendix 1. When the loan agreement
was signed in July 2017, the program contemplated building one- and two-story houses on
smaller lots than had been used in HIRDIP. Starting in 2019, four-story multi-apartment residential
buildings were built in addition to the one- and two-story houses. Starting in 2020, only two-, three-
, four-, five-, and seven-story apartment buildings were constructed. The envisaged impact of the
program was improved quality of life in rural areas of Uzbekistan by providing affordable housing.

11.      The DMF had six performance indicators for Output 1, all of which were achieved. 29,260

were connected to electricity, had access to gas and connected to a piped water supply.

C.       Program Costs and Financing

14.     Program costs were significantly lower than appraisal estimates (Appendix 2). This is
principally because of a sharp devaluation in the value of the Uzbek currency and because of a
change in the type and design of the dwelling units as described in para. 8.

15.     According to the original financing plan, the program was expected to cost $1,064 million,
$240 million (22.5%) of which would be provided by the government, homeowners would provide
$175 million (16.5%), commercial banks would provide $149 million (14%), and ADB would
provide $500 million (47%) (Appendix 3). Actual program costs totaled only $638 million, $78
million (12%) of which was provided by the government, $56 million (9%) by homeowners, none
(0%) from commercial banks, $470.3 million (74%) from ADB, and $33 million (5%) from the
Kuwait Fund for Arab Economic Development. Utility and road construction was funded by the
government and the Kuwait Fund for Arab Economic Development, while housing construction
was funded by the other financiers. About 979.1 kilometers of roads were built according to
government standards for access to and within new rural housing sites (massives).14

22.      The program aimed to accelerate rural development, increase rural job and livelihood

together with a family member who had a disability of the first degree; and (vii) who lived in a

K.     Climate Change

31.     Around 117,000 people benefited from strengthened environmental sustainability
(Strategy 2020 operational priority 3.3). Under the program, climate change risk assessments in
selecting sites where dwelling units would be constructed were adopted for the first time in
Uzbekistan. For each prospective building site, Uzhydromet (a government agency) conducted a
climate change risk assessment. Sites that were assessed to have a high climate risk were not
recommended for approval for construction. Sites assessed to have a medium risk were
recommended for approval provided that required risk mitigation measures were identified, while
sites that were assessed to have a low climate risk were recommended for approval without
additional measures. The IVA concluded that the overall technical quality of climate change risk
assessments was sufficient to provide a basis for concluding that construction sites had been
adequately screened. Uzhydromet reported that it believed that its recommendations had been
influential, especially in mountainous areas.

44.     The program is rated likely sustainable. The envisaged outcome of the program was an
increased number of rural families meeting the social equity criteria benefit from affordable
housing constructed in accordance with national quality standards. This outcome was achieved.
In particular, the good quality of the construction means that the housing should last a long time.

provision of subsidies for vulnerable groups. The new program integrates ARHP’s developed
policies and procedures, including the social prioritization criteria for homebuyers and the

social aspects. 34 Under the new public procurement law, public procurement procedures in
Uzbekistan expected to improve further. Home constructions in high-risk climate areas, identified
by climate change assessment, were banned and design improvements for better insulation and
energy efficiency were introduced, ensuring environmental sustainability.

                   III.     ISSUES, LESSONS, AND RECOMMENDATIONS

A.     Issues and Lessons

53.      RBL program is well suited if it is embedded into a wider government owned and led
program. RBL can be an effective modality for achieving both physical outputs and nonphysical
goals. RBL can also make changes in national policies that go beyond the project’s physical
scope. The climate risk assessments, the procurement processes, and the beneficiary selection
criteria were also used in non-ADB financed provinces. The instructions issued and policies
adopted by the government were “national” in nature and impact. The indirect contribution of this
was the emergence and professional growth of construction companies, forming the basis for
subsequent mortgage market development, causing behavioral change within and neighboring
communities towards living conditions.
                                                                                                  15



54.      The borrower is not obligated to request disbursement of the full amount related to the
fulfillment of any DLI indicator. Accordingly, ADB disbursement projections can be overly
optimistic. This point should be considered in future revisions of RBL policies.

55.    By building the capacity of Uzhydromet staff, the quality and relevance of climate change
risk analysis improved, leading to effective inputs into the construction design, such as adding
wall and roof insulation and strengthening the roof structure.

56.     ADB’s participation in a country-driven program by bringing knowledge and experience in
addition to financing catalyzes interest and cofinancing from other development agencies. ADB’s
ARHP was a critical element for enhancing the entire ecosystem of housing financing and
mortgage market, laying a groundwork for diversified funding and private sector involvement.

57.  Promotion of a social equity criteria and gender quotas is instrumental in economic
empowerment of vulnerable groups and women.

B.     Recommendations

58.     Future projects should carefully assess opportunities to reduce carbon emissions.
To reduce carbon emissions, climate mitigation measures should be as much as possible part of
the proposed construction design. For instance, renewable energy sources such as solar
photovoltaic panels and solar water heaters are important features that should be integrated in
the design of houses and buildings not only because they lead to reduced energy costs and
savings, but because, at scale, they can significantly contribute to a reduction in carbon emissions
that can be directly linked to Uzbekistan’s Nationally Determined Contributions and related climate
action targets, while also contributing to reducing air pollution in regional capitals of Uzbekistan.

59.    While the use of personal protective equipment (PPE) is a requirement under Uzbekistan’s
workplace regulations, the IVA found PPE and workplace safety measures to be inconsistently
enforced in ARHP construction sites. Future construction projects should include fostering a
workplace safety culture by setting up workplace safety plans, training construction managers
and workers on workplace safety and accident prevention, while also ensuring consistent use of
PPE such as hard helmets, visibility vests, steel toe boots, and other required equipment.

60.    Future programs should have baseline, interim, and final social impact assessments.

61.     Although the IVA was used to verify DLI achievements, it would be beneficial to engage
civil society organizations in monitoring and evaluation of the implementation of application
guidelines, including compliance with gender quota implementation. Important program
documents should be made available in the most used local language and published on partners’
and ADB websites. In addition to ensuring transparency and an increased public oversight and
accountability, there may be a need also to create more social media coverage of ABD’s
programs, policies and development priorities.

62.    Covenants. The loan covenants should be maintained in their existing form.

63.    Further action or follow-up. None.

64.    Timing of the project performance evaluation report. The project performance
evaluation report may be prepared immediately.

Description
The technical assistance (TA) was designed to support the effective implementation of all three outputs of
the Affordable Rural Housing Program (ARHP) but was directly linked to output 3 (strengthened national
capacity in program management and performance monitoring). The proposed TA activities included
support for strengthening program systems to effectively manage and implement the ARHP, primarily in
monitoring activities relating to the disbursement-linked indicators (DLIs), non-DLIs, program and
procurement action plans, and safeguards and fiduciary aspects. The consultants hired under the TA also
conducted independent evaluation of DLI achievement.

Expected Impact, Outcome, and Outputs
The TA supported ARHP in achieving its envisaged impact and outcome. The envisaged impact was
improved quality of life in rural areas of Uzbekistan by providing affordable housing. The intended
outcome was increased number of rural families meeting the social equity criteria benefit from affordable
housing constructed in accordance with national quality standards. The TA had four outputs: (i) to assist
the government in effective implementation of the ARHP through a management and performance
monitoring system (MPMS) to enable comprehensive program monitoring, analysis, and information
reporting and sharing; (ii) capacity building for strengthening program systems (including fiduciary,
safeguards, and gender) in the three participating commercial banks (PCBs) and Qishloq Qurilish Invest
(QQI); (iii) technical advice to QQI in complying with the action plan to enhance governance, financial
management, accounting, and reporting; and (iv) independent verification of DLIs.

Implementation Arrangements
The TA with original completion date of 31 March 2022, was implemented from 1 June 2017 to 31
December 2022 with one extension for 9 months to coincide with the extended loan closing date and also
the financial year of the government, resulting in overall TA implementation period of 5.6 years. In August
2017 the TA amount was increased by $750,000 to a combined amount of $1,500,000 to finance
increased consulting inputs approved through a minor change in scope and implementation.

The TA initially planned to provide 14 person-months for 6 international and 20 person months for 4
national verification consultancy inputs, as well as 9 person-months for 3 international and 12 person-
months for 2 national capacity building and program implementation consultancy inputs. With TA
increase1 a number of consultancy inputs increased to 24 person-months for 7 international and 34
person months for 9 national verification consultancy inputs with 15.5 person-months for 4 international
and 49 person-months for 5 national capacity building and program implementation consultancy inputs

                        GENDER EQUALITY RESULTS AND ACHIEVEMENTS

1.      The Affordable Rural Housing Program (ARHP) was classified as effective gender
mainstreaming (EGM). For EGM for a results-based loan (RBL), at least one disbursement-linked
indicator (DLI) or verification protocol must include a gender-specific performance indicator. 1
In addition, the program action plan of EGM RBLs should include specific and monitorable gender
actions to support the achievement of gender-related DLIs and other gender performance
indicators in the Design and Monitoring Framework (DMF). A gender action plan is not required,
and none was prepared for ARHP. However, the gender specialist in the program’s Management
and Monitoring Unit monitored the two gender-related DLIs (DLIs 2 and 3) and the three gender-
related actions (21, 22, and 23) in ARHP’s Program Action Plan.

2.      Historically, property in Uzbekistan has been held in the name of a man; as of 2022,
the percentage of land owned by women was 27.2% compared to 72.8% for men. 2 ADB has
encouraged the government to eliminate this gender disparity.3 In the predecessor ADB housing
program, the average share of women home buyers from 2011 to 2015 was 23%.4 In ARHP, DLI
3 required that the average share of women home buyers increase to at least 30% over the 2018–
2021 period. According to data supplied by the three participating commercial banks, the share
of women home buyers among ARHP home buyers was 59% (Table A7.1).

9.      ARHP had also a major positive impact on women. Women became active in the public
information campaign for the rural housing program and were encouraged to apply to purchase
the new dwelling units. The dwelling units built under ARHP had piped water, which significantly
reduced the household workload and time burden on female beneficiaries. Health and hygiene
culture improved because the houses and apartments included indoor toilets, bathrooms, and hot
water. Living in a house or apartment built under the rural housing program has become a dream
goal for more than 29,000 families.

10.     The dwelling units provided homeowners with modern kitchens and piped water, which
lead to a healthier environment for the family and significant time savings for women who
previously needed to use outdoor kitchens and water pumps to bring water from outside.
The dwelling units also provided sufficient space for women to start small home-based
businesses, such as sewing and selling baked goods. Stores, local food markets, bakeries, and
mini-banks started in rural communities are expected to create jobs, many of them for women.
Better roads and streetlights in rural areas contribute to enhanced security for women and girls.
New playgrounds and sports facilities benefited both girls and boys.
