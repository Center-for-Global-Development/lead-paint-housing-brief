                               IBRD PROGRAM DOCUMENT FOR A PROPOSED LOAN TO CROATIA
 I. COUNTRY CONTEXT AND OPERATION SUMMARY
1.       The proposed Development Policy Loan (DPL) with a Catastrophe Deferred Drawdown Option (Cat DDO)
supports the Government of Croatia (GoC) in strengthening the country’s disaster preparedness and resilience. This Cat
DDO operationalizes the commitment of the Country Partnership Framework (CPF) for Croatia FY25–FY30 to support
increased resilience and institutional capacity for inclusive and sustainable growth. Addressing some of the critical
challenges that could prevent the country from sustaining its strong economic growth and social progress, the Cat DDO
reflects the CPF’s highly selective approach by focusing on reforms for disaster preparedness and response capacities,
resilient infrastructure and services that can withstand emergencies, and strengthening of financial resilience. Accordingly,
Pillar 1—Strengthening institutional capacity for disaster preparedness and resilience—will support foundational reforms
that increase the disaster preparedness of critical infrastructure and services, and that build institutional capacity for
climate adaptation (CA). Pillar 2—Enhancing disaster preparedness and climate resilience in key economic sectors—will
support reforms that address vulnerabilities and foster sustainable development in the energy, tourism, and housing
sectors. Pillar 3—Strengthening the financial resilience of businesses and households—will advance reforms in the
insurance and social protection sectors to enhance coping and recovery capacity for disaster and climate impacts. These
reforms are complemented by the Cat DDO as a contingent financing mechanism that can supplement budget reserves
and enhance fiscal resilience in case of major disasters.
2.      Advancing reforms across key dimensions of disaster preparedness and resilience, the Cat DDO contributes to
the mission and priorities of the World Bank Group (WBG), particularly as they relate to crisis preparedness, the private
sector, and global public goods. The Cat DDO operationalizes the WBG Crisis Preparedness and Response Toolkit by
enabling the Rapid Response Option (RRO).1 It implements recommendations of the 2025 Croatia Country Climate and
Development Report (CCDR) and leverages the ongoing WBG’s engagement, which includes providing Croatia with support
for disaster risk management (DRM), governance, energy, tourism, construction and land, and social protection,
complementing efforts of partners. The Cat DDO will also contribute to the body of knowledge on innovative approaches
to strengthening disaster preparedness and resilience, thereby enabling risk-informed and systematic public action and
the private sector’s contribution, while providing a springboard for future ambitious reforms.
3.       Croatia’s exposure to hazards, history of disasters, and projected future climate combine to highlight the need
for better disaster preparedness and resilience, adaptation, and mitigation. Croatia is exposed to earthquakes, floods,
landslides, extreme heat, droughts, and wildfires. Some 0.9 million people (20 percent of the population) live in high-
flood-risk zones. Heat waves resulted in 830 deaths per year on average between 2022 and 2024.2 The 2014 floods caused
€340 million in damage;3 a wildfire in 2018 burned 67,666 ha and caused €109 million in losses; and a drought in 2022
caused damage of over US$300 million. Changes in climate are projected to intensify such hazards. Several key sectors are
particularly vulnerable to the observed and anticipated climate risks. A projected rise in average temperatures of 1.5°C by
2040 and over 2°C by 2070 could drive more frequent heat waves, droughts, and extreme weather events. More frequent
floods and extreme heat threaten infrastructure, increasing maintenance costs and the risk of service disruptions. Coastal
areas already face increased risk of sea-level rise, which affects infrastructure, agriculture, and tourism through flooding,
erosion, and saltwater intrusion. These sectors could also be affected by increasing wildfire risks. Reduced rainfall and
longer droughts pose additional risks for agriculture, water resources, energy, and health. The CCDR suggests changes in
climate could lower gross domestic product (GDP) by 1.5–2.1 percent by 2050, which is a conservative estimate.

4.       Disaster impacts on Croatia’s infrastructure and buildings could disrupt public services and business operations.
Infrastructures critical for effective emergency response (like fire stations, police stations, hospitals, and schools) are
exposed to multiple hazards, as are roads and energy networks. Almost all emergency response infrastructure is exposed
to seismic risk, and more than two-thirds is exposed to wildfire risk.4 There is a clear need to ensure the operational and
physical resilience of this infrastructure and to avoid disruption of the critical public services that people and the economy
depend on. One-third of the building stock predates seismic provisions, and just 5–10 percent complies with modern
building codes. In the March 2020 earthquake, the housing sector suffered most of the infrastructural damage (64
percent). 513 buildings in the education sector and 214 buildings in the health sector were also damaged.5
5.      Disaster and climate risks translate into macro-fiscal risks and have impacts on welfare; thus, financial and social
protection instruments that support quick response and recovery are crucial. Across all hazard types, disasters are
estimated to cause economic losses of about 0.8 percent of GDP per year. In 2020, earthquakes alone caused an estimated
€16.1 billion in damage and losses (equivalent to 24 percent of GDP).6 Between 2014 and 2025, the GoC requested
European Union (EU) support several times through the Emergency Response Coordination Centre and the Solidarity Fund
and received funding in response to five events (two floods, two earthquakes, and the COVID-19 pandemic). There is a
known insurance gap in Croatia, as only 3 percent of disaster losses are insured, well below the EU average of 25 percent.7
At the same time, disasters and climate-induced hazards put additional pressure on the insurance markets and social
protection system as people are impacted by adverse events.
6.        Disasters have disproportionate impacts on the most vulnerable and exacerbate existing inequalities. Croatia’s
societal vulnerabilities are shaped by demographic shifts and structural inequalities. Despite successful efforts to reduce
poverty, vulnerable groups remain at high risk of poverty. In 2022, Croatia’s at-risk-of-poverty rate was over three
percentage points higher than the Europe and Central Asia upper-middle and high-income country average.8 Vulnerable
groups affected by disaster and climate impacts require shock-adaptive support. Croatia has an aging population, with
one of the oldest median ages in the EU, and a growing share of its older population lives alone or in rural areas. Other
groups who may be vulnerable in disasters include persons with disabilities and with chronic diseases, survivors of
domestic violence or human trafficking, nonprofessional caregivers, and the homeless. Despite improved living standards
since EU accession, poverty and exclusion persist, especially in rural and less developed regions. Disasters can exacerbate
these vulnerabilities, deepen gender inequality, and increase risks of gender-based violence. While the impacts of the
2020 earthquakes were intertwined with the effects of the COVID-19 pandemic, evidence shows that the associated
economic shock disproportionately affected women: for example, women dropped out of work at twice the rate of men
to fulfill family care responsibilities during the crises.9 Croatia’s experience is consistent with evidence from disasters
around the world: disasters amplify existing gender inequalities, as women often face higher economic vulnerability to
shocks because of insecure work, caregiving duties, and lower access to social assistance.
7.      Disaster impacts are particularly severe for women in socio-economically vulnerable situations. Certain
population groups face increased risk of poverty or violence in a disaster – and women tend to be over-represented among
these groups. In Croatia, women represent 79 percent of survivors of domestic violence, and 83 percent of single-parent
families; about two-thirds of in-home care recipients are poor older women, the majority of whom do not receive formal

8.       The proposed Cat DDO helps address key gaps in preparedness and adaptation, thereby strengthening Croatia’s
institutional, physical, financial, and social resilience, and it can lay the ground for future reforms to foster resilient and
inclusive growth. Since the 2020 earthquakes, the International Bank for Reconstruction and Development (IBRD) has
supported GoC with resilient recovery in critical public infrastructure, including in the health sector, and has provided
technical assistance in various areas, while the International Finance Corporation (IFC) has supported the private sector
and sustainable tourism. The Cat DDO leverages these engagements and opens new avenues in line with the WBG’s focus
areas and those of the Croatia CPF, such as the housing sector, renewable energy, and insurance. With a three-year loan
duration and monitoring of results for foundational reforms within a broader reform package, the Cat DDO can act as a
bridge to and inform reforms beyond 2026, when Croatia’s National Recovery and Resilience Plan (NRRP) ends. At the
same time, the Cat DDO provides contingent financing to complement budget reserve allocations. While the GoC has a
reserve fund of 0.5 percent of planned budget revenue, which can be used in responding to disasters, epidemics, accidents
or extraordinary events, its size limits the ability to ensure fiscal resilience to severe disasters. As a disaster risk financing
instrument, the Cat DDO provides a platform to consider future reforms for better management of disaster-related
contingent liabilities.
9.       Concerning Pillar 1 (institutional capacity), Croatia has improved its overall capacity to manage low-to medium-
impact events; however, more severe events—including those expected to become more frequent—could stretch
response capacities. Croatia has a well-established DRM framework, and has invested in national and city-level risk
assessments and strategies as well as warning systems and new regional emergency operational centers. However,
institutional gaps remain and there is also a need to modernize and innovate the DRM system to focus more on multi-
hazard emergency preparedness. Importantly, Croatia has not yet taken a systematic, risk-informed approach to risk
reduction that prioritizes improvement of its critical infrastructure and services. Given Croatia’s high exposure to climate-
sensitive hazards, adaptation investments are critical for long-term resilience and sustainable development. Despite past
reforms, gaps in institutional capacity and intra-institutional coordination have hindered systematic provision of the
information and oversight needed to drive CA action across key vulnerable sectors, including tourism, energy, transport,
and agriculture. None of these sectors are meeting their commitment to adaptation and mitigation, as noted in the CCDR.
10.     Concerning Pillar 2 (preparedness and climate resilience in key economic sectors), gaps in the policy framework
and its operationalization hinder both public and private sector actions in the energy, tourism, and housing sectors.
Reforms are needed to clarify mandates and oversight in all three sectors. In the energy sector, shock resilience should be
improved so that Croatia can meet its potential for renewable energy, and people and businesses can invest in their own
preparedness. In the tourism sector, a systematic and evidence-based approach to investment should be adopted so that
foundational reforms introduced since 2024 can be implemented at the local (tourism board) level and drive sustainable
and climate-informed investments. Finally, while the housing sector has reached a milestone with the reforms begun in
2024 (and continuing through 2026), these reforms and accompanying programs must be operationalized and taken up.
These reforms also present an investment opportunity for the private sector to drive the upgrade and renewal of the

11.      Concerning Pillar 3 (financial resilience of businesses and households), reforms are needed to strengthen the
insurance and social protection sectors. Strengthening private insurance and social protection systems is critical to ensure
they have operational functionality during disaster situations, and to support the financial resilience and coping capacity
of households and businesses against natural shocks. While disaster-related insurance products are available to businesses
and people, the insurance sector faces both demand-side and supply-side challenges, resulting in significant insurance
gaps. On the demand side, public perception of climate and disaster risk has increased, in part due to the 2020
earthquakes; however, pervasive gaps in financial literacy mean that the uptake of hazard insurance has remained low.
Premiums are perceived to be high by both households and businesses, and as a result insurance products are not taken
up beyond basic mandatory coverage. On the supply side, the insurance sector has adopted landmark EU legislation
(Digital Operational Resilience Act [DORA]) but has yet to operationalize it. Moreover, across subsectors and smaller
insurers, further reforms are needed to make hazard insurance products more broadly available. Social protection also
faces challenges. Croatia has a solid social protection system in place, which provides for one-off support in the case of
emergencies, and which also covers natural hazards; but the adequacy and coverage of baseline social support benefits
for vulnerable households need to be further strengthened to effectively support their baseline coping capacity.
 II. MACROECONOMIC POLICY FRAMEWORK
 A. Recent Economic Developments
12.     Croatia is a small, open economy that has significantly developed over the last decade, despite major shocks.
Annual GDP growth averaged 3.4 percent between 2015 and 2024. However, growth was uneven in this period, and the
country was hit by several shocks, including two earthquakes, the COVID-19 pandemic, and the recent energy crisis. The
COVID-19 pandemic caused the deepest recession in Croatia’s history due to the country’s strong reliance on tourism; this
sector contributes close to 20 percent to GDP. Yet the economic recovery was quick thanks to large fiscal and monetary
support schemes, including energy price caps and the inflow of EU funds. In 2022 and 2023, soaring food and energy prices
posed a threat to the recovery, but this was mitigated by several government support packages.
13.     While GDP growth has been strong, it has become increasingly dependent on household consumption and
investment, which were supported by favorable labor market developments, expansionary fiscal policy, and inflow of
EU funds. Strong domestic demand has led to rising imports, while exports of goods and services have been relatively
weak. The tourism sector is reaching peak capacity and faces carrying capacity constraints. Net exports are increasingly
weighing on growth. On the supply side, growth was largely driven by market services sectors, particularly trade,
hospitality, and construction. In parallel, manufacturing has been weak, and between 2023 and 2024 it negatively
contributed to overall economic growth.
14.      Labor market conditions have tightened in the post-COVID period, and wage growth has been strong amid
stagnant productivity, adding to inflationary pressures. The employment rate for the working-age population (ages 20–
64) reached 73.6 percent in the 2024, up from 60.5 percent a decade ago. Strong demand for labor in the context of
natural population decline, outward migration, and aging has created a significant shortage of labor in most economic
sectors, only partly mitigated by the strong inflow of foreign workers, who now account for almost 10 percent of
employment. Tight labor market conditions have put pressure on wages, which have been growing rapidly over the last
three years. In addition, the reform of the wage setting system in the public sector implemented in 2024 resulted in a
strong rise in salaries for public employees. At the same time, subdued labor productivity growth in the context of rising
wages constrains Croatia's external competitiveness. These factors have contributed to continued inflationary pressures,
and the average inflation rate amounted to 4.0 percent in 2024; but it accelerated again in 2025.
15.     Export market share, after three years of solid increase, fell in 2024, reflecting rising competitiveness issues.
The strong rise in salaries and stagnant productivity in the post-COVID-19 period have weighed on competitiveness,
especially in the services sector. In 2024, export of services recorded a real fall for the first time since 2013 (with the

17.     While the European Central Bank (ECB) cut interest rates on four occasions during 2024, the monetary policy
stance in the euro area remains restrictive. Croatia has been a member of the euro area since 2023. Inflation prompted
the ECB to implement its largest monetary tightening since the euro's inception, specifically by raising rates by 450 basis
points until mid-2024. Subsequently, between June and December 2024, the Governing Council cut the deposit facility
rate four times by a total of 100 basis points, reflecting a still restrictive policy stance amid high liquidity. The euro traded
narrowly against the US dollar for most of the year but depreciated sharply at the end of 2024, ending at US$1/€1.03—a
6 percent drop—due to diverging rate expectations and economic outlooks.
18.      The Croatian banking sector remains well capitalized, liquid, and highly profitable. Profitability and low credit
risk persist, though interest rate risk is rising. Supported by a strong labor market and lower rates, household loans grew
rapidly amid loose credit standards, and corporate lending accelerated with increased investment. Thanks to robust
business performance and wage growth, the nonperforming loan ratio dropped to a record low of 2.4 percent by June
2025. Capitalization is strong, with a total capital ratio of 23.2. Higher interest rates and a positive economic outlook
boosted banks’ net interest margins; profitability is expected to gradually decline but to stay above the last decade’s
average. Government exposure is high, with government claims at 21 percent of sector assets, the third highest in the EU.

 B. Macroeconomic Outlook and Debt Sustainability
19.      The macroeconomic policy framework is adequate for the purpose of this operation; it is supported by favorable
growth prospects, Euro area membership, ongoing government efforts to reduce public debt, and institutional reforms
aimed at enhancing resilience to natural catastrophes. The Croatian economy continues to perform strongly despite
economic challenges among Croatia’s main trading partners and a challenging global environment. Adopting the euro
resulted in lower borrowing costs and gave the country access to the European Stability Mechanism, which can support
Croatia in the event of severe financial distress. The Croatian National Bank (CNB), however, continues to use macro-
prudential policy to maintain the stability of the financial system and support the reduction of inflationary pressures.
Expansionary fiscal policy that has supported the country throughout the recent crisis is being reversed in the 2026 budget,
and the government remains committed to reduction in public debt. Moreover, Croatia continues to strengthen its
institutional and legislative framework to improve its resilience against natural hazards, and planned amendments to the
Budget Act will integrate consideration of disaster and climate liabilities into budgeting.
20.      After a strong performance in 2024, real growth is expected to be robust, at an average annual growth rate of
2.8 percent between 2025 and 2028. While domestic demand remains a key driver of growth, private consumption and
investment are expected to slow down (Table 1). Household consumption growth is expected to remain strong but to
decelerate, reflecting slower real wage growth and NCB measures aimed at reducing household demand for new loans.
Inflation in Croatia—which has been among the highest in the euro area in 2025—is expected to start converging toward
the ECB’s target of 2 percent, thus supporting real disposable household incomes. Private and public investment are
expected to slow down due to rising uncertainty related to shifts in global trade policy and the end of NRRP in 2026. Trade
policy shifts might slow down external demand recovery, in turn affecting exports of goods and services, but domestic
demand remains relatively strong and will support solid import growth. Net exports are likely to remain a drag on growth
until 2027 and turn neutral in the final year of the forecast horizon. On the supply side, market services will remain the
main growth driver in the medium term.

21.      The current account will remain in deficit at around 3 percent of GDP, but strong non-debt creating capital
inflows will support the decreasing external debt-to-GDP trajectory (Table 2). While exports of goods and services might
accelerate, reflecting continued economic recovery among Croatia’s main EU trading partners, imports will remain robust
as well, so the goods and services account will remain in a large deficit. This will be offset to a certain extent by the surplus
on the primary and secondary income account due to the strong inflow of personal remittances and interest income.
Hence only a modest improvement in the current account balance is expected by the end of the forecast horizon.
Nevertheless, a large surplus on the capital account that reflects abundant inflow of EU funds, as well as stable foreign
direct investment inflow, will support a further drop in external debt–to-GDP ratio to below 60 percent by 2028.

22.      The ECB’s monetary policy will remain focused on ensuring that inflation stabilizes at 2 percent in the medium
term, while CNB will continue to closely monitor developments in the domestic banking sector and will intervene if
needed to preserve financial stability. ECB’s policy rate cuts since June 2024 should support growth in the medium run,
while inflation is expected to stay at around 2 percent. However, with relatively high uncertainty surrounding the growth
and inflation forecasts and with the impact of recent trade policy measures still unclear, ECB is not committing in advance
to a particular interest rate path. In 2025, CNB implemented new macro-prudential rules limiting consumer lending with
the dual goal of curbing financial stability risks created by rising household borrowing and enhancing household resilience
in challenging economic conditions. It is expected that the banking sector will remain highly profitable and well capitalized.
23.      Expansionary fiscal policy has supported economic growth in recent years but is being reversed in the 2026
budget. Expansionary procyclical fiscal policy has been a crucial economic policy instrument during the recent crises,
including the recession created by the COVID-19 pandemic and the energy crisis (Table 3). However, as of 2026, this
approach is expected to be reversed, and fiscal policy is likely to become contractionary. The fiscal deficit is expected to
decline gradually and will remain below 3 percent in the medium term in line with the EU fiscal rule, as the effects of the
public sector wage reform fade in 2026 and overall price pressures ease. The government has already announced that
some of the support measures, most notably the electricity price subsidy, have started to be phased out from autumn
2025. In parallel, robust growth will continue to support buoyant revenue collection (Table 4). Public debt is expected to
fall below 56 percent of GDP in 2028. Still, with public debt’s relatively high sensitivity to adverse shocks and with possibly
increasing spending needs for defense, the GoC should take two key steps: (i) create fiscal space to address large

25.      Despite Croatia’s solid economic performance, long-term growth could be dampened by structural headwinds

public institutions. To sustain growth and progress on shared prosperity and poverty reduction, Croatia must act on several
fronts. Enhancing productivity remains key and could be achieved by driving private sector investment, diversifying the
economy into higher-value-added sectors, and creating a more dynamic business environment. Croatia also should tackle
the demographic challenge contributing to labor shortages; decouple growth from environmental pressures like water
use and air pollution and manage exposure to extreme weather risks; and strengthen institutions to support development
and effectively address related challenges.

26.      The last International Monetary Fund (IMF) Article IV review for Croatia was published in July 2024. The next

 III. PROPOSED OPERATION
 A. Link to Government Program, CPF, Other WBG Operations, and Corporate Priorities
27.      The Cat DDO supports GoC’s efforts to strengthen its framework and capacity for disaster preparedness and
resilience, improving public sector service and impact, and private action. Competitiveness, resilience of the economy,
and better quality of life for citizens are key commitments in the GoC’s program (2024–2028). The National Development
Strategy 2030 includes “Strengthening resilience to crises” as one of its four strategic priority directions. In 2022, Croatia
adopted its first Disaster Risk Management Strategy until 2030, committing to a proactive approach and listing key
priorities and interventions to be pursued.13 In 2020, the country adopted its National Climate Change Adaptation
Strategy, which outlines a comprehensive plan for climate adaptation up to 2040 and foresees an estimated investment
of €3.6 billion, with a range of measures in key vulnerable sectors.14 A major part of Croatia’s NRRP (2021–2026) is
dedicated to advancing disaster and climate resilience, including for energy efficiency—critical to ensure insulation for
cold and heat waves—in line with the country’s Long-Term Renovation Strategy until 2050, Low-Carbon Development
Strategy until 2030 with a View to 2050 (or Low-Carbon Development Strategy in short), and reconstruction of earthquake-
affected buildings. The NRRP also includes reforms promoting resilience across key sectors, including energy, tourism, and
social protection; these reforms are in line with commitments at the EU and global levels, such as the EU’s Green Deal, EU
Preparedness Union Strategy, and the Sendai Framework for Disaster Risk Reduction (2015–2030).

Pillar 1. Strengthening institutional capacity for disaster preparedness and resilience
PA1. To strengthen the preparedness and resilience of essential public services and infrastructure of national-level
importance, the Borrower has introduced requirements for critical infrastructure operators to conduct systematic disaster
risk assessments, testing, incident reporting, and continuity planning, as evidenced by the enactment of the Law on Critical
Infrastructure.
38.      Rationale: Croatia’s critical infrastructure (assets, facilities, equipment, networks, and systems, both public and
private) provides essential services to society by maintaining vital social functions, economic activities, public health and
safety, and the environment, but it faces heightened vulnerability to disaster and climate risks as well as other types of
emergencies. In addition to geophysical risks, critical infrastructure is increasingly exposed to climate risks from sea-level
rise and more frequent heat waves, floods, and wildfires, which threaten service reliability and long-term economic
resilience. Despite its criticality, there is no systematic, all-sector oversight of risk assessments, risk reduction, and
preparedness (exercise, stress testing, training) measures, nor consistent capacity for emergency preparedness across
sectors.
39.      Policy content: The reform, approved in June 2025, changes Croatia’s approach to critical infrastructure from
primarily focusing on protection during emergencies to adopting a proactive and consistent approach for multi-hazard
resilience of critical infrastructure and services. Aligned with the EU Directive on the Resilience of Critical Entities and
national priorities, the law fills gaps in the DRM system with respect to mainstreaming and operationalizing risk
management principles across sectors. The MoI is the national coordinator for this law. Applicable to 15 sectors21 (174
assets across 30 entities, including state-owned enterprises), the law spells out requirements for critical infrastructure
operators related to annual multi-hazard risk assessments, emergency preparedness testing (exercises) and training,
incident reporting per a set template and protocols (to enhance early detection and action), continuity planning, and
reduction/mitigation of the assessed vulnerabilities and potential impacts, as well as CA measures. The act clarifies
certification of private security providers, the role of security coordinators, and management of data, including public
disclosure, requiring operators to assess and plan for climate-induced risks, such as extreme weather events, flooding,
and droughts. The MoI is developing and will regularly update a national critical infrastructure resilience strategy, and

50.      Rationale: Housing is among the sectors most impacted by disasters. The housing stock is exposed to disaster and
climate risks and is vulnerable due to its age and poorly maintained condition. The 2020 earthquakes caused €6.9 billion
in damage in the sector; some 24,000 residential buildings were affected, including common areas (walls, stairs,
foundations, chimneys, facades, etc.).41 MABs form 33 percent of Croatia’s building stock, with the vast majority built prior
to modern building codes.42 In Zagreb and Rijeka, half of the population lives in MABs built before 2000, and over 30
percent lives in housing considered at high earthquake risk.43 MABs are exposed to climate-related hazards such as
extreme heat, floods, and heavy rainfall, which add pressure to outdated building envelopes, drainage systems, energy
performance, and fire safety. MABs’ vulnerability arises from their design, building practice/malpractice (e.g., illegal
alterations), and lack of maintenance and capital investments.44 In the 1990s, the responsibility for maintaining MABs
moved from the state to private owners and management companies, yet housing (condominium) associations were not
recognized as legal entities. The framework, mandates, and responsibility for maintenance remained unclear, hindering
the uptake of investments, including in safety and energy efficiency measures at scale. Residential buildings (70 percent
of the building stock) are the largest individual energy consumers. Buildings built before 1981 are the least energy efficient.
51.      Policy content: The Ministry of Physical Planning, Construction, and State Assets (MoPPCSA) is reforming the
sector through a series of laws, in line with the National Housing Policy Plan until 2030 and the Action Plan 2025–2027,
adopted in March 2025; the goal is to strengthen the housing stock’s disaster and climate resilience, improve energy
efficiency and livability. The Law on Management and Maintenance of Buildings,45 adopted in May 2025, mandates the
housing associations and building managers be established and formally registered. The associations—through building
managers—are required to conduct regular building checks and approve yearly financial and maintenance plans; these
are meant to guide owners’ investments in MABs’ common areas and equipment, including those relevant to housing
safety, quality, energy efficiency, and self-generation (e.g., solar photovoltaics, heat pumps, electric charging stations).46
The law defines both regular (or routine) and extraordinary maintenance of buildings (urgent and necessary repairs and
investment maintenance). Urgent repairs focus on rapid mitigation of risks to people and physical damage, whereas
necessary repairs and investment maintenance focus on permanent, proactive risk reduction, such as repair and
reconstruction of load-bearing walls, roofs, columns, mezzanines, facades, insulations, foundations, and chimneys, as well
as landslide remediation, and renovation following disasters. All works have to comply with relevant construction,
including energy efficiency and fire safety regulations. In this way, the law addresses the gaps exposed by the 2020
earthquakes, in which common areas (facades, chimneys, etc.) created a serious safety risk and produced large volumes
of debris due to decades of underinvestment, lack of clarity on responsibilities for maintenance and building management,
and malpractice.47 The law also proactively addresses climate-related risks and contributes to priorities set by the Long-

54.      Rationale: The frequency and severity of climate-related disasters in Croatia is expected to increase and thus put
additional pressure on an underdeveloped insurance market. Only 3 percent of disaster losses are insured, meaning that
the uptake of private insurance is substantially below the EU average. This lag exposes households and firms to significant
financial risks and has causes in both the supply side and the demand side of the insurance market. On the supply side,
the quality and consistency of solvency assessments pose persistent challenges to Croatia’s insurance sector, particularly
smaller insurers, and the climate risk assessments conducted for insurance purposes are inadequate. This is especially
important given Croatia’s increasing exposure to disaster and climate-related risks, which require insurers to demonstrate
resilience under stress scenarios. The reform addresses these market constraints and shortcomings, by mainstreaming
resilience planning in the insurance market (to ensure stability and solvency and broaden market participation), while also
contributing to a comprehensive disaster risk financing strategy. Key legislation has been approved that aligns with the
EU’s 2022 DORA and Solvency II, but its implementation hinges on reforms to the supervisory authority of the Croatian
Financial Services Supervisory Agency (HANFA), which will allow it to oversee systematic adoption of stress-testing and
business continuity planning. On the demand side, the limited take-up by both households and businesses is linked to
comparatively high premiums, the lack of mandatory disaster insurance in high hazard zones, and low levels of financial
literacy—especially in more rural areas that see high concentrations of risk (e.g., flooding and agricultural losses).
55.      Policy content: The Insurance Act amendment provides HANFA with strengthened authority to develop and
deploy quantitative supervisory tools, in particular climate-sensitive stress testing and scenario-based solvency
assessments. HANFA is expected to operationalize this provision by updating its supervisory methodology and applying it
across the sector. This will include issuing guidelines for stress testing and climate risk assessments, tailored to the risk
profiles of both large and small insurers, and taking supervisory action where necessary to ensure compliance and improve
the quality of reporting. Measures to enhance transparency and supervisory effectiveness strengthen the enforcement
framework and ensure that reporting and stress tests are translated into effective solvency and continuity planning. These
measures address gaps in the sector’s ability to withstand adverse shocks, in particular climate-related financial risks;
hence, they help ensure that insurers are adequately capitalized and prepared to absorb shocks. With its newly enhanced
supervisory authority aligned with EU and OECD standards, HANFA can facilitate access to the Croatian insurance market
for insurance providers from OECD countries, and can mobilize private capital. On the demand side, to tackle low
awareness among consumers, HANFA has mandated increased investments in financial literacy programs, which include
education on natural hazard insurance. The Action Plan for Strengthening Consumer Financial Literacy 2025–2026 outlines
a systematic program to raise capacity among a range of population groups (e.g., prospective homeowners, students, and
technical associations); it also tracks implementation, and coordinates efforts by HANFA, specialized government
agencies, and private sector actors. The reform is further complemented by the issuance of HANFA Guidelines for
reporting on financial literacy investments by insurance entities in September 2025. Financial literacy measures can pave
the way for more substantive demand-side reforms over time—including adjustments in product design and pricing as
well as the introduction of mandatory or pooled coverage in high-risk areas.

PA7. To strengthen the recovery capacity of socio-economically most vulnerable populations to shocks, the Borrower

57.     Rationale: Changes in climate are expected to make floods, heat waves, and wildfires more frequent and severe
in Croatia, and impacts of these hazards will disproportionately affect low-income households and other vulnerable groups
with low adaptive capacity. While Croatia’s solid social protection system can support most vulnerable populations’ efforts
to cope with and recover from natural shocks, key reforms are needed to improve the adequacy of benefits for and
coverage of vulnerable groups.50 Especially in the context of disaster and climate resilience, reforms are critical for
ensuring adequate support for vulnerable groups who are at heightened risk of falling into poverty and experiencing
socioeconomic hardship due to disasters. Experience from past disasters in Croatia and the wider region suggests that
women in socioeconomically vulnerable situations (e.g., single parents or survivors of domestic violence) are especially
vulnerable in disasters. There is thus a need for strengthened and dedicated social support mechanisms for these groups.
58.      Policy content: The Ministry of Labour, Pension System, Family and Social Policy adopted the Social Welfare Act

thus enhancing their resilience to shocks. Several groups, most of whose members are women, are targeted by this reform

the half previously available. Moreover, the reforms relax eligibility rules for in-home care social services for people unable

women, the OECD estimates that women make up two-thirds of persons with in-home care needs.51 In-home care services
provided through the Social Welfare Act are complemented by the Zaželi program, which offers employment
opportunities to women under EU Structural Funds with limited duration. In parallel, the MoF has adopted a major reform
of the population registry to consolidate data across multiple registries, which in the medium term is expected to improve
the administrative efficiency of the social welfare system, including during disasters. These reforms are complemented by
physical investments in social infrastructure financed through the NRRP and other sources.

front-end fee for the loan will be covered by the Borrower.

exchange control environment of the CNB is satisfactory. In the absence of a recent IMF safeguards assessment,59 this
assessment is based on the satisfactory track record in the operation of designated accounts in previous DPL and IPF
operations, and on the external audits of CNB performed annually by independent auditors. The 2024 audit report includes
an unmodified audit opinion issued by the external audit firm KPMG Croatia. Additionally, the SAO conducted a
compliance audit for the year 2023 and issued an unmodified audit opinion.60 Based on the above, CNB can be relied upon
to account for the WB’s proceeds from this operation. Given the satisfactory performance of CNB, an audit of the deposit

confirmation letter stating that the loan funds have been received and deposited into the designated account assigned by
the Borrower and forming part of the Borrower’s budget management system. Given the strong performance of Croatia’s
PFM system and the low fiduciary risk associated with this operation, there is no need for additional fiduciary mitigation

Pillar 1. Strengthening institutional capacity for disaster preparedness and resilience
2.       PA1 is expected to generate positive social outcomes. Underlying policy actions and results seek to enhance
institutional capacity for disaster response and adaptation, including climate resilience. Croatia’s vulnerability to disaster
impacts is heightened by institutional gaps that undermine proactive risk reduction and effective coordination.62 PA1 and
PA2 are expected to enhance the resilience of critical services and strengthen institutional capacity for adaptation, thereby
delivering direct benefits to poor and vulnerable groups. By enhancing the reliability of essential sectors such as health
care, water, energy, and transport during shocks, the reforms will help ensure service continuity for elderly residents, low-
income households, and persons with disabilities, all of whom have limited coping capacity. Past disruptions have
underscored the risks created by service breakdowns. The 2020 Zagreb earthquake (which resulted in €11.3 billion in
damage and loss) and the 2020 Petrinja earthquake (€5.5 billion in damage and loss) left thousands without heating,
sanitation, and safe housing.63 The 2018 contamination of Slavonski Brod’s water system deprived 90,000 residents of safe
drinking water, while the 2019 HE Dubrovnik fire caused three fatalities and prolonged energy disruptions.
3.       Under PA2, potential social impacts may arise from investments in climate-sensitive sectors such as transport
and tourism. Social impacts are primarily linked to labor and working conditions, as well as community health and safety
concerns associated with infrastructural investments. However, these risks are expected to be minor and short term, as
climate adaptation measures in these sectors are unlikely to entail major infrastructure that could raise significant
concerns. Most adaptation measures in Croatia are nonstructural, and only a smaller share of potential investments
involves physical infrastructure. For measures involving infrastructure, the emphasis is on climate-proofing, so that
projects are not likely to involve greenfield sites or major infrastructure that requires large‐scale land expropriation. Such
activities could generate adverse social impacts if not managed appropriately. The national Climate Adaptation Strategy

4.      Mitigation measures: Under PA2, Croatia’s system for management of impacts has been strengthened and is
increasingly aligned with good international practices. EU membership has strengthened the regulatory framework,
particularly the national environmental impact assessment system. Since joining the EU, Croatia has integrated the acquis
communautaire into its legal framework to align national systems with international good practices. EU membership has
also supported the adoption of global norms and conventions that promote human rights, social inclusion, sustainability,
and equality. Additionally, the implementation of the EU taxonomy for sustainable finance has heightened the relevance
of social considerations. While the taxonomy primarily defines environmentally sustainable activities, it includes
safeguards related to labor rights, human rights, and nondiscrimination; it thus encourages businesses to adopt ethical
practices and embed social responsibility in their operations (Croatia CCDR). Croatia’s legal framework governing labor
and working conditions is generally comprehensive and adaptive. Reforms adopted in March 2025 through amendments
to the Aliens Act reinforce worker protections by requiring nondiscriminatory employment contracts with equal pay,
tightening employer compliance obligations, and introducing a public registry of noncompliant employers that covers
labor law violations, occupational safety breaches, and inadequate accommodation.
Pillar 2: Enhancing disaster preparedness and climate resilience in key economic sectors
5.       PA3 can strengthen the resilience of households and businesses during disasters, and can therefore generate
positive outcomes. Less than 1 percent of Croatians currently use renewable energy, while over half (80 percent among
low-income households) rely on inefficient energy sources such as wood and coal.65 In the longer term, renewable energy
self-production can contribute to lower energy bills and protect households from price shocks. Around 15 percent of those
using inefficient energy sources experience arrears on utility bills (the share is over 25 percent among the poor), while less
than 8 percent of those using more efficient sources such as electricity are in arrears (the share is 15 percent among the
poor). Nevertheless, systemic barriers, including high upfront investment costs, collateral requirements, delayed grid
connections, limited access to information, and ongoing maintenance challenges, may constrain poor and vulnerable
households from investing in distributed renewable energy resources. Croatia’s broader policy framework, including the
updated Integrated National Energy and Climate Plan and the Low-Carbon Development Strategy, envisions subsidy
reforms and carbon pricing that will gradually increase the cost of fossil energy. The OECD reported that fossil fuel
subsidies still covered around 8 percent of GHG emissions in 2023,66 suggesting long-term pressure on households reliant
on fossil fuel–based heating. Without targeted support, vulnerable households are exposed to rising energy costs, which
will exacerbate existing inequalities.
6.       Mitigation measures: To mitigate potential risks and impacts for vulnerable groups related to energy prices
volatility, the GoC has adopted several measures. On September 16, 2025, the 9th Package of Measures to Protect Citizens’
Living Standards and Economic Competitiveness was adopted by the GoC. The package maintains subsidies for vulnerable
households (up to €70 per household) and benefits 117,000 recognized vulnerable energy consumers. It also provides
funding for additional social benefits, including Inclusive Allowance and the GMB. Furthermore, the planned introduction
of the Social Plan for Climate Policy in 2026 will support fossil fuel–dependent households in accessing renewable energy,
thus ensuring an inclusive transition where no group is left behind.
7.       PA4 could generate positive long-term welfare impacts. Tourism contributes around 20 percent of Croatia’s GDP
and supports over 300,000 jobs—about one-fifth of total employment (CCDR). Strengthening the tourism sector’s
resilience to climate risks can directly benefit low-income households, particularly people who work in tourism and are
exposed to income and job fluctuations—a group that includes about 8 percent of Croatia’s poor and about 5 percent of

8.        Mitigation measure: Under PA4, the proposed policy reform seeks to diversify tourism away from climate-
vulnerable areas, with funding prioritized for inland and mountain regions (categories III and IV in the Tourism
Development Index).67 By directing resources to these less prosperous non-coastal areas, the reform aims to foster
development, reduce poverty, and narrow disparities with coastal counterparts.68 Furthermore, by integrating indicators
on stakeholder consultations and the satisfaction of host communities and tourists, the proposed reform is expected to
strengthen engagement processes. Climate adaptation investments will be vetted with local stakeholders, helping to build
social license to operate and thereby minimizing potential adverse social risks and impacts.
9.      PA5 can bring significant benefits for low-income households in Croatia, where about 90 percent of the poor
own their homes and around 8 percent rent at a reduced price or free of charge. Homes are often acquired through
inheritance, gifts, or co-residence, leading to overcrowding and, in many cases, deteriorating conditions. About 12 percent
of poor households (15 percent in rural areas) live in homes with leaking roofs, damp walls or foundations, or rotting
frames and floors. Housing is generally old; about half of the poor live in houses built before 1980, and over 85 percent of
housing has not undergone any improvements in thermal insulation, windows, or heating systems in the last five years.
The situation is more severe for homes already in poor condition, where the share without improvements reaches around
90 percent. Strengthening housing associations can make these homes safer and more resilient to disasters, improve living
conditions, reduce energy costs, and lower long-term poverty risks by reducing the need for costly post-disaster recovery.
10.       However, risks remain, as repair costs could be passed on to tenants and exacerbate pressures on low-income
families; about 21 percent of poor households are already struggling to pay rent—and the share rises to 39 percent
among those renting at a reduced price in cities. About 55 percent of poor households (compared to 28 percent of the
nonpoor) consider housing costs a heavy burden, with rates particularly high in small towns and rural areas. Inequitable
access may also favor wealthier urban associations over smaller or informal communities, while implementation gaps are
likely given the widespread lack of improvements.
11.      Mitigation measure: Legislation is in place to protect tenants from sudden rent increases. The Law on Lease of
Apartments (OG 91/96, 48/98, 66/98, 22/06, 68/18, 105/20) stipulates that rent cannot be increased within the first year
of a signed contract. After one year, landlords may raise rent by up to 20 percent above the average rent in the same
settlement or county for a comparable apartment (based on size, furnishings, and location). The average rent is
determined by the local self-government unit. However, enforcement is uneven and often does not extend to tenants
who lack formal contracts. Additional mitigation measures include subsidized or cofinanced repairs targeted at low-
income households, capacity-building support for small and informal housing associations, and equity-focused allocation
rules that prioritize vulnerable communities across both rural and urban areas.

is expected to generate significant positive social impacts. In addition, tighter enforcement of contingency planning and

Environmental Risks Analysis
14.     The Cat DDO is not expected to directly result in significant positive or negative effects on or changes in the
environment, forests, or other natural resources. The operation is anticipated to generate overall long-term positive
environmental outcomes through its support for enhanced disaster preparedness and climate resilience. These benefits
are expected to materialize across key economic sectors, including tourism, energy, and natural resource management.
The Cat DDO is expected to contribute to increased renewable energy generation and diversification of energy sources,
which in turn should improve energy security and reduce greenhouse gas emissions. Furthermore, by strengthening the
insurance sector’s capacity to absorb shocks and remain operational during crises, the Cat DDO will facilitate faster
remediation and recovery in sectors that depend on natural resources, forestry, and functional ecosystems.
15.      Under PA1, which aims at strengthening institutional capacity for disaster preparedness and climate resilience,
the Law on Critical Infrastructure is aligned with the EU Directive on the Resilience of Critical Entities and national
priorities. The expected improvements include better cross-sectoral coordination and increased institutional capacity,
which should ultimately reduce environmental damage, including harm to forests and ecosystems, in case of disasters.
16.      Under PA2, which focuses on strengthening institutional capacity for CA, the enactment of the law will result in
the establishment of a focal point unit that incorporates climate considerations in sectoral policies and monitors policies’
implementation. The law is expected to foster investments in climate-sensitive sectors. While these investments may
involve civil works, some of which could occur in environmentally sensitive areas, they are not expected to include large-
scale infrastructure and will be implemented under the framework of EU environmental regulations. Thus, any potential
impacts on forests and the environment are expected to be limited and manageable.
17.      Under PA3, which supports the preparedness of the power sector and its end-users through the promotion of
self-supply from renewable energy sources and diversification of electricity supply, the Amendments to the Law on
Renewable Energy Sources and High-Efficiency Cogeneration are designed to encourage prosumers to invest in renewable
energy generation and in battery storage systems. While the long-term increase in battery use may lead to the generation
of significant quantities of waste, Croatia has an operational system for the collection and management of electronic,
hazardous, and battery waste. Therefore, no significant adverse environmental impacts are anticipated.
18.     Under PA4, which aims to strengthen the resilience of the tourism sector to climate risks and impacts, the
adoption of two ordinances—the Ordinance on Indicators for Monitoring the Development and Sustainability of Tourism,
and the Ordinance on the Methodology for the Development of Destination Management Plans—provides authorities
with instruments to enable transparent, indicator-based assessment and monitoring systems, improve sectoral data, and
support the implementation of climate risk–informed measures and investments. Although the resulting management
plans may lead to investment pipelines that include civil works, these will be focused on enhancing the resilience of the
tourism sector and are expected to have a positive effect on the preservation of ecosystems and natural landscapes.
