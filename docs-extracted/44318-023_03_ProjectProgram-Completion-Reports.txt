                                     I.       PROJECT DESCRIPTION

1.     In 2009, the Government of Uzbekistan launched a program (the rural housing program)
to construct new rural communities throughout the country. 1 The program was designed to
reduce socioeconomic disparities between the country’s urban and rural populations by
increasing living standards, incomes, and job opportunities in rural regions. Under the program,
847 houses were built in 2009 and 6,800 houses were built in 2010. A mortgage loan was made
to the buyer of each of these houses by Qishloq Qurilish Bank (QQB), a state-owned
commercial bank established in 2009 to provide such mortgages in rural areas.2

2.      In 2010, the government requested ADB’s support to expand the program over the next
5 years (2011–2015). In August 2011 ADB approved a multitranche financing facility (MFF)
called the Housing for Integrated Rural Development Investment Program. 3 The MFF had three
components. The first aimed to provide housing loans through participating commercial banks to
targeted creditworthy borrowers for the purchase of new homes in rural areas. The second and
third aimed to improve the capacity of local governments to implement integrated rural
development plans and investment promotion strategies, and to improve the enabling
environment for micro and small enterprises (MSEs) to expand or establish new businesses and
create jobs in rural areas. Under the MFF, ADB would provide the government with $500 million
in loans to support component 1, while the government would be fully responsible for financing
and implementing components 2 and 3.

3.        As originally designed, ADB’s support was to be delivered in four tranches—one for
each year during 2012–2015. Ultimately, ADB’s support was divided into three tranches—the
first for 2012, the second for 2013 and 2014, and the third for 2015.4 The government requested
a loan of $200 million for the tranche 1 project. Of this amount, $199,600,000 was to be onlent
by the government to QQB to provide mortgage loans (subloans) to qualified homebuyers, and
$400,000 would support the activities of the project implementation unit (PIU) established under
the Ministry of Economy (MOE) and pay for certain reports to be prepared under the project.
The loan was approved on 9 September 2011. A $200 million loan for the tranche 2 project was
approved in October 2013, and a $100 million tranche 3 loan was approved in August 2015. Of
the tranche 2 loan, $198.56 million was allocated for mortgage loans to homebuyers, as was all
of the tranche 3 loan. The tranche 2 and 3 loans are expected to close by the end of 2016.

was relevant at both appraisal and completion. The rural housing program was a flagship
government program, with more than $3.3 billion in capital investments made during 2011–
2015. The government’s welfare improvement strategy sought to improve housing for citizens,

B.      Project Outputs

6.       The tranche 1 project had three outputs: (i) the provision by participating commercial
banks of housing loans to targeted creditworthy subborrowers in rural areas, (ii) an improvement
in the capacity of local governments to prepare and implement integrated rural development
plans and investment promotion strategies, and (iii) the enhancement of the enabling
environment for entrepreneurs and MSEs to establish new businesses or expand existing ones
in rural areas. The tranche 1 project’s design and monitoring framework (DMF) is in Appendix 2.

7.      Output 1. The DMF set a target of providing at least 10,300 mortgage loans with a value
of at least SUM570 billion to targeted rural beneficiaries, of whom at least 30% should be
women. Tranche 1 actually financed 10,279 mortgage loans by QQB with a value of SUM687
billion—8,363 loans in 2012 and 1,916 in 2013.8 These were provided in 146 of the country’s
159 rural districts, and 21% went to women.

8.      The mortgage loans were used to finance the construction of houses in more than 300
locations in rural areas throughout the country, most of which were near existing communities.
Housing sites ranged in size from 3 to 200 houses. One-quarter of them contained 10 or fewer
houses, and 90% had no more than 60.

9.     On average, the new homeowners contributed down payments equal to 40.6% of the
purchase price, with the remainder being funded by a 15-year mortgage loan. The loan amounts
were all the same for a given year: SUM62,920,000 in 2012 (equivalent to $31,713 as of 31
December 2012), and SUM79,590,000 in 2013 ($36,141 as of 31 December 2013). The 2012

training, of whom 13% were women.

12.    Output 3. An output 3 target of an increase in total microcredit lending in rural areas
from SUM485 billion in 2010 to SUM835 billion in 2012 was not achieved in nominal terms or on
a percentage basis. However, the 2010 baseline of SUM485 billion used in the DMF was
incorrect, because it was actually the total of microcredit lending nationwide, not in the rural
areas alone. Based on the correct baseline, rural microcredit lending actually increased by 59%
from 2010 to 2012, compared with the 72% increase implied by the DMF target. It rose from
about SUM318 billion to SUM506 billion—or about SUM188 billion, compared with the original
increase targeted of SUM350 billion.

13.      The DMF listed two indicators for output 3 with no targets. These were simply stated as
the number of newly established MSEs in project areas and the number of new or revised
policies, regulations, and laws adopted. In 2012 and 2013, 1,533 MSEs were established by
homebuyers, of whom 38% were women. In the same 2 years, the government adopted 28 new
or revised policies, regulations, and laws that improved the enabling environment for MSEs.

C.       Project Costs

14.     Project costs totaled $697.7 million, which was equivalent to about 1.3% of Uzbekistan’s
gross domestic product for 2012. 10 This was 2.4% lower than the appraisal estimate of $714.6
million.11 House construction accounted for 77.7% ($542.1 million). Most of the remaining costs
were for the construction of 253 kilometers (km) of streets and access roads, 328 km of gas
pipes, 410 km of water pipes, 246 km of electricity lines, 201 schools, and 28 rural health clinics.
Private developers built 32 community centers, 65 shopping complexes, 12 markets, 26 retail
shops and bakeries, and 12 small banks.

D.       Disbursements

15.    It was anticipated at appraisal that the entire $200,000,000 tranche 1 loan would be
disbursed in 2011 and 2012. Actual disbursements totaled $199,744,701 and were spread over
4 years: $159,639,488 in 2012, $40,093,635 in 2013, zero in 2014, and $11,618 in 2015. This
was due to several reasons. Loan effectiveness was delayed by 6 months (para. 19). Only
8,510 houses were built in 2012, compared with the 10,300 contemplated at appraisal. This in
turn reduced the number and amount of the mortgage loans issued in 2012. In addition,
a technical and financial performance review (TFPR) for rural housing program houses
constructed in 2009–2011 and three studies that were also expected to be financed out of the
tranche 1 loan in 2012 were not completed until 2015 (para. 17).12

E.       Project Schedule

16.     At appraisal, it was contemplated that the loan would be declared effective in October or
November 2011, thereby enabling an initial disbursement of $150 million to help finance
construction beginning in March 2012. Instead, loan effectiveness did not occur until April 2012
(delaying the start of construction by 2 months), and the first disbursement did not occur until
July 2012.

17.     The project implementation plan contained in the facility administration manual provided
that the contract for the TFPR was to be advertised in the fourth quarter of 2011 and that the
TFPR would be completed in second quarter of 2012. However, the contract for the TFPR was
not advertised until September 2013 and was not awarded until October 2014. This pushed
completion back to May 2015 from the originally planned 2012. Much of this delay was due to
disagreements within ADB over the scope of the TFPR, the method for consultant selection, and
whether only supreme audit institutions13 should be eligible for selection. However, the borrower
was responsible for some of the delay (para. 28). Because of the three-year delay, the
recommendations from the TFPR and the three studies prepared could not be considered when
the houses for 2013, 2014, and 2015 were designed, as had been originally intended.

F.       Implementation Arrangements

18.    QQB was selected as the implementing agency for component 1 because it was the only
bank with experience in making loans under the government’s rural housing program. MOE was
the executing agency and the implementing agency for components 2 and 3. This was
appropriate, since the MOE was the lead government agency responsible for local governments
and the promotion of MSEs.

G.      Conditions and Covenants

19.     ADB approved the tranche 1 loan on 9 September 2011. The loan agreement, which
was signed on 16 February 2012, provided that it would not be considered effective until the
subsidiary loan agreement between the borrower and QQB had been executed and delivered.
The loan agreement became effective on 20 April 2012 following the signing of the subsidiary
loan agreement on 29 March 2012 and the receipt by ADB of satisfactory legal opinions. The
period of 5 months between loan approval and loan signing is not considered excessive for
Uzbekistan because of the time-consuming process of preparing and adopting the government
resolution authorizing the signing of a loan agreement with an international financial institution
such as ADB. 14 The 2 months needed after the signing of the loan agreement to meet the
effectiveness condition of the execution and delivery of a subsidiary loan agreement was also
reasonable.15

20.    The covenants in the FFA and the loan agreement and in the project agreement
between QQB and ADB were relevant. Almost all covenants were complied with. The
exceptions are described in paras. 21–27 or indicated in Appendices 1, 3, or 4. Covenant
breaches did not materially affect project performance.

21.     The FFA, the loan agreement, and the project agreement required QQB to maintain
adequate credit and risk management policies, operating systems, and procedures.16 QQB’s
2014 audited financial statements indicated a high level of delinquent housing loans and loans
for which pledge agreements had not been signed. In response to a request by ADB, QQB
adopted an action plan in July 2015 that included making improvements to its loan
administration and collection procedures. As a result, the percentage of 2012 loans delinquent
more than 90 days declined from 26.3% at the end of 2014 to 9.9% at the end of May 2016.
QQB expects a government resolution to be approved in October 2016 that will speed up the
registration of pledge agreements for 2012 loans, 56.6% of which were still unregistered as of
31 December 2015.

22.    The FFA signed in July 2011 and the loan agreement and project agreement, which
were negotiated in July 2011 and signed in February 2012, provided that the grace period for
2012 subloans would be 6 months. However, a presidential resolution signed a month prior to
the signing of the loan agreement set the grace period for 2012 subloans at 12 months.17 This
meant that all of the subloans signed in 2012 violated the relevant provisions of the FFA, the
loan agreement, and the project agreement until the discrepancy was discovered and remedial
amendments to change the grace period to 12 months were signed in September 2013 (FFA)
and December 2013 (loan agreement and project agreement) to extend the grace period
allowed to 12 months.

23.     The FFA, the loan agreement, and the project agreement all required QQB to maintain a
return-on-average-assets ratio of 0.5% for each of the 2 most recent completed fiscal years.

24.     At the time of program approval, Qishloq Qurilish Invest (QQI) was a wholly owned
subsidiary of QQB. It was also both (i) the construction supervisor for the building of the houses;
and (ii) the owner of the 12 brick manufacturers and one door, window, and roof factory
supplying materials to the contractors building the houses for the state housing program.
To eliminate these conflicts of interests, the FFA required that (i) QQB establish QQI as an
independent legal entity by 1 January 2014, and (ii) QQI sell the 13 construction supplies
companies by 1 January 2013. In May 2013, QQI became an independent legal entity (thereby
satisfying the first requirement) and transferred ownership of the 13 building materials
companies to a new subsidiary of its former parent, QQB. Although the ownership transfers
(which in any case missed the 1 January 2013 deadline) may have legally been “sales”, ADB
determined that QQB’s ownership of the companies constituted a conflict of interest; therefore a
covenant requiring their sale to independent third parties was inserted in the loan agreement for
the tranche 2 project.18

25.    The FFA required the government to carry out annual technical and financial

A.       Overall Assessment

52.    The tranche 1 project is rated successful. It was implemented as conceived in all but one
respect. Of the $199.6 million provided for mortgage loans, $20.8 million was applied to loans
for houses constructed in 2013 rather than in 2012.

B.       Lessons

53.     An ADB-financed nationwide project that involved 272 building sites, 838 bid packages,
and 2,319 bids in a single year required a massive amount of effort by ADB staff and other
stakeholders.45 Prior review by ADB of 838 contracts in less than 3 months in a single year was
and is infeasible. This lesson was incorporated in the design for the program’s second and third
tranches. They provided for post review (sampling) of contracts for works procured through
national competitive bidding.

54.     The appointment by ADB of a dedicated international public management specialist to
work on the project full-time was an excellent decision. However, the lack of support by a full-
time international procurement specialist (either an ADB staff member or a consultant) was a
serious deficiency in 2012 and 2013. This was rectified in 2014 during the implementation of the
tranche 2 project.

55.      The intended project outcome was to downstream housing loans to moderate and lower
income beneficiaries, with a focus on women. The project demonstrated that it was possible to
achieve this goal by conducting an expanded and improved public information campaign and
prioritizing applicants based on their responses to a brief questionnaire (footnote 26).

C.      Recommendations

56.     The current scope and scale of the government’s rural housing program can improve the
living standards of only a small portion of the country’s rural population. 46 The government
should consider steps to encourage banks to provide loans to rural residents to build their own
houses or make improvements to their existing houses. Local governments should also
consider allocating vacant or undeveloped locations in rural cities to individuals willing to
construct new houses there. Some rural communities suffer from poor road connections with
market centers and periodic power and gas outages. These important problems need to be
addressed to improve living conditions.

57.    The three house designs offered under the government’s rural housing program (para.
10) are too expensive for some families who want new homes. To make the purchase of
housing affordable for people with lower incomes than those who can afford the current designs,
the government should consider offering new types of housing, such as apartment buildings and
houses with smaller or fewer rooms. A multifamily building design would particularly suit
communities close to cities and locations where little land is available for construction.

58.    Affordability could also be improved by lengthening the repayment period beyond 15
years and increasing the maximum allowable mortgage loan for the government’s rural housing
program, which is currently set at 1,000 times the minimum wage. This would decrease the
down payment needed for buying a house.

59.    The government should identify the legal and regulatory impediments to the
development of a secondary mortgage market. Eventually, banks should be able to sell
mortgage loans that they originate to long-term investors, such as pension funds and insurance
companies, either directly as whole-loan sales or through securitization.

60.     As recommended by the technical and financial performance audit for 2012 47 the
government should consider using different building materials to build houses. These include
polystyrene concrete blocks, foam concrete blocks, and glass-fiber plastic, which can make the
rooms warmer in winter and cooler in summer. Green options, such as solar water heaters and
photovoltaic solar panels and improved insulation materials in floor, walls, roofs, and ceilings
should also be explored.

61.     To improve procurement, the government should develop simplified bidding procedures
for the rural housing program, including standard bidding documents and bid evaluation
templates. The procedures, contract opportunities, and bidding results should be published on a
well-publicized website. To eliminate the time-consuming process of evaluating the technical
and financial qualifications of each bidder, the government should simplify the process of
qualifying contractors through a transparent, efficient online system for pre-registration of
contractors based on their qualifications. Contract sizes of more than 10–15 houses should be
considered to reduce the number of contracts. To reduce the number of mistakes made by the
government’s procurement personnel (para. 29), they should be given more training on the job
and in workshops.

               SUMMARY OF GENDER EQUALITY RESULTS AND ACHIEVEMENTS

1.      Gender equality was incorporated into the overall design of the tranche 1 project under
the Housing for Integrated Rural Development Investment Program. The project was
categorized as effective gender mainstreaming. The project design and monitoring framework
included several gender indicators. During project preparation, gender analysis was carried out
to assess (i) potential gender-differentiated impacts, as well as the options to maximize the
benefits to women and minimize the adverse impacts; and (ii) the capacity of government
agencies, community-based organizations, and women’s groups to assist in project preparation
and implementation. A gender action plan (GAP) was prepared to incorporate gender findings in
the project design, promote participation by women in project-related activities, and improve
their ability to benefit from the project. Almost all of the proposed gender actions were carried
out during project implementation.

2.     The project had a major positive impact on women. Women became active in the public
information campaign for the rural housing program and were encouraged to apply to purchase
the new houses. Although the vast majority of all houses in rural areas of Uzbekistan are owned
by men, 25% of the applications for 2012 and 26% of the applications for 2013 were submitted
by women. Applicants were ranked using a 19-question scorecard that gave preferences to
women by awarding extra points to teachers and health care workers (two professions in which
women predominate), widowed and divorced women with children, and persons with home-
based businesses (also mostly women). Women became the registered owners of 20% of the
2012 houses and 23% of the 2013 houses.

3.      The rural houses built under the program had piped water. This significantly reduced the
household workload and time burden on female beneficiaries who had previously needed to
bring the water from exterior pumps. Houses provided sufficient space for women to start such
home-based businesses as sewing and making baked goods. During group discussions,
women expressed appreciation for the clean water, modern cooking facilities, and small
gardens provided under the project. Health and hygiene culture improved because the houses
included indoor toilets, bathrooms, and hot water. Rural women, particularly young wives, stated
that they were very happy to reside in the houses. Living in a house built under the rural housing
program has become a dream goal for many rural families. In addition, shopping centers, food
markets, bakeries, hair salons, and mini-banks created in the new housing communities
generate jobs, most of them for women.

4.     Women comprised 13% of the 11,000 local government staff that received training
under the project in 2012. 5,700 women entrepreneurs participated in business and job
opportunity training sessions in 2012 and 9,060 women registered new businesses. Women
prepared 6,500 business plans in 2012 and 9,700 in 2013.

                            Stories of Women and Their New Houses

Before moving into a new house built under the state rural housing program in 2012, M. and her
husband and three children lived with her husband’s parents and his elder brother, his elder
brother’s wife, and their six children. All facilities in the old house, including the toilet, bathroom,
and kitchen, were outside. The home had no central heating or regular supply of gas or running
water. M.’s parents-in-law provided financial help for their son’s family to purchase the new
house, which has all the modern conveniences. M. found that she had more free time and
decided to pursue further education, reflecting her belief that better education opens up more
opportunities for better and more stable employment and the higher income that can ensure a
good education and a better life for her children. M.’s parents are helping to pay for her
schooling. Her husband, a builder, supports M. in raising the children and doing home chores.

                                              * * * * *

After graduation from a medical institute, N. worked as a doctor and lived in an extended family
home with her husband’s parents and her husband’s two brothers and their families. Each
family had very limited space. As a result of conflicts in the family, N. divorced her husband and
moved back to her parents’ home where she and her two small children had to live together with
her married brother and his family. N. felt like a guest in her parents’ home. She dreamed of her
own space, buying her own furniture, and choosing what curtains to have and what food to cook.
She was excited when she learned from the local government about the possibility of taking a
loan to buy a new house. N. decided to sell the car that her parents had purchased for her to
make the down payment for her house. She realized that she needed to start a business to be
able to repay the loan. She opened a small business center that provided training in baking for
girls and an English language class. A year later, she had to close this business down and
became a trader. She now makes a decent income that has enabled her to feed her children
well and even to buy a new car. She is thinking seriously about going back to her medical
career. N does not want to lose her professional standing and plans to open a specialized
medical center in her massif (community of houses). And she has started dreaming about one
day taking out another mortgage loan, because she wants her son and future daughter-in-law to
eventually be able to live in their own separate house.

                                              * * * * *

M. previously lived with her husband’s parents and his three married brothers. M. and her
sisters-in-law had numerous chores around the house and on the family’s plot of land. Among
other things, they had to bring the family’s water supply in from outside of the house. Heating
water and cooking were done on the furnace in the outside kitchen. She carried coal to heat the
rooms and shoveled snow off the roof in winter. A family decision was made that M.’s family
would be the first one to move into a new house as her husband was the eldest brother. Now
that M. and her family live in a new house, her children are much healthier and get fewer colds
because of the home’s hot running water and indoor bathroom and kitchen. The rooms are
warm in winter. M. has the basic modern home appliances: a gas stove, a water heater, a
washing machine, and a refrigerator. The family is planning to buy a microwave in the near
future. M. uses the additional free time provided to her by these amenities to earn money by
sewing dresses for her neighbors and teaching their preschool age children. Her income,
together with her husband’s salary and income from selling the vegetables they grow in their
yard, is enough to cover all of the family’s expenses.

Before moving into a new house, D. lived with her husband’s parents, an unmarried brother, and
a married brother and his wife. The old house was built of clay and hay, and children had to
share bedrooms with their parents. It had no central heating. The kitchen, toilet, and bathroom
were outside. Water had to be brought into the home from an artesian well. Because D. worked
as a teacher, she had little time to help her mother-in-law with the house chores and the
livestock that the family raised on their land. She was uncomfortable about the fact that this
heavy burden fell mostly on the shoulders of her sister-in-law, who did not have outside
employment. Because the parents-in-law had so little space and one more son yet to marry,
they sold some livestock to make the down payment on a new home into which D. and her
husband, who was their eldest, could move and set up their own household

D., who is currently on maternity leave, reported that she enjoyed the new house because it has
the facilities she needed to take proper care of her new child. These include hot and cold
running water indoors; an indoor kitchen and toilet; central heating; and a gas stove, washing
machine, and vacuum cleaner. The mortgage loan for the house is in D.’s name, and she and
her husband need to make the payments on time. Earning income is crucial. Her husband
currently makes the loan payments, while D. covers the household costs. At first, she earned
money by sewing curtains and dresses for her neighbors. Later, neighbors in the small
community asked her to teach English to their children. She is currently teaching 10 children two
or three times a week, and her monthly income has increased substantially. Given the
importance of D.‘s business and the income it earns for the family, D.’s mother started to come
to the home to help care for D.’s child when she is working. Her parents-in-law plan to buy a
house built under the state rural housing program for their second son so that he can also have
a comfortable home.
