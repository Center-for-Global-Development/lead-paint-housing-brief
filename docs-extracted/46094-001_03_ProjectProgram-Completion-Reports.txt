                                       I.       PROJECT DESCRIPTION

1.       The Neighborhood Upgrading and Shelter Project (Phase 2) sought to improve living
conditions in selected urban areas in Indonesia.1 The project aimed to demonstrate an integrated
approach to upgrading slum neighborhoods by (i) providing resources directly to disadvantaged
communities to improve their living conditions; (ii) establishing settlements for poor families; and
(iii) as part of capacity development, assisting local administrations in managing pro-poor urban
development, including developing multisector strategies to handle urban poverty issues beyond
infrastructure upgrades.

2.      The outcome was improved infrastructure and access to service delivery in slum
neighborhoods in 20 Indonesian cities, including Bandar Lampung, Bengkulu, Batam, Tanjung
Jabung Barat, Palembang, Tanjung Balai, Semarang, Pekalongan, Pasuruan, Serang, Sukabumi,
Banjarmasin, Kapuas, Palangkaraya, Makassar, Palopo, Bone, Kendari, Ambon, and Bima.2 The
project’s outputs are (i) strengthening institutional capacity for managing pro-poor urban
development, (ii) upgrading infrastructure in slum neighborhoods, and (iii) establishing new
settlements for poor families. The project design and monitoring framework (DMF) and the
achievement status of project impact, outcome and outputs are in Appendix 1. The Asian
Development Bank (ADB) approved a loan of $74.4 million to help finance the project.

                                 II.        DESIGN AND IMPLEMENTATION

A.       Project Design and Formulation

3.      In support of the National Medium-Term Development Plan (RPJMN), 2010–2014 the
project was designed to help reduce poverty and close income inequality gaps in disadvantaged
urban neighborhoods by upgrading basic public infrastructure in slums. It helped meet the
Millennium Development Goals to significantly improve the lives of slum dwellers and reduce the
proportion of urban households without sustainable access to safe drinking water and basic
sanitation. It also contributed to the implementation of the Cities without Slums Program or Kota
Tanpa Kumuh to address rapid urbanization.3

4.     The project was included in ADB’s country operations business plan, 2013–2014 for
Indonesia and helped achieve the targets of the results framework of ADB’s country partnership
strategy for Indonesia, 2012–2014 and ADB’s Strategy 2020.4 The project was aligned with ADB’s
Urban Operational Plan, 2012–2020.5

5.    The project design was appropriate as it was built on the lessons from the government’s
foremost poverty reduction program.6 It supported government efforts to improve overall living

implementation as the government actively coordinated with the DGHS on project progress and
attended review missions. Commitment and support from local governments and communities
were sufficient to achieve the expected project outputs.10

8.    The project remained relevant at completion and contributed to the implementation of the
RPJMN, 2015–2019.11 This aligned the project with the Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs)
because the project contributed to opening access to affordable housing and livable settlements.12

B.       Project Outputs

9.     Output 1: Institutional capacity for managing pro-poor urban development
strengthened. The output was fully achieved. All 20 local governments endorsed the Slum
Improvement Action Plan (SIAP),13 which cities referenced to implement slum alleviation
programs. In addition, 176 female local government staff participated in training programs and
short courses and 37 other government staff received a master’s degree on community-driven
development approaches in urban development (31% out of 30% target).

10.    Output 2: Infrastructure in slum neighborhoods upgraded. The output was
substantially achieved. Under the project, 209 neighborhood upgrading action plans (NUAPs)
were completed in 2017 and 276 community action plans were compiled from 2018 to 2019. About
770.69 kilometers of neighborhood roads were upgraded, and water supply facility and system

completion report (PCR) mission considered the NSD construction as successful. All beneficiaries
interviewed said their quality of life in NSDs drastically improved as a result of (i) having improved
access to basic services; (ii) living in a safer environment with less exposure to risks; and (iii)
having their financial situation improved because of lower rent.

12.     Local governments established an NSD management board16 in Palopo and in Bima in
2019 to collect subsidized rental fees from beneficiaries; rent has been collected regularly since
May (Palopo) and August 2019 (Bima). The Kendari government is unable to collect housing rent
because it has yet to establish an NSD management board. On the other hand, the Kapuas has
established an NSD management board and rental fees but could not collect rent because the
houses were being used as COVID-19 isolation facilities.

C.      Project Costs and Financing

13.      At appraisal, the project was estimated to cost $102.0 million: (i) $74.4 million from ADB’s
ordinary capital resources for civil works, institutional development, and consulting services;
(ii) $6.4 million from the central government and $15.2 million from local governments for
infrastructure improvement, SIAP preparation, NSD land provision, project management, taxes
and duties, and financial charges during implementation; and (iii) $6.0 million from beneficiaries
and the private sector for building NSD houses.

14.     At completion, the cumulative project cost was $120.5 million. Actual ADB financing fell to
$71.2 million because the rupiah depreciated against the United States dollar, and loan savings
of $3.2 million were canceled in 2019 and 2021. Central government financing more than doubled
to $13.0 million because DGHP had to finance the NSD housing construction, and beneficiary
financing decreased to $2.4 million. Local government financing was higher at $33.9 million to
fund civil works beyond what was envisaged at project design, indicating good local government

D.       Disbursements

15.     Disbursement of the loan proceeds followed ADB’s Loan Disbursement Handbook (2012,
as amended from time to time) and the project administration manual. An advance account was
set up and managed by DGHS. The account was useful as the project covered a large number of
packages in various sizes and made faster disbursements. The funds were transferred directly
from the advance account to BKM bank accounts for community civil works. To use the funds,
BKMs must submit a fund withdrawal plan, verified by the community advisor and acknowledged
by the city coordinator, to the local commitment-maker officer. For large civil works, the contractor
submitted the payment request along with a progress report to the commitment-maker officer and
payment was transferred from the advance account to the contractor’s bank account. Appendix 6
presents the fund flow.

16.     The loan was scheduled for disbursement from the second half of 2014 to 2018. However,
project delays pushed the first disbursement to 29 October 2015. ADB approved removing the
$7.4 million advance account ceiling on 19 August 2016 to provide DGHS flexibility to manage
the loan proceeds in the advance account and to expedite disbursement. The final disbursement
was made on 19 March 2020. Appendix 7 compares disbursements at appraisal and completion.

E.       Project Schedule

17.     The project experienced implementation start-up delay. It was approved on 31 March 2014
and became effective in July 2014. However, civil works procurement was delayed because
project management consultants were mobilized only in August 2015. ADB approved a minor
change in scope on 25 August 2017 to address the delay, including (i) adjusting the number of
upgraded infrastructure based on NUAPs and changing the fund transfer to community accounts
for the slum upgrade component (output 2),17 (ii) reducing NSD participating cities and changing
from build-and-sell to build-and-rent arrangements to adapt to the government regulation issued
on 25 May 2016 for the NSD component (output 3),18 (iii) extending the loan closing date from
June 2018 to 31 December 2019 to allow construction to be completed, and (iv) reallocating $2.3
million from the civil works category to the training and workshops and consulting services
categories.19 The unused balance was refunded in June 2020 and the loan savings were canceled
in January 2021, but the project’s account was closed only on 2 July 2021 because of prolonged
reconciliation of the advance balance.20 Appendix 8 details the project implementation schedule.

G.       Consultant Recruitment and Procurement

20.     Consultant recruitment. Nine consulting service packages with estimated inputs of 2,297
person-months were planned at appraisal for ADB funding.23 At completion, the inputs increased
to 3,557 person-months to capture the extended project duration. While DGHS planned to post
the notice for advance recruitment of NMCs and RMCs on the ADB website in November 2013,
following ADB’s Guidelines of the Use of Consultants (2013, as amended from time to time), ADB
approved the advance contracting procedure only on 4 December 2013. DGHS had opted to use
MPWH’s e-procurement system for consultant selection. However, as agreed during loan

B.      Effectiveness

46.   The project is rated effective. Achievements exceeded the target outcome indicators:
779,605 households living in slum neighborhoods benefited from the infrastructure upgrades
(Appendix 2); and 99.1% of community members were satisfied with the infrastructure
improvements, including 99.0% of female respondents (Appendix 15).39

47.      Output 1 was fully achieved, and outputs 2 and 3 were substantially achieved. The
adjusted DMF indicator on infrastructure under output 2 was based on NUAPs developed by
communities and differed from the appraisal estimate since neighborhoods to be covered under
the project had not been decided then. The PPP indicator under output 3 was removed because
private developers and financial institutions were no longer part of the NSD component (para. 18).
Local governments allocated their own budgets to maintain infrastructure, and DGHS continued
turning infrastructure over to local governments (footnote 15) and verifying infrastructure assets
in 2021. DGHP started building an additional 20 housing units at the Bima NSD in 2021 while an
allocation for 58 additional units is being proposed for 2022. The Kendari government continued
efforts to establish an NSD management board in 2021, and the Kapuas government has been
monitoring COVID-19 cases to see when the NSD houses used as isolation facilities can be used
by the beneficiaries (Appendix 3). Implementation of the social and environmental safeguards
was less effective because (i) the livelihood restoration programs in Kapuas were not completed,
and (ii) an SPPL and a UKL-UPL were not prepared before construction in 2015 and 2016. The
project was less effective for gender and social inclusion because achievement is below 80%.

C.      Efficiency

48.     The project is rated efficient. The economic net present value was $31.89 million and the
economic internal rate of return was 19.4%, lower than the estimates at appraisal, and exceeded
the 12.0% threshold at $45.23 million and 20.8%. The number of households benefiting from the
upgraded sanitation facilities and system and the improved income-earning potential from small
business were higher than estimates. However, exchange rate differences resulted in calculated
benefits decreasing by $1.0 million for households that benefited from upgraded sanitation and
$1.8 million for beneficiaries with improved income-earning potential. The sensitivity analysis
conducted at completion tested the project’s sustainability and economic benefits against adverse
scenarios. Results showed the project remained economically viable against a 20% increase in
O&M cost and a 20% decrease in project benefits. The economic analysis is in Appendix 16.

D.     Sustainability

50.     The project is rated likely sustainable because no significant technical or financial
challenges are foreseen for infrastructure O&M. The community manages O&M for infrastructure
under community civil works (Appendix 17) while O&M for large civil works is financed by local
governments and water utilities. The NSD management boards in Palopo and Bima use monthly
rental fees complemented by local government funds to finance O&M costs. The Kendari
government is still establishing an NSD management board, and the Kapuas government is
monitoring COVID-19 cases in its area to determine the reutilization of NSD houses. As a result,
O&M of NSD in Kendari and Kapuas are fully financed by the local government.

51.     As the infrastructure does not generate income, assessing value for money from cash flow
streams as measured by the net present value and internal rate of return during project
implementation cannot be conducted. Appendix 18, Table 18.1 shows the responsibility of each
local government department for infrastructure O&M. Because revenue from the rent only partly
covers the O&M costs of NSD, while O&M cost of the community civil works and large civil works
are financed by the local government budget, the water utility, and village funds, the project is not
financially viable. The project completed an SIAP for each project city during planning (footnote
13). The SIAP has become the readiness criterion for slum upgrades in other cities under other
similar projects.

E.     Development Impact

52.    The project impact is rated satisfactory. The project helped reduce slum areas from
3,181.28 to 1,407.00 hectares in 20 participating cities (Appendix 19). Since the project was

53.      The infrastructure upgrade (Appendix 2) increased the service coverage for drainage in

facilities have been connected to solid waste management and wastewater disposal facilities. By
the end of the project, the number of low-income people in the project areas decreased slightly
because improved access to clean water supply reduced living costs, improved neighborhood
roads reduced transportation costs, and improved water supply and sanitation facilities reduced
medical costs. Appendix 17 compares the situation before and after project implementation.

                      IV.    ISSUES, LESSONS, AND RECOMMENDATIONS

A.        Issues and Lessons

61.    Start-up delay and mid-implementation revision. Harmonizing consultant selection
procedures for advance procurement of the NMC and RMCs and using MPWH’s e-procurement
system should have been discussed during project readiness and before loan negotiations. Since
PPPs could not be implemented because of an unforeseen policy change in 2016, which should
have been considered a project design risk, DGHP was engaged in 2017 to provide NSD houses.

62.      Project monitoring. The project was implemented in 209 participating neighborhoods in
20 cities. Although the number of cities is smaller than the previous project,43 DGHS needed to
have monitoring capacity and a system to capture the detailed activities implemented in such a
dispersed and decentralized level. As civil works were implemented in neighborhoods, which were
mostly managed directly by BKMs, close facilitation by the city coordinator and community advisor
is critical to ensure that project activities follow the project administration manual. Sukabumi’s
fund embezzlement case confirmed that monitoring and facilitation strengthening of BKMs is
essential. Furthermore, DGHS needs to ensure that the capacity to prepare project financial
statements is available within its organization to avoid the rejection of the APFS due to incomplete
project financial statement (para. 39), particularly after the loan closing where the support of the
consultant is limited or no longer available.

63.    Prolonged asset handover process. Infrastructure handover to local governments is
delayed. DGHS has verified more than 50% of infrastructure assets, notified local governments
in 2021 to expedite preparing the supporting documents, and continued verifying the remaining
assets. Infrastructure upgraded through community civil works, on the other hand, has been
completely turned over to KPPs. Direct disbursement to BKMs improved the communities’ sense
of belonging, as reflected in the quality of O&M implementation. The community initiatives were
appropriate, although KPP membership is informal and voluntary (Appendix 17).

64.    Coordination with project stakeholders. The project benefited by engaging existing
BKMs in project implementation because the BKMs are familiar with such community-driven
development projects and with ADB procedures and requirements (footnote 14). NSD housing
was provided under DGHP’s budget, and the steering committee should facilitate coordination
between DGHS and DGHP to reach the NSD housing target.

65.     Gender action plan compliance. The project provided (i) better access for women and
low-income households to improved infrastructure; and (ii) platforms for women’s increased
participation in public settings, such as public consultations, socialization, BKMs, KPPs, and
RKPs. GAP implementation to meet gender targets, such as collecting and using sex-
disaggregated data and having women lead community organizations and take construction work,
was challenging because the initiatives were relatively new in the project locations. Relevant
guidelines were convoluted and prevented gender knowledge, specifically GAP implementation,
from being transferred properly to the NSD projects; and being used to prioritize beneficiary
selection. While regular review focused on the quantitative achievement of GAP implementation,
the qualitative analysis of women’s public participation and leadership was undocumented. As a
result, it is challenging to measure the impact of women’s involvement in the overall project
planning and implementation.

66.     Safeguard compliance. Weak consultant performance during the early stage of project
implementation and insufficient ADB support for safeguards before 2017 (para. 29) led to
noncompliance with safeguard requirements. A UKL-UPL was not prepared for civil works in 2015
and 2016. A LARP was not prepared before construction of the drainage in Pekalongan and no
LARP approval from ADB for the construction of the bridges in Kapuas started in 2016.
Implementation of the livelihood restoration programs is delayed and completed in 2021. The
land donation have been in compliance with provisions of donation set forth in the resettlement
framework, except the donation made in 2016 which have no record and the revised final
verification report on voluntary donations is still pending (para. 41). Safeguard requirements
should be disseminated frequently to BKMs and LCOs through regular community forums and
workshops with the local government to ensure that all safeguard actions are documented and
reported properly. ADB clarified safeguard requirements and compliance during the missions and
site visits since 2017 and continued coordination meeting after loan closing to address
outstanding safeguard issues and compliance with ADB’s Safeguard Policy Statement.

67.      Innovative approach to active resettlement. The project provided comprehensive
resettlement with adequate housing and facilities under NSDs. Assessment on the location of the
NSD site was critical to ensure sufficient economic opportunities. In addition, transparent selection
of beneficiaries and the involvement of beneficiaries in NSD design and management are crucial
for long term success of the NSD. Furthermore, dedicated budget and institutional responsibility
of the management and operation of the NSD is important for long term sustainability of the NSD.
The Kapuas local government opened fishponds in the NSD to support the community’s economic
activities (para. 44). The Batam and Palopo governments replicated the project approach and
                                                                                                15


used their own budget to finance slum upgrades in their cities. The SIAP has also become the
required document for cities to implement slum alleviation activities financed by other donors.

B.     Recommendations

68.     Future monitoring. Asset handover should begin once construction is completed to avoid
delay. While community civil works improved the community’s sense of belonging, met community
needs for infrastructure and job opportunities, and supported local business, material availability
in each location and time needed for training and dissemination need to be considered for projects
with small civil works. Moreover, because the contracts were widely dispersed and funds were
managed directly by the community, verification of disbursements by the commitment-maker
officer and the supervision consultant needs strengthening to ensure the validity of the
disbursement proposal and the accountability of the community, community advisors, and city
coordinator; and to avoid potential fraud.

69.     Robust or enhanced coordination or dialogue between the project team, contractors, and
the local government responsible for UKL-UPL is necessary to ensure monitoring commitments
are fulfilled. Training on ADB guidelines and project requirements must be assessed. Where
necessary, training programs must be adapted, or their frequency increased to improve on-site
compliance. Land donations for community civil works should be recorded in writing. Such
measures are essential to ensure project compliance with ADB’s Safeguard Policy Statement.

70.     Disseminating communication materials to raise awareness and understanding of gender
equality and social inclusion as well as the GAP—which is developed based on the needs and
interests of specific target groups, such as women, households headed by women, the elderly,
and children—will also help projects integrate gender mainstreaming more effectively. Conducting
situational gender analysis beforehand and designing initiatives, such as gender training for the
communities that address local sociocultural values and gender stereotyping by explaining its
clear benefits, is recommended to yield strategic gender changes. Similar projects also need to
pay more attention to women’s economic potential to allow women to participate in and benefit
from project construction outside civil works, such as livelihood and community development.

71.      Further action or follow-up. While the asset handover to local governments is ongoing,
DGHS needs to continue verifying the remaining assets. In addition, DGHS should follow-up with
local governments to ensure that (i) all outstanding livelihood restoration programs are completed
in Kapuas in 2021; (ii) an NSD management board in Kendari is established and rent is collected;
(iii) houses in the Kapuas NSD are reused for poor households after the Regent Decree No. 154
dated 6 April 2020 is lifted (Appendix 3); and (iv) NSD housing construction in Bima is completed
in 2021 and the allocation for 58 units in 2022 is confirmed.

72.     Timing of the project performance evaluation report. The project performance
evaluation report may be prepared after 2023 when all project infrastructure assets have been
fully operating for 3 years. By that time, all components should be fully operating and the living
conditions of people in urban areas of the participating local governments should have improved.

                            STATUS OF NEW SITE DEVELOPMENT

1.      The project has continued the New Site Development (NSD) to provide affordable housing
for poor households with adequate basic infrastructure. NSD was originally intended to develop
a model of partnership for each NSD city between central government, local administrations,
developers, and financial institutions. The project was to take a catalytic role in developing
examples for replication and promote public–private alliances in five cities, where (i) local
governments will provide land from their own resources and issue land titles for potential new
house owners (title are required as collateral to receive loans from financial institutions); (ii)
Ministry of Public Works will provide basic infrastructure (roads, water and electricity supply, and
sanitation facilities) financed by loan proceeds; (iii) private developers will prefinance the house
construction, and coordinate connections to the main network of service providers (water and
electricity); (iv) financial institutions will provide loans to poor families to buy (or construct) new
houses; (v) nongovernment organizations (NGOs), in close cooperation with the local
administration, will organize socialization campaigns to explain the NSD scheme to poor families
living in slum neighborhoods of the project city, develop transparent criteria to select poor
beneficiary families to be eligible to apply under the NSD scheme, and facilitate the selection
process.

2.      Five out of 20 project cities were originally targeted to be the location of NSD, which were
Bengkulu, Palopo, Palangkaraya, Pekalongan, and Palembang. However due to various reasons
related to land provision, Bengkulu, Palangkaraya, Pekalongan, and Palembang stepped back
from the NSD. Following the approval of project minor scope change in August 2017, the number
of participating cities was reduced to four, which included Palopo, Kendari, Kapuas, and Bima.
However, the original target of 800 houses was retained during the minor change in scope in 2017
even though according to the approved NSD technical proposals of the four cities the total carrying
capacity of the NSD locations was only 763 units.

3.      The government enacted Government Regulation No. 14 of 2016 on Development of
Housing and Settlements Area. The issuance of the regulation affected the overall implementation
of the NSD component as the regulation limits the transfer of government-owned assets to other
parties, e.g., community and private entities. To retain the original approach of build and sell, a
separate presidential approval for the transfer of land to each community group would be required.
Nevertheless, the government proposed changing the approach from purchase to rental, which
was consistent with other affordable housing schemes.

4.      Under the new approach, i.e., build and rent, the local government will provide the land
and the Directorate General of Human Settlements (DGHS) will provide the basic infrastructure.
The new approach implied that the private developers and financial institutions which was
originally expected to provide housing for the poor beneficiaries were no longer involved in the
NSD, signifying that the public-private partnership approach cannot be implemented. Instead, the
provision of NSD houses will be financed and implemented by the central government through
the Directorate General of Housing Provision (DGHP). After completion of the infrastructure, the
assets will be transferred to local governments. The houses will be rented to beneficiaries,
selected by local government at subsidized rental rates. The local government needs to establish
a unit within their structure to manage the housing units, including the operation and maintenance
of the units.

5.     NSD Kendari. The dissemination of the slum alleviation program was conducted in March
2017 and the local government established the selection committee trough Mayor Decree No.
1420 of 2016. The dissemination of beneficiary selection was conducted in 2018 and collection
22     Appendix 3



of administrative requirements, e.g., expression of interest, ID card, and family card from potential
beneficiaries was conducted in 2018 and early 2019. The government decided to establish a
separate management for NSD, yet the establishment process was prolonged until 2021 as the
local government continued to assess the requirements and resources for the establishing the
NSD management.

6.      The number of houses built in NSD Kendari met the target of 225 units all of which are
already occupied. Nevertheless, there are only 78 households that have signed the rental
agreements since December 2018 which means that the demand for the NSD house is high but
indicated that the city did not follow the procedures agreed in the NSD proposal. Responding to
the issue, the government planned, by the end of 2021, to conduct evaluation and verification of
the status of occupancy for the remaining households who have not signed the rental agreement.
The rent tariff has been set at Rp79,757 or around $5.5 per month as outlined in the contract
agreement. No rent has been collected yet as the local government tried to focus on administering
the legal aspect of house occupancy and establish the NSD management.

7.      NSD Palopo. The dissemination of the slum alleviation program was conducted in
November 2017 and the local government established the selection committee through Mayor
Decree No. 108 of 2017. The dissemination of beneficiary selection was conducted in 2018 and
collection of administrative requirements, e.g., expression of interest, ID card, and family card
from potential beneficiaries was conducted in 2018 and early 2019. The government had
established a separate management for NSD through the Mayor Decree No.160/II/2018 dated 7
February 2019. Furthermore, the government had stipulated rental tariff through Mayor Decree
No. 152/I/2019 dated 18 January 2019. The tariff in 2019 was set at Rp60,456 or around $4 per
month for the 28 square meter type and Rp75,261 or around $5.5 per month for the 36 square
meter type and the tariff increases each year. Rent had been collected since May 2019.

8.      The number of houses built in NSD Palopo met the target of 120 units of which 100 units
were occupied by December 2019. The units were fully occupied in 2020 yet 11 households were
later evicted from the house as they violated the NSD rules, i.e., not occupying the house after
the rental agreement was signed, not paying the rent, and transferring the house to others without
approval of the government and the NSD management. Following the local government policy to
provide facility for disaster emergency response, the government designated 5 units for disaster
emergency, leaving 115 units occupied in 2021.

9.      NSD Bima. The dissemination of the slum alleviation program was conducted in July 2018
and the local government established the selection committee through Mayor Decree No. 66 of
2018. The dissemination of beneficiary selection was conducted in September 2018 until April
2019 and collection of administrative requirements, e.g., expression of interest, ID card, and
family card from potential beneficiaries was conducted in 2018 and early 2019. The government
had established a separate management for NSD through the Mayor Decree
No.188.45/521/650/VII/2019 dated 19 July 2019. Furthermore, the government had stipulated
rental tariff through Mayor Decree No. 188.45/379/650/V/2019. The tariff in 2019 was set at
Rp89,00 or around $6 per month and the tariff increases each year. The rent had been collected
since August 2019.

10.     The number of houses built in NSD Bima at project completion was 140 units, or lower
than the target of 218 units. The occupancy of NSD houses was 90 units at project closing and
increased to 140 in 2020. The government proposed for allocation to DGHP the construction of
40 units in 2020. The request cannot be fulfilled due to budget refocusing toward the coronavirus
disease (COVID-19) outbreak. The government had secured the DGHP allocation for the

construction of 20 units in 2021 and the preparation for construction is ongoing. Meanwhile, the
allocation for the construction of the remaining 58 units is being requested for 2022.

11.       NSD Kapuas. The dissemination of the slum alleviation program was conducted in April
2018 and the local government established the selection committee through Regent Decree No.
600 of 2017. The dissemination of beneficiary selection was conducted in January 2018 and
collection of administrative requirements, e.g., expression of interest, ID card, and family card
from potential beneficiaries was conducted from March to May 2019. The government had
established a separate management for NSD through the Regent Decree
No.369/DPUPRPKP/2018 in October 2019. Furthermore, the government had stipulated rental
tariff through Regent Decree No.441/PUPRPKP TAHUN 2019 dated 14 October 2019. The tariff
in 2019 was set at Rp76,660 or around $5.5 per month and the tariff increases each year. The
government waived the rent payment in the first year of occupancy or up to end of 2020. However,
in 2021 rents could not be collected due to COVID-19 in Kapuas.

12.      The number of houses built in NSD Kapuas at project completion was 165 unit, or lower
than the target of 200 units. By the end of project, there were only 21 households occupying the
houses as the government continued to provide canopy and kitchen for the remaining 144 units.
Out of the remaining 35 units not yet constructed, the government secured DGHP allocation in
2020 for the construction of 16 units. However, the construction in 2020 did not occur due to
COVID-19. Moreover, the Regent issued a Decree No.154/BPBD TAHUN 2020 dated 6 April
2020, which stipulated that the NSD houses be designated as COVID-19 quarantine facilities.
Given that 21 households already occupied the houses, the government made two rows of
housing as the barrier between the houses designated for COVID-19 quarantine facilities and
those occupied by the 21 households.

                         CIVIL SOCIETY ORGANIZATION ENGAGEMENT

1.      At appraisal, the project was estimated to have meaningful civil society organization (CSO)
participation through information generation and sharing, consultation, collaboration and
partnership. During implementation, the project worked with the communities through community-
based organizations or Badan Keswadayaan Masyarakat (BKM). The summary poverty reduction
and social strategy of the project confirmed the BKMs are CSOs. In addition to BKMs, following
the completion of infrastructure construction, operation and maintenance (O&M) groups that
consisted of communities who used the infrastructure conducted the infrastructure O&M.

2.      The BKMs were involved through a series of meetings. The first meeting discussed
community institutional capacity assessment. The second meeting discussed the result of the
neighborhood self-assessment including the condition of the existing infrastructures and listing of
infrastructure construction and upgrading needs. The third meeting reviewed the Neighborhood
Upgrading Action Plan. The fourth meeting discussed the preparation of civil works
implementation. The fifth meeting evaluated the implementation of infrastructure upgrading and
construction of the previous year.

3.     Information generation and sharing. The project published posters containing project
information, which were gradually distributed to the participating cities from 2016 to 2018. Project
documents and reports were disclosed on both project and the Asian Development Bank (ADB)
websites. During the BKM meetings, project information and progress were also disseminated to
BKMs, while the BKMs provided information on the condition of existing infrastructure to verify
the needs of infrastructure construction.1 Furthermore, the BKMs were also engaged in the
dissemination of grievance handling mechanism. Hence, there was high information generation
and sharing in the project.

4.       Consultation. During the third meeting, the BKMs evaluated the revised Neighborhood
Upgrading Action Plan (NUAP) that was preapproved by the Local Coordination Office (LCO).
Furthermore, the BKMs were consulted in the preparation of the annual work plans including the
type and amount of community self-contribution to the upgrading activities. The consultations on
the civil works carried out by the contractor were also conducted with the BKMs on environmental
safeguards. Furthermore, a public consultation on the environmental management and
monitoring plan (UKL-UPL) for the New Site Development (NSD) was organized by engaging the
BKM.2 Thus, there was high consultation on the project.

5.      Collaboration. BKMs were involved in the preparation of NUAPs as well as the
community action plans (CAPs), which specified the detailed works and payment modalities.
Furthermore, the BKMs represented the communities during the signing of community-based
contracts with the LCO. During the implementation of civil works, the community advisor, LCO,
and project management consultants provided technical guidance to the communities.
Furthermore, BKM, through the facilitation of community advisor, monitored the implementation
of environmental mitigation measures. Therefore, there was a high collaboration in the project.

6.    Partnership. The BKMs, with the facilitation of community advisor, carried out the project
implementation at neighborhood level. This includes the BKM to manage the utilization of block

             SUMMARY OF GENDER EQUALITY RESULTS AND ACHIEVEMENTS

I.      Project Description

1.       The Neighborhood Upgrading and Shelter Project (NUSP-2) aims to improve Indonesia’s
urban areas’ living conditions by improving infrastructure and access to service delivery in project
sites’ slum neighborhoods. Three outputs were utilized to reach the outcome: (i) strengthening
the institutional capacity for managing pro-poor urban development, (ii) upgrading the
infrastructure in slum neighborhoods, and (iii) establishing low-income families’ new settlements
through the NSD (New Settlements Development) projects, where the project built a new
neighborhood with a good access to water, electricity, and road for low-income household through
a build-to-rent scheme. It was implemented from 2014 to 2019, where NSD projects are
continuing. Targeted low-income households and slum areas in 20 local governments in
Indonesia, this project was categorized as effective gender mainstreaming (EGM).

II.     Gender Analysis and Project Design Features

A.      Gender Issues and Gender Action Plan Features

2.      Through the Gender Action Plan, NUSP-2 aims to enhance women's ability to participate
in the decision-making process and ensures that women and men will benefit equally from
improved urban infrastructure. The key gender issues addressed by the project included (i)
women's, including female headed households, particular needs and preferences related to type
and location of public and neighborhood facilities; (ii) reduced work and time burdens related to
familial and community chores; (iii) greater access to information and participation in local
decision-making processes related to basic services and infrastructure; and (iv) improved
ownership of and control over resources and assets. Specifically, the project was designed to
enhance women's ability to participate in the decision-making process of basic infrastructure,
including sanitation facilities development, and ensure that women and men in the project sites
benefit equally from increased access to improved infrastructure.

B.      Overall Assessment of Gender Related Results and Achievements

3.      At the end of the project, the gender action plan (GAP) of NUSP-2 had 26 actions and 8
quantitative targets.1 It achieved 92.30% actions and 62.50% targets, which are equivalent to 24
out of 26 for the actions, and 5 out of 8 for the targets. The GAP’s aggregate achievement was
85%. Those unachieved targets and actions were about the low number of women’s participation
in several parts of the project and the difficulties in ensuring, utilizing, analyzing, and developing
the sex-disaggregated data into a further application.

member, BKM secretariat, and member of UPK, UPL, and UPS, was 33%3 out of the required

D.        Participation, Access to Project Resources, and Practical Benefits

8.       Women’s participation in public activities (e.g., public consultation, socialization,
project-led community organizations). A total of 59,077 women participated throughout the
project consultation and meetings, and they received the same access as men to information,
such as dissemination workshops and training.8 The gap between women and men in participation
in project consultation and meetings was partly due to the inability of the project to fully engage
women in the activities. The invitation letter to the events for instance was addressed only to the
head of household, which are predominantly men, due to the patriarchal values that position men
as the leaders and representatives of the families in neighborhood meetings and other public
activities. As a result, women are not used to being involved actively and speak up in public group
discussions with majority of men in attendance.

9.      When women are in public, their activities in fact still revolve around stereotypical roles
assigned to women such as PKK or arisan. To address this, Rembug Khusus Perempuan (Special
meetings for women group) was established throughout project locations to serve as platform for
women to freely discuss various community issues related to the project. Women are also part of
the community forum (Forum Silaturahmi Masyarakat) in Kota Bima and sit in the management
positions (10 out of committee members, 3 are women) established to maintain facilities and serve
as a platform to discuss community issues, including monthly housing rent, or neighborhood
security. However, the PCR mission has not observed strategic changes in women leadership.
Target 4 of GAP showed that 971 women (31%) took leadership role in the BKM. The data did
not exclusively record the women as heads of the BKM, but also subdivision heads and other key
positions in the organization. Women as agents of change, who have a degree of influence in the
community has not been observed.

10.     Improved knowledge and capacity of women in government agencies and
community. A total of 4,258 community women and female government staffs (35%) out of
12,255 participants received training in all community-based capacity development activities
generated through the project, such as implementation, monitoring and evaluation, financial
management, relevant civil works, and operations and maintenance (O&M). Women throughout
the projects also experience other practical benefits in multiple aspects. In education, 176 women
(31%) out of 561 participants received a scholarship to enroll in Universitas Gajah Mada master’s
degree program and short training in space planning topics, such as Neighborhood Settlement
Upgrading and Urban Land Management. The knowledge on O&M the communities received
from the project encouraged them to initiate new activities. In the KPP group in Toro subdistrict,

12.     Integrating/mainstreaming gender considerations in project planning and
implementation (project management interventions). On the aspect of project management,
there was low awareness and understanding of the gender action plan (GAP) and lack of
supporting roles of the gender focal points (GFPs). The PCR mission shows that GAP was not
disseminated for the NSD project. Dissemination to both community and the government were
limited to the project’s O&M procedure and code of conduct. Both governments from Bima and
Palopo were not aware of GAP and collected sex-disaggregated data without further processing
and analysis. At least in the NSD project, there is a gap between the consistency and flow of
gender information throughout the NUSP-2 project. As it is relatively new, the sex-disaggregated
data collection was deemed an achievement. At the same time, more assessment needs to be
done on whether the data is being effectively used and analyzed. It is also important to note that
throughout the project there were no specific initiatives to address gender division of labor, gender
stereotyping, women’s leadership, and related impact analysis throughout the project that would
be useful reference to yield more analysis on gender strategic changes in the communities, in the
PCR.

E.     Strategic Changes in Gender Relations

13.     In Palopo and Bima, both the community and government officials strongly favor existing
gender roles and gender division of labor within households and communities. Men were
perceived to be doing, and actively volunteered to do, heavy tasks such as construction jobs or
even visitation to the fields, which require considerable time spent outdoors. In comparison,
women were perceived as more suitable and accordingly were assigned to the job that needs
more thorough and careful execution, for example accounting and administration. There is also a
notion where men should protect women and allow or letting the women do crude work is
perceived as diminishing family pride, which affects women's involvement in the civil works
project. Furthermore, there were no changes before and after the project was finished regarding
gender roles and stereotypes. This to some extent has affected the way gender and social
inclusion was being mainstreamed in the project. Even though the government and the community
recognize the advantage and benefits in providing sex-disaggregated data and increasing the
52     Appendix 12



number of women participants in a project, there is no inclination to make a task, both before,
during, and after the project, such as technical and construction work, perceived as suitable for
men available for women.

14.     Current everyday lives affect each gender. Between Palopo and Bima, they did not show
stark differences in how the project affect gender relationships in everyday life. A distinct
household division of labor was also observed. Men were functioning as earners who work outside
the home to provide the family with their income. In contrast, women were supporting the family
by doing household work and taking care of the children. However, several situations, such as
when women are away or busy with other tasks, could make men spend more time helping to do
the housework. Due to the improved living quality in the housing after the project, for instance,
men are seen to spend more time in their home and women had extra time from the reduced time
of doing daily work such as collecting water. When the women were busy with their housework
and the men were at home, they would help them. Alternatively, when an unexpected situation
comes, and it reduces the men's incomes, the women would help to look for extra income, usually
from selling daily goods or food. Men’s roles as proxy to women when it comes to household
chores, and conversely, women’s position as proxy to men when it comes to income generation,
remain unchanged in the communities. In the case of women heads of households, the NSD
group is the one who comes into support as together with other women they managed to start
collective business, which helped them with their daily expenses.

15.    In Palopo and Bima, both men and women in families will discuss big decisions together,
especially those related to finance. However, the final say will be made by the husbands, as heads
of households. For daily household spending, women usually get trusted to manage the families’
petty cash. In terms of accessing water and electricity, there is no discrimination against each
gender. However, knowledge resources available from outside of the project were generally
associated with gender roles and stereotyping assigned to women and men limited to the gender
perceived suitable for the activity. For example, a cooking class was more targeted at women,
whereas a construction workshop was targeted at men.

F.     Contribution of Gender Equality Results to Overall Loan Outcomes and
Effectiveness

16.     Each output in NUSP-2 integrates gender aspects: (i) strengthening the institutional
capacity for managing pro-poor urban development, where its second performance indicators
require at least 100 local government staff that were trained are women; (ii) upgrading the
infrastructure in slum neighborhoods where the representation of women is required; and (iii)
establishing low-income families’ new settlements through the NSD projects, where it pushes
women heads of households as a priority for the selection of beneficiaries. The post-project
survey showed that more than 90% of females were satisfied with the project. Interviews with the
women from the NSD project also show satisfaction, as living in the newly developed
neighborhood greatly improves their lives.

17.      The project, through GAP, has initiated activities to improve the communities’ capacity
and active involvement in the infrastructure’s operation and maintenance (O&M). Women’s
representation in these capacity building activities and O&M Group (KPP) memberships are also
boosted. Post project, community-based KPP registers multiple post-project activities operated
and handled by women groups, such as in Makassar, Bone, Palopo, Bima, and Pasuruan. Those
types of post-project sustainability by the beneficiaries were O&M administrative work and waste
management in which the women communities will set aside profit from the work to finance O&M
activities. O&M construction such as road reparation, bridge, and drainage were observed to be
                                                                                 Appendix 12    53


limited following the minimum amount of women participation in those aspects due to the strong
perception of existing gender roles.

18.      Sex-disaggregated data and gender focal points (GFPs) initiated through GAP were
expected to serve as anchors in development planning and implementation beyond the project.
However, since the initiatives were relatively new, the PCR mission revealed that government
officials in Bima and Palopo did not implement sex-disaggregated data and assigns GFPs before
and after NUSP-2. This made the data collection unable to contribute further than fulfilling the
targeted number requirement and report pages. Eight cities, such as Batam, do escalate the data
for their respective SIAP documents. However, the unequally distributed practice made the
attempt to achieve the target result ineffective. Those were especially considering that the cities
that implement those practices are under 50% of participating cities.

D.     Lesson Learned and Recommendations

19.     Continuity and equal understanding are crucial factors in achieving GAP targets in a
project that spans multiple years. GFP was designed to ensure that the gender mainstreaming
attempt was delivered from the project. What was lacking in NUSP-2, especially the lack of gender
interventions in the NSD project, was caused by miscommunication among the project
implementers in ensuring the NSD project to assign GFP and subsequently, implementing GAP.

20.     Gender stereotyping and strong gender division of labor were evident in project locations.
This in turn has affected the way women and men were involved in the project in which minimum
women’s involvement in public activities and leadership roles were observed. There was no,
however, specific initiative in the project that addresses these gender issues. In the end, related
GAP actions and targets were not achieved, and gender strategic changes were not significantly
seen.

21.     Implementation of gender targets and actions is considered relatively new, and at least in
Palopo and Bima, the people still have a deep set of sociocultural values on how women and men
should operate and perform their roles in society. To mitigate this, the following are
recommended:
      (i) The Asian Development Bank (ADB) and the Project Management Unit (PMU) should
            ensure future projects to keep the flow of information on GAP standardized, steady,
            and thorough during the whole project through strengthened coordination between
            gender specialists, GFPs and other technical teams. Capacity-building sessions on
            the concept and scope of gender and social inclusion in general and GAP
            implementation specifically should be conducted among the project teams on regular
            basis, preferably before, during and when the project is approaching its end term.
      (ii) A more intensive approach to raise awareness and understanding of gender issues
            and GAP implementation targeting women and men in the communities through
            regular training should also be explored. Relevant tools such as Gender Action
            Learning System (GALS) to address women and men’s power relations and decision
            making within the households and women’s leaderships can be used as references.
            This is particularly important to help shatter the traditional gender roles and gender
            stereotyping that are proven harmful in achieving gender equality. As also explored in
            the previous sections, communication materials on gender and social inclusion can
            be developed and disseminated to the communities by taking into account specific
            needs and interests of specific groups such as women, women heads of households,
            children, and the poor.
54     Appendix 12



     (iii)   To trigger women’s involvement in project construction, there needs to be more efforts
             within the project to increase women’s interest in civil works. This can be done through
             socialization that any job is a woman’s job and application of technology or machinery
             to ensure the work can be done by women and men. Exploring women’s local potential
             outside the technical aspect is also suggested to improve the project’s direct benefit
             for women.

B.      Land Donation

25.     As the project adopts the Community-Driven Development (CDD) approach, the land
donation was based on community self-survey from which the community identify the need for
infrastructure mentioned in the Neighborhood Upgrading Action Plan (NUAP) document. The
need for piece of land for infrastructure was then being identified, discussed and assessed during
the Kelurahan Meeting II (Musyawarah Kelurahan/Muskel II) through meaningful consultation.
Further, in the Muskel III, the community finalizes and agrees upon the CAP, including the land
donation and proceed with signing the statement of land donation later on. In fact, these land
donations were voluntary which also the forms of community contribution to the project without
any coercion, as they were aware of the benefit or positive impacts of the infrastructures being
built.

26.     The procedure applied in the project for land donation is as follows:
        (i)    After identifying the piece of land needed for the project, the BKM, the community
               advisor and/or city coordinator checked the ownership of this land along with the
               supporting document (land title) and visual inspection on the site.
                                                                                          Appendix 13        67


       (ii)       The related parties then had a meeting with all heirs of the landowners, or if the
                  land was state-owned, the meeting involved village chairmen, key persons and
                  village representatives, to come up with an agreement on the land donation or land
                  use permit or passing permit. This meeting was held in a participatory, consultative
                  and persuasive manner, assuring there was no coercion to those who are opted
                  for the contribution.
       (iii)      Having secured the agreement, the community advisor then prepared all the
                  supporting documents to clear the status of land, including statement letter on
                  voluntary land donation or land use permit or passing permit, and form of land
                  information and APs’ economic profile. This form provided information on the
                  proportion of land donated, and brief socio-economic profile of landowner to make
                  sure that the voluntary donation would not severely affect the living standard of the
                  APs.
       (iv)       The BKM and community advisor conducted land delineation and measurement,
                  sketched the map of the piece of land to be included in the land donation
                  statement, and wrote the minutes of meeting (Berita Acara) of this process.

27.    The process of land provision that may need land donation had been done in an open and
transparent manner through the Muskel II and III. Written agreement on donation have been made
and signed by the donator and witnessed by several parties representing communities, LKM,
TIPP, and neighborhood. Some examples of these written records have been included in semi-
annual social monitoring reports. The verification of voluntary donation was carried out in 2019
by an independent party and draft report was submitted to ADB in March 2020. ADB provide
comments on the report, yet no revised report was submitted to ADB for approval.

D.           Safeguard Reporting

34.     The project was categorized B for involuntary resettlement, hence the Resettlement
Framework was prepared and disclosed on ADB website in January 2014. As five cities triggered
involuntary resettlement, DDR for acquiring land in each city was prepared and disclosed on ADB
website. Reporting on the implementation of the involuntary resettlement was on a semi-annual
basis. The first social monitoring report, which covered the period up to June 2017 was disclosed
on ADB website in December 2018. The subsequent semi-annual reports were periodically sent
to ADB and disclosed on ADB website with the latest report submitted and disclosed covered the
period of January - December 2020.

E.           Lessons and Recommendation

35.    Due to the absence of land acquisition related documents prior to the commencement of
construction in Pekalongan and Kapuas, construction was at risk to be stopped as no mitigation
measures prepared and agreed to be delivered to the APs. Strong facilitation from the consultants
and robust monitoring by ADB and the CPMU are critical to ensure mitigation measures are
implemented prior to construction.

36.     Given that after the project was closed several income restoration programs were not yet
fully delivered to the APs in Kapuas, close coordination between the CPMU and local
governments have been done to ensure that local budget is available to facilitate the program.
Local governments have agreed to complete the pending issues in 2021. Further monitoring
needs to be carried out by the CPMU (DGHS) on the program implementation. A closer
coordination with the local government for social safeguards and an endorsement on social
safeguards by local government needs to be paid attention for the social safeguard program in
the future projects.

37.      No proper documentation of land donation, particularly in 2015 and 2016 showed poor
recording at community level. The role of community advisors and city coordinators is critical to
ensure that the process of land donation is recorded appropriately through written documentation.
To enable the community advisors and city coordinators to carry out this critical role, capacity
development though refresher training for instance to the community advisors and city
coordinators, the importance of the recording of the land donation process can be further
prioritized.

                                             ECONOMIC ANALYSIS

A.       Background

1.      The Neighborhood Upgrading and Shelter Project (Phase 2) sought to improve living
conditions in urban areas of Indonesia.1 The project was prepared in the context of the National
Medium-Term Development Plan (RPJMN)2 2010–2015 to contribute to reducing poverty and
closing gaps in income inequalities in disadvantaged urban neighborhoods by upgrading basic
public infrastructure in slums. It contributed to meeting the target of Millennium Development
Goals (MDGs) in achieving significant improvement in the lives of slum dwellers and reducing the
proportion of urban households without sustainable access to safe drinking water and basic
sanitation. Furthermore, it was to contribute to the implementation of the Cities without Slums
Program to address issues of rapid urbanization.3

2.       The project aimed to demonstrate an integrated approach to upgrade slum neighborhood
by (i) providing resource directly to disadvantaged communities to improve their living conditions;
(ii) establishing new settlements for poor families; and (iii) as part of the capacity development,
assisting local administrations in managing pro-poor urban development including the
development of multisector strategies to address urban poverty issues beyond infrastructure
upgrading.

3.     The project outcome was improved infrastructure and access to service delivery in slum
neighborhoods in 20 Indonesian cities.4 The project’s outputs are (i) strengthening institutional
capacities for managing pro-poor urban development, (ii) upgrading infrastructure in slum
neighborhood, and (iii) establishing new settlements for poor families.

B.       Economic Evaluation

4.     The economic analysis at completion presents the costs and benefits of the project at
completion following ADB’s Guidelines for the Economic Analysis of Projects 2017. The economic
analysis at completion follows the same methodology used during appraisal.

5.      Main assumptions. At appraisal, the project assumed quantifiable and unquantifiable
economic benefits. Among the unquantified benefits include reduction in the number of
unproductive days and reduced time for accessing water and sanitation services. Improvements
in water, sanitation, and drainage was seen to improve the overall health of the community.
Further unquantified benefits of flood control infrastructure include (i) significant additional days
of operation of government services where these are no longer being flooded, (ii) improved health
of inhabitants were existing sanitization services are no longer being flooded, (iii) reestablishment
of lost production and production days for small businesses where their stock and sales points
are no longer flooded, and (iv) depending on the extent of flooding usually experienced, a
potentially large reduction in the maintenance costs of public infrastructure, such as pathways
that no longer get washed away.

6.      The economic analysis at appraisal identified the following quantifiable economic benefits
(i) improved income earning opportunities (ii) improved healthy environments and (iii) reduced
cost of drinking water. For consistency of methodology, these same benefits will be valued at
completion cost benefit analysis. The detailed explanation on the assumptions behind the benefits
and how they were derived are explained in the next paragraphs.

7.      At appraisal, the benefit of improved income earning opportunities (increased
incomes) assumed that people engaged in informal economic activities will have a better working
environment in communities for income-earning opportunities of small home industries,
particularly related to food processing, handicraft, batik, and small repair services. Upgrading
roads and pathways, and improving water and power supply, and providing common working
spaces will improve the conditions for community-based small-scale businesses of informal
entrepreneurs (e.g., food-stall owners, small restaurants, repair shops). Organized clean common
spaces will be used for drying batik, making handicrafts, processing food, and selling products.
Improved roads in slums will provide easier and safer access for consumers to reach shops and
workshops. Improved drainage systems will contribute to reduced damage and loss of stocked
products due to flooding. At appraisal, about 20% of the 670,000 households impacted by the
project, or 134,000 households, are engaged in small businesses as their primary source of
income; the project was expected to improve their monthly net income by at least 3%, starting in
year 4. For calculation purposes at appraisal, a monthly household income of Rp3,500,000 was
assumed for households involved in informal sector activities.

8.      The benefit of improved healthy environments (avoided public health cost) was
obtained from an estimate avoided public health costs associated with the discharge of raw
sewage into open water bodies. The avoided health costs of having access to a piped sewerage
system are estimated on the order of Rp47,500 per person per year for urban households.5 This
assessment was carried out for the ADB-financed Metropolitan Sanitation Management and
Health Project but since urban slum households face the same health costs, this was used as
proxy for the health benefits of the project: at appraisal 268,000 households are expected to
benefit from improved sanitation services. With an average 5 persons per household, 1,340,000
people were estimated to be affected. At a benefit of Rp47,500 ($4.32) per person per year, this
was estimated at appraisal $5.79 million per year starting in year 4.

9.      At appraisal, the project benefit of reduced cost of drinking water (avoided cost of
alternative sources of water) targeted improving water supply facilities and systems to benefit
134,000 slum households. These households are assumed to not have access to safe drinking
water, and thus are required to buy drinking water from vendors at a monthly cost of Rp50,000.
After the project these households will benefit from new water supply facilities and will not need
to buy drinking water from vendors. It was assumed that these households will pay a monthly fee
for water from the new water supply facilities (Rp15,000), and still boil the water for drinking
purposes (Rp5,000 for gas), the saved costs for water will be Rp30,000. Thus, at appraisal, the
annual savings are estimated at about $4.4 million starting in year 4.

ADB’s ordinary capital resources to finance the civil works, institutional development, and
consulting services; (ii) $21.6 million from government, comprising $6.4 million from the central

infrastructure will cost $50,000 annually. At completion due to absence of data the same costs

there is no recurrent cost from year 1 to year 3. OPEX costs recur from year 4 onward.

  STORIES OF PROJECT IMPLEMENTATION BY THE COMMUNITY IMPLEMENTATION
                            ORGANIZATIONS

1.      Neighborhood Upgrading and Shelter Project Phase 2 (NUSP-2) utilized a small direct
community contracting package to pay the community civil works. The project package was
planned by the facilitated community implementation organization (Badan Keswadayaan
Masyarakat [BKM]), and the fund was directly channeled to them (Appendix 6). The benefits of
this mode of fund flow could be observed as (i) building the CIO’s sense of belonging to the
project, thus empowering and built the community capacity; (ii) plan developed by the CIO could
cover oversight from more centralized planning; (iii) it efficiently injecting the fund to the local
community through work opportunities and local enterprise through procurement. However, it was
not without challenges. First, non-centralized construction control will cause material usage, and
construction quality varies from one place to another. Second, planning and implementation time
must be prepared to face a more extended period due to the time taken for training and
disseminating to the community. The following paragraphs explore excellent practices which
emerge from the project.

2.      From tidal flooding prone area to a green and clean neighborhood. The common
challenge in Pekalongan is tidal waves, which cause flooding in neighborhoods, of which one of
them was a slum neighborhood in Tirto neighborhood. The road damage in the subdistrict due to
the inundation led to mobility restrictions. Furthermore, the infrastructures, e.g., drainage, solid
waste, were already in poor conditions, which dragged the neighborhood into slum area. The
project intervention led to the shift from partial handling concept to collaboration concept between
multiple stakeholders. The collaboration also included the improvement of arterial road, financed
by the local government budget and upgrade of roads and drainage, financed by the block grants.
The infrastructure upgrade led to no inundation on the roads, house yards, and vacant land. The
community also contributed to landscape and cultivate corps and flowers as well as run its
maintenance which was encouraged by the BKM. This has improved Tirto neighborhood into a
slum-free area which is also green and clean. The success story in Tirto neighborhood had made
the area as one of the pilot neighborhoods in Pekalongan City.

                                      FINANCIAL ANALYSIS

1.      Financial analysis provides a sound analytical basis to conclude whether the executing
agency and/ or implementing agency is financially capable of implementing the project and
operate and maintain it in a financially sustainable manner over the project's economic life.
Meanwhile, financial evaluation assesses the project's inherent financial viability without any
reliance on external support, and whether, during the operational phase, it will generate adequate
cash resources for the project owner to operate and maintain the investment, and service the
capital. However, due to the nature of the infrastructure built is generally social infrastructure that
does not generate income such as drainage, environmental roads, solid waste services,
wastewater services, street lighting, fire engines and green open spaces, so the value of money
from cashflow stream as measured by the net present value (NPV) and internal rate of return
(IRR) during the project could not be done.

2.      The parties responsible for operating and maintaining the Neighborhood Upgrading and
Shelter Project – Phase 2 (NUSP-2) infrastructure are the local governments and the communities
in the project sites. The local governments are responsible for the operation and maintenance
(O&M) of the entire infrastructure including major damage, while the community is expected to
operate and maintain the minor repairs. The local government through their departments carry
out the O&M based on the principles of autonomy and assistance tasks. Table 18.1 shows the
responsibility of each department such as the Department Public Work is responsible for
managing drainage, road and street lighting, the Department of Environment is responsible for
managing solid waste, fire systems, waste water, toilets, green open spaces, Water Utility
Company (PDAM) is responsible for the provision of drinking water, the Department of Housing
and Human Settlement is responsible for managing the New Site Development (NSD), and the
village is responsible for the infrastructure which built based on community participation.
Meanwhile, the source of funds to finance the O&M of NUSP2 infrastructure comes from various
sources including the local budget (APBD), PDAM, and the village funds. The community can
also provide their contributions.

3.      The local government budgets through their departments, allocate their O&M budget for
the entire local governments. Meanwhile, 50% of village funds, which come from the central
government get can be used to cover O&M costs. These village funds are transferred from central
to the local government and be allocated to the village. The total annual O&M budget for village
fund of 20 cities is Rp30.6 billion or $2.198 million. In addition, there are 260 NSD houses in 2020
with an average monthly rental rate of Rp100,000 or a year of Rp312 million or $0.022 million. In
2022, the number of houses to be rented will increase to 420 houses due to the rental contract
by Mayor of Kapuas and Kendari will be implemented, therefore and estimated annual rental
income will be Rp504 million or $0.036 million. The rental fee in 2020–2021 for Kapuas cannot
be implemented because the house is used for the isolation process of patients suffering from the
coronavirus disease (COVID-19). As for Kendari, the local government is collecting data,
disseminating information related to NSD regulations to beneficiaries, and evicting illegal
residents and those who are not from low-income communities. The budget from village funds as
well as NSD house rental fee will be $2.20 million or 4.4 times higher than the estimated O&M
costs at appraisal of $500 million.

4.     Because of COVID-19, the central government refocused its budget by increasing health
spending and providing stimulation to the economy, reducing the local government and village
fund budgets. On the financial analysis, the assumption of the village fund to maintain O&M, is
reduced to Rp100 million from originally Rp150 million per village. By refocusing on the village
112    Appendix 18



funds and NSD house rental fee, the total budget for O&M will be $1.47 million or 2.9 times higher
than the estimated O&M costs of $500 million.

5.     The O&M costs for infrastructure and NSD have been covered by sources of funds from
local government budgets, village funds and house rent, therefore this project is financially
sustainable with or without COVID-19 adjustment and can be used by beneficiaries continuously.
