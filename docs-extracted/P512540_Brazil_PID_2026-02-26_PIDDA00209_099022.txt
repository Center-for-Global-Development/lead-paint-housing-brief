 B. Introduction and Context

 Country Context

The municipality of Fortaleza has not grown and generated jobs commensurate with its size and economic status due
to urban fragmentation that prevents the city from reaping the benefits of urban agglomeration and poor fiscal standing
that hinders its ability to provide public services and infrastructure to support growth and jobs. Despite its size and
dynamism, Fortaleza’s labor markets, firms, and services remain spatially disconnected. This fragmentation weakens firm-
worker matching, raises mobility and service-delivery costs, and amplifies exposure to climate and security risks, ultimately
constraining productivity and employment. At the core of this challenge lies weak and misaligned land-use regulation,
where zoning and planning instruments have failed to steer compact, mixed-use, and transit-oriented development. This
operation addresses these challenges by supporting urban policy reforms that permit denser housing near jobs center and
transport axis, and fiscal reforms to increase revenues and control expenditures.

Despite being a major economic engine in Northeast Brazil, Fortaleza is not fully converting its scale and dynamism into
productive and inclusive employment. As Brazil’s fourth largest city—with 2.4 million residents1—Fortaleza generates 37
percent of Ceará’s GDP and employs 53 percent of the state’s workforce.2 Its service-driven economy (83 percent of GDP)
benefits from strong tourism demand and a growing digital ecosystem, anchored by its strategic coastal location and more
than fifteen international submarine cables. Tourism plays a central role and it’s a top priority for Fortaleza: the
municipality receives around 3 million visitors per year, generating about 200,000 direct and indirect jobs, and in the first
half of 2025 alone, 2.1 million tourists generated R$13 million in economic activity and sustained over 322,000 jobs, a
year-on-year increase of 55,000.3 This highlights the importance of urban conditions – such as safety, heat mitigation, and
safety—for the service sectors that underpin local economic growth. Major private investments, including Amazon Web
Services planned R$7.7 billion data center initiative, underscore Fortaleza’s potential as a regional hub for high-value
services.4 The Pecém Industrial and Port Complex5 –which will enable clean hydrogen production–also offers important
spillovers for Fortaleza, particularly as the relocation of fuel storage operations from the Mucuripe Port will free 46.89
hectares of prime land for mixed-use development.6 Yet these advantages remain underleveraged due to deep-rooted
structural challenges of spatial fragmentation, environmental and security constraints, and widespread informality.

Rapid but unplanned urban expansion has entrenched fragmentation and eroded productivity. Over the last two
decades, Fortaleza’s urban footprint has expanded by more than 70 percent while population grew by only 20 percent,
producing one of the highest levels of spatial dispersion among Brazilian state capitals. This pattern reflects zoning and
land-use regulations that have allowed increased urban sprawl at the periphery while failing to incentivize density, mixed
use, and redevelopment in serviced areas. The result is a city where households and firms are increasingly distant from
one another, weakening labor-market integration and raising infrastructure costs. Sprawl has also fueled widespread
informality. Around 60 percent of residents live in areas with low or very low quality of life, and 856 informal settlements
house more than 271,539 families with inadequate services. As formal housing near employment centers becomes scarce
or unaffordable, low-income households are pushed into environmentally fragile or flood-prone areas. This spatial
mismatch reinforces fragmentation, increases climate exposure, and limits access to jobs—particularly for workers
dependent on public transport.
Fragmentation is compounded by mobility inefficiencies and rising climate stress, which directly reduce productivity.
Peripheral neighborhoods experience average commuting times exceeding 60 minutes each way, shrinking effective job-
search areas and weakening firm-worker matching. These effects fall disproportionately on low-income households,
women and informal workers. Environmental pressures intensify these constraints. Consistent with the Country Climate
and Development Report (CCDR) findings, rising urban heat is emerging as a material drag on productivity, especially in
service, construction, transport, and tourism sectors.7 Fortaleza exhibits some of the highest urban heat island intensities
among Brazil’s coastal cities due to vegetation loss, extensive impermeable surfaces, and unregulated construction. Heat
studies show surface temperatures exceeding 27°C in dense built-up areas in Fortaleza.8 Extreme heat reduces labor
productivity, raises health costs, and slows economic activity in many cities, with projected GDP losses in major urban
centers reaching 5 percent or more in the coming decades. 9 Informal settlements—often located in poorly serviced, heat-
exposed areas—bear the highest burden: households are most exposed, often living in neighborhoods with little green
space, poor-quality housing, and limited mobility options beyond walking or public transport.
Severe security challenges further weaken agglomeration benefits. Fortaleza records one of the highest violent crime
levels in Brazil, with a crime index of 75.85 and very low perceptions of nighttime safety. Homicides rose by 13 percent in
2024, with some districts experiencing increases above 65 percent.10 Crime patterns overlap spatially with informality,
poor lighting, heat stress, and weak state presence—areas where fragmentation limits both policing effectiveness and
informal surveillance. These conditions sharply constrain evening mobility and commercial activity, suppress tourism
spillovers, and deter private investment. The interaction between fragmentation, heat, and insecurity creates a reinforcing
cycle in which workers and firms cannot fully exploit proximity, density, and scale—core drivers of urban productivity.
The proposed US$194.76 million standalone DPF directly addresses these constraints through a coherent reform
package to support improved zoning, urban upgrading, safety, and fiscal reforms to unlock productivity and job creation
in the long term. Zoning and land-use actions through a coherent Master Plan tackle fragmentation at its source by
promoting compact, mixed-use, and transit-accessible development, strengthening agglomeration and labor-market
integration. 11 Urban upgrading measures—particularly in informal settlements—combine drainage, green infrastructure,
lighting, and public space improvements to reduce heat exposure, enhance safety, and improve access to jobs. Expanded
public lighting and surveillance systems address one of Fortaleza’s most acute barriers to productivity and inclusion by
improving real and perceived safety, enabling evening economic activity, and reinforcing tourism and service-sector

growth. Fiscal reforms—including cadaster modernization, improved property tax valuation, and strengthened public
lighting fees—ensure that these spatial and safety gains are financially sustainable. Stable and predictable revenues allow
the municipality to maintain infrastructure, reduce investor risk, and crowd in private participation. Together, these
reforms establish a clear results chain: prior actions on zoning, safety, climate resilience, and fiscal sustainability reduce
fragmentation; reduced fragmentation strengthens agglomeration; stronger agglomeration raises productivity, resilience,
and inclusive job creation. By explicitly linking spatial planning to economic outcomes, the DPF lays the institutional
foundation for Fortaleza to convert its scale and assets into sustainable urban development and long-term economic
transformation.

 Relationship to CPF

The proposed operation is aligned with Brazil’s Country Partnership Framework (CPF) FY2024–FY2028. The Brazil CPF
for FY24-28 (Report No. CPF0000013) was endorsed by the World Bank Board on April 9, 2024, and is built on three High-
Level Objectives (HLOs): (i) greater productivity and employment; (ii) greater inclusion of the poor and underserved
populations; and (iii) a greener economy with reduced vulnerability to climate shocks. The DPF supports HLO1, specifically
objectives 1.2 (greater and greener competitiveness) and 1.4 (expanded and modernized infrastructure), HLO2,
specifically objective 2.1 (improve access to essential services and products), and HLO3, specifically objective 3.3
(promoting green and resilient cities and communities). Aligned with the CPF, the proposed operation aims to strengthen
the municipality’s urban and fiscal sustainability, improving its planning, management, and funding of interventions across
several sectors relevant for Fortaleza’s economic competitiveness, including through leveraging private sector capital and
expertise, while preserving environmental protection areas and providing social housing.

 C. Proposed Development Objective(s)
 To promote urban and fiscal sustainability in the municipality of Fortaleza

  Key Results
The measures supported by the operation are expected to catalyze job creation and long-term economic transformation,
by reducing spatial barriers, strengthening labor mobility, and helping correct the regulatory and land-use distortions that
fragment labor markets—ultimately supporting a more dynamic urban economy capable of generating better and more
widely shared jobs. Fiscal reforms will help strengthen public finances, both improving short term fiscal performance and
safeguarding long-term sustainability.

 D. Project Description
The DPF strengthens Fortaleza’s urban and fiscal sustainability by establishing the foundations for a more efficient and
productive pattern of urban development. Reforms promoting compact, transit-oriented growth, stronger environmental
management, and well-located social housing reduce spatial barriers, support labor mobility, and enhance the city’s
capacity to generate jobs. Measures advancing climate resilience improve livability and economic performance, while
expanded funding sources and minimum allocations for social housing help catalyze private investment in inclusive,
job-enabling urban regeneration. Complementary fiscal reforms—strengthening revenues and improving expenditure
discipline—help sustain investment and crowd in private participation, reinforcing the conditions for job density, job
quality, and more equitable growth.

 E. Implementation

 Institutional and Implementation Arrangements
The Municipal Secretariat of Finance (SEFIN) and Urban and Environment (SEUMA), as the two implementing agencies of
this operation will be responsible for collecting and monitoring information related to program implementation and
progress toward the achievement of the results. The Bank team will work closely with SEFIN and SEUMA to define results
indicators that are measurable and attainable, giving preference to those that are already planned to be collected as part
of the prior action policy instruments or are otherwise regularly tracked by the municipal agencies.

 F. Poverty and Social Impacts, and Environmental, Forests, and Other Natural Resource Aspects

 Poverty and Social Impacts
The prior actions contained in Pillar I will have an indirect positive impact on poverty creating fiscal space for pro-poor
investments. PA1 and PA3 will increase the capacity of public investment by the municipality, implying an improvement
in the services offered to the population in general, but on which the poorest layers are more directly dependent. Poor
households will be protected from negative impacts of the changes to the urban property tax cadaster, as low-value
properties continue to be exempt from the tax and therefore mitigate regressive effects. PA2 will have two positive
impacts – the reduction of spending by low-income households who become exempt from paying the public lighting fee
and who benefit from the impacts that derive from the improvement of the public lighting system in terms of mobility
and safety.12

The prior actions of Pillar II will have direct positive impacts on the poorest and most vulnerable population of the city of
Fortaleza. These impacts are related to the expansion of the real estate stock and investments for the improvement of
social housing (PA4 and PA6), the improvement of access to public transport and the reduction of the degree of exposure
to risks and impacts exacerbated by climate change (PA5). It is estimated that the revision of the Fundurb (PA6) will double,
between 2025 and 2027, municipal investments in housing improvements and triple the number of families served. This
action has a significant impact in terms of gender equity and social inclusion: the explicit targeting of social housing policies
and the establishment of minimum funding allocations ensure that resources reach those most in need, with anticipated
positive effects on gender equity and the inclusion of marginalized groups.13 Through the Municipal Policy on Climate
Change, the municipality will prioritize strategies to combat urban heat, reduce the vulnerability of communities exposed
to extreme events and the effective participation of the most vulnerable social groups in the formulation, execution,
monitoring and evaluation of public policies related to climate.14

The operation is likely to have potential beneficial effects on Fortaleza’s environmental priorities, particularly under Pillar
II (PA4 and PA5). Potential significant adverse effects of the operation on the environment, forests, and other natural
resources are unlikely. The approval of the revised Plano Diretor (PA4) and Municipal Climate Change Policy (PA5) would
strengthen the municipal environmental policy by increasing the land area designated as zones of relevant environmental
interest and strengthening regulations to protect green areas and increase soil permeability within the city, as well as by
increasing strategic arborization to create green corridors and reduce temperatures, contributing to the climate
adaptation of the urban environment. No significant environmental impact or benefit is expected from the other Prior
Actions as defined at this stage.

 G. Risks and Mitigation

The overall residual risk rating for the operation is assessed as moderate. The institutional capacity for implementation
and sustainability is rated substantial risks to the achievement of the PDO. A description of these risks and their respective
mitigation measures is provided below.

Institutional Capacity for Implementation and Sustainability are substantial. Implementing property tax increases may
be challenging without penalties for noncompliance. To mitigate this, the reform package will adopt a phased rollout,
allowing piloting and gradual scaling as institutional capacity strengthens, alongside clearer compliance mechanisms.
Strong capacity for monitoring and enforcing the new zoning code is essential. While expanding environmental zones and
earmarking land for social housing may constrain developable land and put upward pressure on prices, this risk will be
mitigated through targeted land-use planning, coordinated sequencing of zoning changes, and complementary housing
programs for low-income households. Insufficient funding for the Municipal Climate Change Policy also poses a risk; to
address this, the municipality will diversify financing sources, prioritize cost-effective actions, and leverage technical
assistance from the World Bank and other partners. Robust monitoring and evaluation systems will track progress, enable
adaptive management, and ensure corrective measures can be introduced as needed.

Other key risks are rated as moderate. Macroeconomic risks stem from Brazil’s broader fiscal pressures and exposure to
external shocks, but Fortaleza’s prudent fiscal management help to mitigate these concerns. Sectoral and technical risks
reflect the complexity of multi-sector reforms and the need for effective inter-agency coordination, while institutional
capacity risks are managed through targeted training and leveraging lessons from previous operations. Fiduciary,
environmental, and social risks are also considered moderate, supported by Fortaleza’s established regulatory frameworks
and oversight mechanisms. Stakeholder risks are addressed through active engagement and transparent communication.
