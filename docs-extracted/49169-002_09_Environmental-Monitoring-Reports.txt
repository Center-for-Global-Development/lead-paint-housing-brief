EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

 1. This Environmental External Monitoring (EEM) report presents the findings of the 4th semi-annual
environmental external monitoring for the Ulaanbaatar Green Affordable Housing and Resilient
Urban Renewal Sector Project (AHURP), covering the period from July to December 2025. The
report was prepared by the environmental external monitoring joint venture of “BOAD” LLC and
“Unet Baigali” LLC, in compliance with the Asian Development Bank (ADB) Safeguard Policy
Statement (SPS) (2009) and relevant environmental regulations of Mongolia.
 2. The monitoring covered 8 active construction sites across the Bayankhoshuu and Sharkhad
subcenters. Environmental monitoring activities included measurements of air quality, noise, and
water quality conducted on 7 October 2025, and inspections of occupational health and safety
(OHS), waste management, and soil quality conducted on 6 November 2025. The monitoring also
assessed the implementation status of site-specific Contractor’s Environmental Management Plans
(CEMPs), compliance with environmental safeguard requirements, and overall site environmental
performance.
 3. AHURP aims to support climate-responsive urban development in Ulaanbaatar by transforming
ger areas into low-carbon, climate-resilient, and affordable eco-districts. The project will deliver
approximately 5,190 green housing units and redevelop around 40 ha of land into mixed-use,
climate-resilient urban areas. The intended impact is improved living conditions through enhanced
access to safe, healthy, and environmentally sustainable urban infrastructure.
 4. Overall, environmental performance across the monitored construction sites is assessed as
generally satisfactory. Most environmental impacts associated with construction activities remain
localized, temporary, and manageable through the implementation of mitigation measures. Air
quality, water quality, and soil quality monitoring results were within applicable Mongolian standards.
However, noise levels exceeded permissible limits at several monitoring points, indicating the need
for strengthened noise control measures. Contractors have taken initial mitigation actions, including
restricting construction activities during nighttime.
 5. Implementation of CEMPs across contractors ranged from 83% to 97%, with most contractors
achieving performance levels close to or above 90%. The lowest implementation rate (83%) was
recorded at the B13/1AB site, indicating the need for targeted corrective actions and closer
supervision. Key gaps identified include inconsistent implementation of waste management
measures, incomplete documentation, and variable quality of environmental reporting.
 6. Waste management remains an area requiring further improvement across several sites. Issues
include incomplete waste segregation, inadequate temporary storage arrangements, and insufficient
tracking of waste disposal. In addition, contractors’ reporting practices require strengthening,
particularly in providing verifiable evidence of CEMP implementation and compliance.
 7. A Corrective Action Plan (CAP) has been developed and shared with all contractors to address
identified non-compliances. Site-specific recommendations focus on strengthening waste
management practices, improving environmental monitoring and reporting, enhancing contractor
capacity, and ensuring consistent implementation of mitigation measures. Environmental training
and on-site guidance have been conducted to support contractors in improving compliance.
 8. In conclusion, the project remains compliant with ADB Safeguard Policy Statement (SPS, 2009)
and national environmental safeguard requirements, with no significant or irreversible environmental
impacts observed. Continued attention is required to improve noise management, waste handling,
and reporting quality. Implementation of the recommended corrective actions will further strengthen
environmental performance during the next reporting period.

     1.2 Purpose of the report and detailed tasks of EEM
11. The EEM aims to evaluate the AHURP's compliance with the environmental safeguards policies
of the ADB and Government of Mongolia (GoM). It seeks to identify potential environmental impacts
in project sites and impact zones, propose measures for preventing and mitigating negative
environmental effects, and address issues that could adversely affect the environment during project
implementation. This involves conducting field analysis, investigations, and comprehensive
environmental monitoring which covers soil, water, noise, air quality assessments as necessary to
ensure outstanding environmental performance of the AHURP. The objective and assignment of the
EEM is to:
a) Ensure that implementation is following the project’s Environmental Management Plan (EMP)
     and environmental safeguards requirements of ADB and GoM;
b) Conduct periodical environmental monitoring, field surveys, analysis, and sampling;
c) Assess and identify potential impacts and suggest measures to prevent, reduce and mitigate
     environmental negative impacts and other issues during the project implementation onsite and
     within the impact zone;
d) Conduct training of and consultations with stakeholders as and when required;
e) Prepare and submit required reports;
f) Review, check and evaluate the credibility and accuracy of periodical project progress reports
     (environment sections) and internal environment monitoring reports.
12. The purpose of this report is to conduct an external and independent inspection of the
 environmental safeguards performance of construction sites B13, B15, S27/2 and S27/5 under the
 period of the second half of 2025, and to develop recommendations if any deficiencies are found,
 to inform the public about the improvement of the environmental and social performance of AHURP
 by providing external evaluation and training to project stakeholders.

6   CONCLUSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
6.1 Overall Conclusion
107. This EEM confirms that construction activities under the Sharkhad (S27/2, S27/5) and
Bayankhoshuu (B13, B15) subcenter developments are generally implemented in compliance with
national environmental requirements and standards (MNS) and are broadly aligned with applicable
ADB safeguard requirements. Environmental impacts remain largely localized, temporary, and
manageable through the implementation of mitigation measures. These overall findings are
supported by the results of environmental quality monitoring and site inspections, as summarized
below.
108. B15 Site – Landscaping Work: Nano Ecology LLC is responsible for the landscaping and external
works under the B15 subproject at the Bayankhoshuu subcenter. At the time of the EEM site
inspection, no active works were ongoing, as the contractor’s activities had been temporarily stopped
onsite due to winter preparation. The last activity involved tree planting on 27 September 2025, which
falls within the appropriate autumn planting season. Consequently, the contractor was not present on
site, and the onsite assessment was not undertaken during the EEM inspection. However, the
contractor implemented its 2025 CEMP, and the corresponding report was submitted to the UBED,
where it was evaluated as 95.8% satisfactory.
109. Ambient Air Quality. Across all monitored sites, gaseous pollutants (NO₂, CO, O₃) largely
complied with Mongolian standards and WHO guideline values. Exceedances were primarily
associated with particulate matter (PM10, PM2.5) and sulfur dioxide (SO₂) at specific monitoring
points in S27/5, S27/2, B13, and B15. These exceedances were generally minor and classified as
“moderately polluted” under the Air Quality Index, indicating localized and short-term impacts rather
than systemic deterioration. The results suggest that dust generation from construction activities,
combined with surrounding traffic and urban background pollution, remains the principal air quality
risk factor.
110. Noise. Environmental noise monitoring was conducted at 4 project sites (S27-5, S27-2, B15,
and B13) under generally clear weather conditions and light wind speeds. Measurements were taken
at three locations at each site, including the upwind area, within the construction site, and the
downwind area.
111. The measured noise levels ranged from 50.02 to 71.50 dBA. At the S27-5 site, noise levels were
relatively low and no significant project-related noise impact was observed due to minimal
surrounding activities. At the S27-2 site, the noise level measured within the project site slightly
exceeded the WHO (1999) guideline but remained within the permissible limit of MNS 4585:2016;
higher values at surrounding points were mainly influenced by nearby traffic and pedestrian
movement.
112. At the B15 and B13 sites, relatively higher noise levels were recorded (up to 67.86 dBA), primarily
due to active construction activities and heavy vehicle movement during the monitoring period.
113. Overall, the monitoring results indicate that noise levels were largely influenced by temporary
construction activities and surrounding urban traffic, and generally remained within the permissible
limits of the Mongolian standard MNS 4585:2016.
114. Soil Quality. Soil monitoring shows no widespread contamination, with heavy metals generally
within MNS 5850:2019 limits. Localized arsenic exceedances were observed, mainly at B15 and
slightly at B13-4, likely due to natural background conditions rather than construction impacts. Overall,
no significant project-related contamination was identified; continued monitoring is recommended.
115. Water Quality. Groundwater quality at Bayankhoshuu B13 remained within baseline conditions
and complied with relevant national standards (MNS 6148:2010; MNS 0900:2018). The Water Quality
Index classified groundwater as “Good,” and no construction-related adverse impacts were identified.
This demonstrates effective protection of groundwater resources during the reporting period.
116. Occupational Health and Safety (OHS). OHS performance across most contractors was
generally satisfactory to compliant. At S27/2 and S27/5, compliance rates were high, with only minor
non-compliances related mainly to site organization. At B13 and B15, most checklist criteria were
met; however, isolated deficiencies particularly incomplete fire safety equipment were identified.
Overall, OHS management systems are functioning but require continued supervision for
improvements to ensure consistent implementation across all contractors.
117. Waste Management and Grievance Redress Mechanism (GRM). Waste management
performance shows significant variability between contractors and sites. While some contractors
(e.g., E&F blocks at S27/2) demonstrated satisfactory compliance, others particularly at B13 recorded
substantial non-compliances, including inadequate hazardous waste storage, insufficient waste
segregation, poor record-keeping, sanitation shortcomings, and weak public grievance
communication. In several cases, site inspections could not be completed due to contractor absence
or seasonal suspension of works, limiting full compliance verification. Waste management remains
the weakest environmental management component and requires urgent and systematic corrective
action at underperforming sites.
118. In summary, environmental monitoring results indicate that project-related impacts remain
generally limited in scale and manageable in nature. Air quality impacts are minor and site-specific,

                                                   46
groundwater quality remains unaffected, and soil contamination is not widespread. Environmental
safeguards performance has improved compared to previous monitoring periods, with notable
progress observed at the Sharkhad subcenter. Notwithstanding these improvements, waste
management performance remain the key areas requiring further strengthening. Addressing these
gaps through more consistent implementation, strengthened supervision, enhanced environmental
capacity of contractors, and effective follow-up of corrective actions will be important to sustain
compliance and further enhance overall environmental performance.

7   VALIDATION OF INTERNAL ENVIRONMENTAL MONITORING
123. The 12th semi-annual internal Environmental Monitoring Report (EMR), prepared by the
PMO for the period July-December 2025, documents the implementation of environmental
safeguards for AHURP. The report outlines compliance with ADB SPS (2009), relevant GoM
environmental regulations, and site-specific CEMPs. Key internal monitoring findings,
including environmental performance indicators, occupational health and safety management,
and corrective actions, were reviewed and cross-validated against external monitoring
observations. Overall, the internal monitoring framework remains systematic and well-
structured. However, several recurring non-compliances indicate the need for stronger
enforcement of follow-up actions and improved contractor capacity in safeguard
implementation.

The 12th Internal EMR summary
Project Implementation
124. Construction progressed at Bayankhoshuu (B15, B13-1/B13-2) and Sharkhad (S27-2,
S27-5), with building works at various stages ranging from structural completion to final
finishing. Several packages have reached 60-80% completion.
Environmental Management and Compliance
125. A total of 11 internal monitoring visits were conducted by PMO, PIMS, and EFDP
environmental specialists at both Bayankhoshuu and Sharkhad sites.
Recurring non-compliances were identified in:
    - Gaps were identified in the timely implementation of corrective actions for non-
        compliances identified by EEM
    - Waste segregation and temporary storage management
    - Sanitation facility maintenance
126. While many issues were corrected, several non-compliances remained pending at the
end of the reporting period, indicating a need for more consistent follow-up and closure
verification.
Environmental Quality Monitoring
127. The environmental quality monitoring was conducted by contractors at Bayankhoshuu
and Sharkhad as well as EEM company at Bayankhoshuu B15 and B13 and Sharkhad S27-2
and S27-5 sites including air, and soil sampling that were analyzed at the accredited
laboratories.
128. Arsenic (As) was not analyzed as it was not included in the standard soil analysis package
selected by the contractor. Arsenic analysis will be explicitly requested and included in the
next monitoring and reporting period to ensure full compliance.
Community Engagement and Grievance Redress
129. The AHURP GRM was revised in 2025, and formally approving the updated GRM
procedures and establishing roles, responsibilities, and response timelines for receiving,
reviewing, and resolving grievances submitted by project-affected persons and stakeholders.
130. As part of the GRM requirements, information on the project activities is disseminated
through the project website (https://ub-greenhousing.mn), the project’s Facebook page
(AHURP), the information board at the Business Incubator Centre at the Bayankhoshuu site
with affected households and residents, GRM boxes at the construction sites at the
Bayankhoshuu and Sharkhad.
131. A total of 44 participants (14 men and 30 women) from construction contractors and the
project team were briefed on the key principles of the GRM during the safeguards training
sessions held during the reporting period.
132. No grievances related to environmental safeguards were received during the reporting
period through GRM channels.
Institutional Strengthening
133. Capacity-building workshops were held in March and April 2025, focusing on waste
management, internal environmental control, GRM procedures, and environmental reporting.
Contractors reported improved awareness, however, high turnover of contractor
environmental focal points continue to hinder consistent safeguard performance.
Green Certification
134. The first phase of the project has been successfully implemented, and 590 apartments
have received EDGE (Excellence in Design for Greater Efficiencies) certification of
international green building standards which issued by World Bank group
Conclusion
135. The 12th internal semi-annual EMR confirms overall compliance with ADB SPS (2009) and
national environmental safeguards and demonstrates ongoing monitoring, corrective action
enforcement, and community engagement. However, the recurrence of waste management, non-
compliances indicates that corrective actions are not always being sustained at the contractor
level. It is recommended that:
   •   Corrective action tracking be strengthened through documented closure verification,
   •   Contractor environmental focal persons remain consistent.
   •   Refresher and on-site based environmental trainings need be conducted to ensure
       continuity of safeguard performance.
   • Continue to ensure that CEMPs and their implementation reports prepared by contractors
       are reviewed by the PMO and PIMS prior to submission to UBED to ensure accurate and
       complete reporting.
These measures will improve alignment between internal and external monitoring outcomes and
support sustained compliance throughout the construction period.

Key Environmental and OHS Performance Issues
138. Air Quality: Repeated exceedances of SO₂ and particulate matter (PM₁₀ and PM₂.₅)
were recorded at some locations. While partly influenced by urban background conditions,
mitigation measures such as dust suppression and proper equipment maintenance were not
consistently applied during the final phase.
139. Noise: Exceedances of permissible noise levels were observed, particularly in areas
affected by surrounding traffic. Noise control measures were implemented inconsistently by
the contractors.
140. Waste Management: Recurring issues included inadequate waste segregation, improper
storage (including hazardous waste), and incomplete record-keeping. In some cases,
hazardous waste management requirements were underestimated or not fully addressed.
141. OHS: Gaps were identified in fire safety arrangements, inspection records, and PPE
management. Safety measures were not consistently maintained, particularly toward the end
of construction.

Overall Observations

142. A generally acceptable level of environmental and OHS performance was observed
across completed contracts, particularly during the main construction stages. However, a
consistent pattern emerged whereby performance weakened for several contractors during
the final stage, as priority shifted toward physical completion and handover. This resulted in
delayed corrective actions, incomplete documentation, and unresolved issues at closure.
Project-wide monitoring further indicated continued air and noise exceedances at several
locations, uneven implementation of HSE measures across contractors, and a decline in site
housekeeping and environmental controls toward demobilization.
143. Despite these issues, certain parameters, such as water quality and selected OHS
practices, remained satisfactory at some sites, demonstrating that compliance is achievable
with sustained management attention.
Key Lessons Learned
144. The experience gained from completed contractors provides important lessons for future
construction packages. The findings highlight that environmental management must remain
an active and continuous process throughout the entire construction lifecycle, including the
final stages of subproject delivery. Particular emphasis should be placed on maintaining
environmental control measures, completing outstanding corrective actions, and ensuring that
all documentation requirements are finalized prior to project closure.
145. Based on the performance observed across completed contracts, the following key
lessons are identified for future contractors:
     • Environmental and OHS compliance must be maintained consistently through the final
        construction and handover stages, not only during peak construction periods.
     • Waste management systems, including segregation, storage, and documentation,
        must be established early and maintained until site demobilization. All contractors
        should ensure that hazardous waste is managed through formal agreements with
        licensed waste management companies.
     • Air quality control measures, including dust suppression and equipment maintenance,
        must remain operational until all site activities are fully completed.
     • Noise mitigation practices should be systematically implemented and monitored,
        particularly in urban environments influenced by traffic conditions.
     • Fire safety and emergency preparedness systems must be fully operational and
        regularly inspected throughout the construction period.
     • Corrective actions identified during monitoring must be tracked to closure, with clear
        documentation confirming full implementation prior to contract completion.
     • The presence of qualified and experienced environmental and OHS personnel of
        contractor at site level is critical for effective safeguards implementation. Capacity
        building should be initiated from the early stages of construction and extended to
        project managers and engineers, not only environmental staff. Strong management
        support is confirmed to be essential to ensure allocation of resources, timely
        implementation of corrective actions, and overall improvement of environmental
        compliance.

1. INTRODUCTION
A. Purpose of the Report

1. The 12th semi-annual Environmental Monitoring Report (EMR) covers the “Ulaanbaatar Green
Affordable Housing and Resilient Urban Renewal Project” (AHURP) environmental monitoring
progress for July – December 2025. The report was prepared under the supervision of the Project
Management Office (PMO) with the assistance of the Project Implementation Management
Support (PIMS) environmental specialists.
2. The report is presented on a semi-annual basis as required by ADB’s project implementation
guidelines and environment safeguards, and in line with Government of Mongolia’s (GoM)
national environmental legislation focusing on environmental protection and management.
3. The semi-annual EMR provides:
    -   a progress report on the status of the implementation of the Project’s Environmental
        Management Plan (EMP)
    -   an overview of environment management works, and mitigation measures undertaken
    -   An assessment of the environmental impacts of the project’s activities and key
        environmental issues
    -   information on the project’s environment monitoring plan and activities, and recommended
        measures for further improvement
    -   public consultations organized at project sites regarding Health, Safety and Environment
        (HSE) concerns
    -   health and safety of public and personnel issues and measures undertaken
B. Project Background

4. The AHURP aims to bridge current gaps in climate-responsive urban development and green
affordable housing in support of redeveloping Ulaanbaatar’s informally settled ger areas. The
objective of the project is to deliver sustainable and comprehensive solutions to transform the
substandard, climate-vulnerable, and heavily polluting ger areas of Ulaanbaatar city into
affordable, low carbon, climate-resilient, and livable eco-districts.
5. Each sub-project consists of the redevelopment of a selected ger area into an eco-district. The
housing units to be constructed in one eco-district should comprise 15% for social housing, 55%
for affordable housing, and 30% for market-rate housing. Implementation of the project’s Phases
may overlap depending on available resources and the speed with which the necessary feasibility
studies and due diligence are completed.
6. The project's expected outcome is increased access to low carbon and climate-resilient eco-
districts and green affordable housing in Ulaanbaatar ger areas. The project has three outputs:
-   Output 1: Resilient urban infrastructure, public facilities, and social housing units constructed
    in ger areas.




                                                 87
-     Output 2: Long-term financing to developers for low carbon affordable housing, market-rate
      housing, and economic facilities in ger areas and to households for green mortgages
      increased.
-     Output 3: Sector policy reforms implemented, and capacity strengthened.
7. The overall project implementation is expected to span from late 2019 to 2027.

B. Institutional responsibilities for Environmental management
32. The EMP for AHURP’s areas of Selbe and Bayankhoshuu, that was developed as required
by ADB’s safeguard policy statement during the project preparatory phase, guides the
implementation of the eco-districts through the pre-construction, construction, and post-
construction operation phases to ensure that unnecessary environmental and social impacts
identified in the IEE do not occur, and to measure and document any unexpected impacts of the
subcomponents.
33. The EMP defines mitigation, and monitoring measures and describes the institutions,
responsibilities, and mechanisms to monitor and ensure compliance.
34. The management responsibilities for the implementation of the EMP are based on the
following:
           a. The Municipality of Ulaanbaatar (MUB)is the Executing Agency (EA) for AHURP
              and has overall responsibility for the implementation of the EMP via high-level
              decision support provided, when necessary, by a Project Steering Committee
              (PSC) established by MUB for AHURP.
           b. The PMO is responsible for the day-to-day management of the EMP
              implementation with appointed safeguard staff (SS). A licensed EIA Mongolian
              Firm will be engaged to conduct all environmental baseline studies and
              assessments.
           c. Support for the implementation of the EMP will be provided by PIMS, along with the
              safeguard officer of ADB. The construction package contractors will be responsible
              for implementing the EMP with their own contractor CEMPs that they will develop
              from the EMP as part of their bid documents.
35. Affected khoroos through their designated counterpart with PMO will actively participate in:
(a) public disclosure of AHURP IEE, EMP and EMRs, (b) the community awareness program on
health and safety impacts of AHURP implementation; (c) establishment of health and safety
baseline data prior to construction; (d) review of Environmental Monitoring Reports (EMRs) and
results of environmental monitoring by contractors reports which will be available at khoroo
offices, and (e) ensure necessary corrective actions are taken for reported environmental/social
exceedances that are confirmed as being caused by contractor negligence.
36. ADB is responsible for reviewing,clearing and disclosing relevant assessments and reports,
and conducting periodic review missions to review, among others, the implementation of required
environment safeguards.

42. During internal environmental monitoring at the construction sites, several environmental
non-compliance issues were identified that required corrective actions. The most recurrent issue
related to inadequate waste management, including accumulation of construction waste on site,
lack of timely removal to designated disposal facilities, insufficient waste segregation, and
absence of proper labeling of waste bins, particularly at the Bayankhoshuu sites (B13, B15) and
during follow-up monitoring in November and December. In some instances, waste was
improperly disposed of in open areas adjacent to residential fences.

43. Sanitation and wastewater management issues were also observed, including insufficient
cleaning and disinfection of toilets and inadequate domestic wastewater management at selected
sites, notably at the Sharkhad site (S27-2), where wastewater containment required improvement
as replacing with a larger sewerage tank. In addition, spill response preparedness was found to
be inadequate, as spill kits were missing or not properly used at several sites, prompting the
installation of spill kits and reinforcement of spill response practices.

44. Furthermore, site organization and housekeeping deficiencies were identified, including
poor storage and segregation of construction materials, disorganized work areas, and general
lack of cleanliness at some sites, particularly during the early monitoring period. Temporary site
infrastructure, such as fencing, was damaged during adverse weather conditions and required
repair and reinforcement. Minor gaps in community communication tools, including the condition
and placement of grievance/suggestion boxes, were also noted and subsequently addressed.

45. These findings underscore the need for continued supervision, improved housekeeping
practices, timely implementation of corrective actions, and strengthened contractor compliance to
ensure full adherence to environmental safeguard requirements. Corrective actions were
undertaken by the contractors and informed to the PMO accordingly as shown in Table 32.

76. The Health and Safety management of the project activity is reflected in the IEE, DEIA and
EMP documents in parallel with the environmental quality standards and criteria for Mongolia.
The environmental standards provided by the Environmental, Health and Safety Guidelines of
the IFC/World Bank (2007) are also considered to supplement GOM standards, if required.
77. A Health and Safety team composed of EFDP Safety Specialist and NOSK Safety Specialist
in charge of the supervision, and thirteen (13) OHS Specialists appointed by contractors at

and Hygiene (2008), Law on the Disaster Protection (2003) and the Law on the Fire (2015) and
other relevant standards during the construction at the project sites.

contractor and implemented at project sites by the respective Safety Officers. Within the plan's
framework, daily safety instructions were given to construction workers every morning before
work at the project sites. Occupational health and safety training, site management, and
prevention from potential occupation hazards at the workplace were provided to workers at the
Bayankhoshuu and Sharkhad sites by the safety team during project implementation.
79. Regular weekly health and safety inspections were conducted at Bayankhoshuu and
Sharkhad sites by the NOSK Safety Specialist. The field inspections identified occupational
safety-related non-compliances, and corrective measures were put in place promptly by
contractors.

80. On 27th and 28th October 2025, a two-day training on working at height was organized under
the project for engineering team members and contractors. A total of 16 participants including 12
men and 4 women attended the training organized by the WAHA – Working at Height Association,
a professional occupational safety training organization. The training focused on working-at-
height safety requirements and rescue techniques, including risk identification, fall prevention
measures, and the correct use of personal protective equipment (PPE). Practical, scenario-
based exercises were carried out to strengthen participants’ ability to apply safe working
practices under real site conditions.

81. At the end of the training, participants successfully passed the theoretical examination with
a minimum score of 80% and the practical assessment with a minimum performance score of
85%. Certificates authorizing participants to work at height were issued upon successful
completion of the training. The training agenda, list of participants, certificates, and photo
documentation are provided in Appendix 5.
82. Contractors ensured safety of workers at the Bayankhoshuu B15 and B13 and Sharkhad
S27-2 and S27-5 sites. Safety reports were prepared by the contractors and submitted to NOSK
in charge of the project's construction supervision.

Key issues identified, actions taken, and additional actions recommended

85. Based on internal monitoring results, key environmental issues identified during the
reporting period included inadequate waste management practices, poor sanitation conditions,
missing spill kits, absence of grievance-related tools, improper storage of equipment, and
unregulated wastewater discharge. These findings highlighted the need for strengthened site
management and continued oversight.

11. CONCLUSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS

87. The overall implementation of the project’s environmental safeguards has continued to be
carried out in compliance with the environmental safeguard requirements of the Asian
Development Bank (ADB) and applicable Mongolian environmental laws and regulations. The
project environmental team secured the necessary approvals and permissions from the UBED to

                                                   117
support CEMP implementation by contractors. During the reporting period, multiple contractors at
the Bayankhoshuu and Sharkhad sites obtained CEMP approvals for implementation, while newly
contracted companies commenced preparatory activities following contract signing in October–
November 2025, with construction works scheduled to start in Q1 2026 due to the usual pause of
works during winter. Several construction packages were completed, with state commissioning
conducted for completed works at Bayankhoshuu and Sharkhad, while a limited number of
commissioning acts remain pending due to external works or administrative processes.
88. Internal environmental monitoring was conducted regularly at the Bayankhoshuu (B15 and
B13) and Sharkhad (S27-2 and S27-5) sites, including bi-weekly inspections during active
construction and additional end-of-season checks. Monitoring identified recurring issues mainly
related to waste management, including waste accumulation, delayed removal to designated
disposal sites, and inadequate segregation. Sanitation and wastewater management gaps,
missing or improperly used spill kits, minor fencing damage, poor housekeeping and material
storage, and shortcomings in grievance and information tools were also observed. These findings
indicate the need for continued supervision and timely corrective actions to maintain
environmental compliance.
89. A total of eleven contractors has been engaged across the Bayankhoshuu (seven
contractors) and Sharkhad (four contractors) sites. Initial challenges related to communication,
environmental awareness, and familiarity with international safeguard requirements particularly
among newly mobilized contractors were observed. However, continuous monitoring, on-site
guidance, and targeted training by the project environmental team have contributed to improved
understanding and implementation of environmental safeguards. Nevertheless, frequent turnover
of contractor environmental focal points remains a key challenge, affecting continuity, institutional
memory, and the sustained application of corrective measures.
90. To address identified capacity gaps, the project placed strong emphasis on strengthening
contractors’ knowledge of environmental safeguards and occupational health and safety. An
environmental safeguards training was conducted on 7 October 2025, with a total of 14
participants (5 men and 9 women) representing construction contractors, focusing on key CEMP
requirements, environmental compliance obligations, and site management responsibilities. In
addition, a two-day working-at-height training was organized on 27–28 October 2025 for
engineering team members and contractors. A total of 16 participants attended the working-at-
height training, including 12 men and 4 women, of whom 14 participants (5 men and 9 women)
represented construction contractors. Together with regular on-site guidance and environmental
monitoring, these capacity-building activities contributed to improved awareness and more
consistent implementation, monitoring, and reporting of CEMPs across the project sites.
91. Independent EEM continued to play a critical role in verifying environmental compliance and
contractor performance at the project sites. The fourth round of EEM was conducted at the
Bayankhoshuu and Sharkhad sites on 7–8 October 2025, focusing on overall environmental
compliance. In addition, waste management and safety monitoring were carried out at both sites
on 7–8 November 2025. Monitoring assessed contractors’ performance against the approved
CEMPs and identified areas requiring corrective actions. During the reporting period, gaps were
identified in the timely implementation of corrective actions for non-compliances identified by
EEM. Delay mainly stemmed from the time taken to formally communicate the EEM findings to
contractors, as EEM reports were submitted to the PMO up to three months after the site visits.
92. To address this issue, improvements are required to ensure that key non-compliances are
shared with contractors quickly after on-site EEM, prior to issuance of the full EEM report, together
with a clear timeline for corrective actions. During the reporting period, the second EEM was
disclosed on the ADB website, and the third EEM report was submitted to ADB by the PMO in
December 2025.

93. Environmental monitoring and sampling for air, soil, water, and noise were undertaken by
contractors and the EEM team at the Bayankhoshuu and Sharkhad sites. Monitoring results
confirmed that all measured parameters complied with relevant Mongolian standards (MNS). No
heavy metal contamination was detected, and no water pollution or bacterial contamination was
identified in water kiosks located near the project sites. Identified minor issues were addressed
through site-specific mitigation measures and corrective actions outlined in the respective
CEMPs.
94. EBAs were conducted for the SCADA site at Selbe and the 4,918 meter 10 kV electrical
transmission line at the Sharkhad subcenter. The Sharkhad EBA supported the issuance of the
GEIA conclusion, while the Selbe SCADA EBA highlighted environmental sensitivities, leading to
a subsequent site adjustment and ongoing revision of the assessment. These baseline studies
provided an essential foundation for environmentally responsible project planning and
implementation and will continue to guide compliance during upcoming construction phases.
95. Community-based participatory monitoring (PCM) was conducted at the Bayankhoshuu and
Sharkhad sites on 13 and 14 October 2025, respectively. Two monitoring groups were formed,
each comprising 15 community members, resulting in the participation of a total of 30 community
representatives, including 21 women, residing in the vicinity of the construction sites. PCM
strengthened community awareness of environmental and safety requirements, supported the
early identification of site-level non-compliances, and enhanced transparency and trust between
the project and local communities. Overall, the participatory monitoring process contributed to
reinforcing environmental safeguards implementation and promoting shared responsibility for
community safety and environmental protection.
96. Information on the GRM continued to be widely disseminated through environmental and
social safeguards training sessions, public consultations, and community meetings. During the
reporting period, a total of 44 participants (14 men and 30 women) from construction contractors
and the project team were briefed on the key principles, procedures, and communication channels
of the GRM as part of safeguards training activities. The revised GRM was formally approved in
2025 through an official PMO order, strengthening roles, responsibilities, and response timelines.
No grievances related to environmental safeguards were received during the reporting period
through GRM boxes, contractors, khoroo, or any other channels, indicating that the GRM
remained accessible and functional throughout the reporting period.

OFFICE OF THE MAYOR OF ULAANBAATAR DEPARTMENT FOR THE DEVELOPMENT OF
HOUSING AND GREEN AREAS OF THE CAPITAL CITY ORDER

Date: 16 September 2025

Order No.: A/10

Ulaanbaatar City

Regarding the Establishment of a Complaint Handling Procedure

Pursuant to Article 6.3 of the Law on Administrative Procedure, Articles 7 and 22 of the Law on
Citizens’ Petitions and Complaints, and Article 7 of the Law on Urban Development, and in
connection with ensuring the implementation of citizens’ rights to submit petitions and
complaints regarding the activities of the Capital City Housing and Green Area Development
Department, IT IS HEREBY ORDERED AS FOLLOWS:

    1. To approve and implement the Procedure for Receiving, Reviewing, and Resolving
       Petitions and Complaints submitted by citizens and legal entities in relation to projects
       and activities implemented within the jurisdiction of the Capital City Housing and Green
       Area Development Department.

    2. To assign responsibility to relevant divisions and staff members to ensure that petitions
       and complaints are registered, reviewed, and resolved in accordance with applicable
       laws and regulations, within the prescribed timeframes.

    3. To ensure transparency and accessibility of the complaint handling process by
       providing clear information to citizens on complaint submission channels, review
       procedures, and response timelines.

    4. To monitor implementation of this procedure and take corrective measures where
       necessary to improve the effectiveness of grievance handling.

    5. This Order shall take effect from the date of issuance.
