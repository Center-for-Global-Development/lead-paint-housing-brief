                         I.   PROJECT DESIGN AND IMPLEMENTATION

A.   Rationale

 1.      Bangladesh’s gross domestic product grew at an average annual rate of 5.9% from fiscal
year (FY) 2009 to FY2013.1 The country’s urban population grew from 20.0% in 1991 to 29.30%
(33.56 million) in 2011, reaching 31.5% (52.04 million) by 2022.2 About half of the 42.0 million
urban population (footnote 2) lived in 329 pourashavas (secondary towns). 3 Economic and
population growth led to substantial changes in these towns, including rural–urban transformation,
growth in employment opportunities, spatial changes, and increased demand for improved
infrastructure and services. However, continued population growth—accelerated by rural-to-urban
migration—was expected to exacerbate municipal service delivery unless the challenges of weak
infrastructure and governance were addressed.

2.      For decades, pourashavas struggled to provide adequate urban services because of aging
infrastructure, inefficient resource use, and an inability to keep up with rapidly increasing
demands. The growing population, coupled with ongoing rural-to-urban migration, placed
mounting pressure on existing systems, exposing gaps in service delivery and governance.
Without targeted intervention and comprehensive reforms, these challenges threatened the
quality of life and long-term development goals in urban Bangladesh.

3.      Overall road conditions had deteriorated because of insufficient maintenance and
increased traffic.4 At appraisal, drinking-water supply coverage in urban areas was recorded at
86.0%, while access to piped water supply was only 32.0%.5 Water supply in pourashavas was
characterized by inconsistent availability, poor quality, and, in some cases, arsenic contamination.
Only 55.0% of the urban population had access to improved sanitation facilities, slightly below the
national coverage of 57.0% and lower than the South Asia average (footnote 5). Solid waste was
unsystematically dumped in open areas, posing public health risks and emitting greenhouse
gases. Public toilet facilities, especially for women, and sewer network coverage were limited.
Stormwater drainage systems were inadequate, leading to flooding and reduced climate change
adaptive capacity. Furthermore, pourashavas faced challenges in providing amenities such as
kitchen markets, streetlights, parks, playgrounds, community centers, and bus terminals. They
were characterized by inadequate governance and limited community involvement in project
planning and implementation. People living in slums were excluded from decision-making, lacked
basic amenities and services, and had limited access to livelihood training. Incentives to improve
governance in areas such as accountability, transparency, gender equity, poverty reduction,
financial management, and sustainability were limited, and overall efforts remained weak.

4.     The Urban Governance and Infrastructure Improvement Project (UGIIP I) implemented a
performance-based funding allocation approach through the Urban Governance Improvement
Action Program (UGIAP).6 Building on UGIIP I, the Second Urban Governance and Infrastructure

allocated funds to 20 top-performing pourashavas nationwide, based on a separate UGIAP.13
Performance indicators for both window A and window B pourashavas are provided in Appendix
2. Adoption of performance-based allocation through the UGIAP significantly enhanced
governance in the funded pourashavas.

8.     Impact. The project aimed to improve urban living conditions in line with the Seventh Five-

were appointed to undertake (i) performance monitoring and evaluation, (ii) benefit monitoring
and evaluation, and (iii) gender development and poverty alleviation services. Overall, the
implementation arrangements functioned adequately to deliver the project efficiently.

G.      Procurement

19.     As envisaged at inception, all procurement of civil works, goods, and services followed
ADB’s Procurement Guidelines (2015, as amended from time to time). Three procedures were
used: (i) international competitive bidding; (ii) single-stage, one-envelope national competitive
bidding; and (iii) shopping. In addition, community participation in procurement was adopted for
most of the slum improvement works. National competitive bidding followed the government’s
Public Procurement Rules, 2008, with agreed modifications set out in the procurement plan.
Consultants were recruited following ADB’s Guidelines on the Use of Consultants (2013, as
amended from time to time). Three consulting firms were recruited using quality- and cost-based
selection: (i) MDS consultants (para. 17), (ii) GICD consultants (para. 17), and (iii) project
readiness consultants for preparation of the IUGIP (footnote 21). In addition, 12 individual
consultants (para. 17) were recruited through competitive individual consultant selection.

20.     A total of 378 civil works packages were procured through national competitive bidding
using the government’s e-procurement system. The large number of packages reflected the
nature of the sector development project, in which fund allocations to pourashavas occurred in
three phases based on performance against UGIAP indicators (para. 5). Procurement and
management of these contracts, however, presented significant challenges for both the PMU and
the PIUs. The PIUs undertook procurement of civil works, while LGED served as the approving
authority. This arrangement helped strengthen PIUs’ capacity in procurement and contract
management. Of the 22 goods packages, five were procured through international competitive
bidding and 17 through national competitive bidding or shopping. The overall performance of the
MDS, GICD, and individual consultants was deemed satisfactory. The project readiness
consultants carried out feasibility studies, due diligence, and preparation of bidding documents to
ensure project readiness at approval of IUGIP (footnote 21). Slum improvement committees,
contracted by the pourashavas, implemented the slum improvement works. This arrangement
allowed the priorities of vulnerable communities to be incorporated into project planning and
supported quality control during implementation. Annual and cumulative contract award
projections against actuals are in Appendix 6.

22.    Environmental safeguards. The project was classified as category B for environmental

clearance certificate was obtained in May 2016 from the Department of Environment to meet the
requirements of the Environmental Conservation Act 1995 and the Environmental Conservation
Rules 1997; the certificate was renewed annually.

23.    The project appointed an environmental focal person in the PMU and each of the 56 PIUs.

prepared and disclosed on ADB’s website. Lapses in safeguard due diligence for SWM and FSM
subprojects were addressed through corrective actions identified during review missions and in
semiannual environmental monitoring reports. A grievance redress mechanism was established.
The project planted 48,794 tree saplings at SWM and FSM sites as an environmental
enhancement measure. Training on environmental safeguards was provided to 4,286 trainees
from the PMU, pourashava staff, and contractors. Details of the environmental safeguards and
management plan are in Appendix 9.

24.     Social safeguards. The project aimed to avoid and minimize involuntary resettlement and
impacts on Indigenous Peoples within the project area. The project was classified as category B

ADB's Safeguard Policy Statement. A resettlement framework was prepared following
Bangladesh’s Acquisition and Requisition of Immovable Property Ordinance, 1982 and ADB’s

during implementation. Affected persons were consulted during the preparation and
implementation of the resettlement plan. Land acquisition was required only for solid waste and
fecal sludge treatment plants. A total of 60.01 hectares of private barren land was acquired from
249 titled households from 32 pourashavas. A summary is provided in Appendix 10. The
government funded all compensation related to land acquisition and resettlement. The
implementation of the resettlement plan was satisfactory. A total of 332 non-titled holders were
temporarily affected by market subprojects; they were provided with temporary sites during
construction to continue their businesses.

medical costs and loss of income associated with waterlogging. Details of the economic analysis,
including assumptions and methodologies, are provided in Appendix 12.

D.      Sustainability

37.     The project is rated likely sustainable. The project comprised both revenue-generating
and nonrevenue-generating subprojects. Financial re-evaluation was undertaken only for the
revenue-generating subprojects and followed ADB’s guidance note.35 Financial costs included
capital expenditure and O&M, while financial benefits included revenues received from users of
the revenue-generating subprojects. The weighted average cost of capital (WACC) was
calculated at 2.12%. The overall financial internal rate of return (FIRR) of the sample revenue-
generating subprojects was estimated at 2.7% (financial net present value [FNPV] of Tk28.01
million), higher than the WACC of 2.12%. The FIRR of the market development subprojects was
estimated at 5.6% (FNPV of Tk73.69 million), making it significantly higher than the WACC. The
FIRR of the sample water supply subprojects was estimated at 2.6% (FNPV of Tk8.3 million),
while the FIRR of the bus-truck terminal subproject was estimated at –2.47% (FNPV of –Tk46.2
million). The number of new subscribers of the piped-water supply systems had been
overestimated at appraisal, leading to an overestimation of potential revenue. The financial
performance of the sample bus-truck terminal subproject was not satisfactory because completion
took longer than expected.

38.     Analyses of incremental recurrent O&M costs and the repayment requirements for debt
financing, along with analyses of the current and projected government budgets, indicate that
pourashavas are likely to meet the financial requirements for sustaining the projects through a
combination of their own finances and government transfers. The pourashavas have well-
established systems for maintenance planning and budget allocation. The projects are technically,
environmentally, and socially sustainable and have no political or institutional risks. The analyses
also show that total O&M costs will constitute only a negligible share of the total revenue or
expenditure of the government. The financial sustainability assessment is in Appendix 13.

living conditions and service delivery in the pourashavas is substantial. The enhancement of the
quality of life of the pourashava residents because of improved roads and drains, solid waste
disposal, sanitation, and water supply facilities is expected to reduce migration to Dhaka and other
larger cities in Bangladesh. The project and its predecessors have clearly shown that allocating

I.         Overall Assessment

45.     Overall, the project is rated highly successful. The project was highly relevant at appraisal

infrastructure and pourashava governance, and in making pourashava decision-making and
planning an inclusive process. The project is rated efficient, as the recalculated EIRR of the

rated likely sustainable, considering pourashavas’ enhanced institutional capability, increased
volume of revenue (especially from the much-improved tax collection system and revenue-
generating ADB-funded subprojects), and the commitment from the government as well as
development partners.

                     III. ISSUES, LESSONS, AND RECOMMENDATIONS

A.      Issues and Lessons

46.    The project resulted in the following operational and strategic issues and lessons some of
those are already captured in a knowledge product published by ADB:38
       (i)     The consultative and participatory mechanisms of TLCCs and other committees
               significantly improved urban governance, citizens’ awareness, and public
               accountability.
       (ii)    Slum improvement packages implemented by women-led slum improvement
               committees proved effective in procurement, implementation, and O&M.
       (iii)   Performance-based fund allocation clearly incentivized pourashavas to improve
               governance, infrastructure quality, and service delivery standards.
       (iv)    Ease of making payments (i.e., a computerized tax collection and billing system)
               helps to enhance tax and fee collection.
       (v)     Water meters are gaining popularity among users. Strong citizen engagement can
               help popularize them in the pourashavas.
       (vi)    Lease money and rent collected from municipal markets can be a good revenue
               source for pourashavas. However, a functioning grievance redress mechanism is
               needed to ensure that existing shop owners are not adversely affected.
       (vii)   Implementing GAPs and PRAPs from pourashavas’ own revenues, rather than
               relying solely on project funds, ensures ownership and long-term sustainability.
       (viii) Mobile maintenance teams have been effective in repairing infrastructure, which
               increases asset longevity and reduces rehabilitation costs.

47.    Non-compliance with the advance fund and SOE guidelines delayed the project’s financial
closure. In addition, in some instances, the APFS did not reconcile with ADB disbursement
records, and 61 audit observations remain unresolved, primarily reflecting weak accounting
capacity as well as operational and compliance weaknesses (para. 28).

B.      Recommendations

48.     The following recommendations are proposed to sustain and scale the project results:

        (i)     Water quality should be regularly monitored at water treatment plants, hand tube
                wells, and household connections. In addition, treated water from sanitary landfills
                should be systematically tested before discharging (paras. 22, and 23)
        (ii)    The number of civil works packages should be reduced to make project
                management more effective and efficient (para. 20).
        (iii)   Pourashavas should gradually transition from the current computer-based financial
                management system to a fully web-based digital platform to enhance accessibility,
                transparency, and real-time monitoring (paras. 11).

49.     To strengthen financial management practices, the executing agency should prepare a

financial statement for audit. The executing agency should establish a systematic process to

tracked and addressed. Progress on these submissions should be regularly reviewed and
documented in the QPRs. Establishing a time-bound action plan and updating it regularly
throughout implementation will be beneficial.

recommended:
      (i)   The pourashavas should set up a robust monitoring mechanism for effective GAP

            facilities.39

may be carried out in 2026. An evaluation of the governance reforms and infrastructure
improvement could yield useful lessons on the impact of reforms and interventions.

                           GENDER EQUALITY RESULTS AND ACHIEVEMENTS

A.         Gender Issues

1.      The active representation and participation of women in municipal governance structures
and decision-making processes are essential for gender-responsive municipal service delivery.
Women ward councilors (WWCs) elected from reserved seats have limited capacity for active
participation in municipal decision-making. Moreover, men counterparts dominate over the
WWCs, and women members of different forums and municipalities generally do not pay sufficient
attention to establishing an environment where women’s voices can be heard. Municipal citizens,
particularly women and individuals from low-income communities (LIC), are often unaware of the
range of services that municipalities provide. They have limited awareness of health, hygiene,
solid waste management, and service billing, including taxes and water bills etc. Municipal
infrastructure development that does not consider and effectively address women’s needs further
constrains women’s mobility. Poor living conditions in slums increase women’s misery and
hardship, which include fetching water from a distance, nursing weak family members, addressing
poor health, and safety and security at night.1 This limits their opportunities in higher education
and employment, as well as their participation in consultations and capacity for active participation
in various forums. However, the project deemed that if the inclusiveness of poor and marginalized
groups in municipal decision-making is enhanced, women and people from LIC will actively
participate in the municipal governance structure and decision-making and development works
and have better access to service delivery. Municipalities can achieve sustainable, inclusive
development by benefiting women and people from LICs.

B.         Project Gender Features

2.      As the project was designed to directly contribute to women’s empowerment and gender
equality, with specific targets at both the output and outcome levels, it was categorized as gender
equality objective (GEN), and a gender action plan (GAP) was developed and implemented. The
GAP had 29 gender performance indicators, including 2 outcome gender performance indicators
and 4 output indicators of the design and monitoring framework (DMF) (Appendix 1).

3.       The key features of the GAP were (i) providing new or improved access to piped water
supply to 60,000 households; (ii) earmarking at least 1.0% municipal revenue budget of which
spent 50.0% for implementation of pourashava-specific GAPs in 23 pourashavas; (iii) ensuring
women’s participation (target: 30.0%) in all community consultation meetings related to
identification of location for local infrastructure; (iv) 29 allocating separate office space and toilets
for WWCs in pourashava buildings; (v) equipping 80.0% pourashava with bus terminals (if
possible, launch ghats (boat landing area) and railway station) with separate toilets for women
and as feasible, women- and child-friendly waiting rooms and booking counters; (vi) 100.0% of
pourashava that meet the intermediate criteria of UGIAP and implement public parks
improvement under the project having well-lit and safe sitting areas and separate toilets for
women; (vii) engaging women (target 20.0%) in construction work and inclusion of this provision
in bidding document; (viii) training and engaging women (target: 700) including members from
slum improvement committees (SICs) on operation and maintenance of community infrastructure;
(ix) inclusion of clauses on gender-relevant national core labor standards (CLS) in all bid
documents; (x) orienting all contractors on all CLS with focusing on gender-related clauses; (xi)

C.      GAP Implementation Arrangements

4.      The project designated one executive engineer as the project gender focal person (GFP)
and one gender and social development officer to oversee the implementation of the GAP and
ensure the regular submission of quarterly progress reports to the ADB Bangladesh Resident
Mission gender team. The project also engaged a consultant team for gender development and
poverty alleviation, consisting of three national consultants: one senior gender specialist, one
junior gender specialist, and one data analyst, on a continuous basis to ensure the GAP’s proper
implementation and results monitoring.

D.      GAP Achievements

5.      The project’s gender results were assessed as successful, with 100% of 29 gender
performance indicators achieved. The project benefits for women and girls from greater gender
equality initiatives through following:2

E.      Increased Economic Empowerment

6.    The project empowered women economically by equipping them with income-generating
(IGA) skills and engaging them in construction work. Through municipality-tailored GAP
implementation using earmarked funds, 16,741 women received IGA support via skills training

3,000 and 40,000, and many shared their skills with others, expanding income opportunities within
their communities. The project also generated 3.26 million construction workdays, with 27.0%
(882,648 days) completed by women. Women were primarily engaged in tailoring, boutiques,
handicrafts, poultry, grocery businesses, mobile repair, and street vending. Their earnings were
invested in children’s education, small businesses, home improvements (e.g., water and
sanitation), land, and gold jewelry. Evidence on empowerment and social outcomes was drawn
from field monitoring reports and qualitative assessments undertaken by the project. The
publication Our Talks: Success Stories of Self-Reliant Women in UGIIP-III under LGED (January
2022)3 documented 40 case stories from 35 project pourashavas, covering beneficiaries with
diverse geographic and livelihood backgrounds. In addition, 12 focus group discussions with
about 150 women—including IGA beneficiaries, women entrepreneurs, Slum Improvement
Committee chairs, and members of Women and Children Affairs Standing Committees—were
conducted during project review and project completion report missions to validate results.4

7.      The qualitative findings indicated that participation in income-generating activities
increased women’s confidence and involvement in household and community decisions,
particularly on education, property, and livelihood matters. Women entrepreneurs and committee
members also reported being consulted by pourashava officials on planning and budgeting
issues, demonstrating enhanced leadership and institutional participation.

8.     The project enabled women to save money by opening bank accounts in their own names
and gain control over their resources. The IGA supports recipient women under the municipal
GAP started regular earnings. Investing their income for self and family needs, 1,755 women
opened new bank accounts and started regular monthly savings with the remaining income. They
found the savings helpful while expanding their business, covering tuition fees, expenses for
higher studies, or any other family emergency, as the women themselves decide. Besides, 290
women SIC chairs opened new bank accounts jointly with the respective municipalities for CAP
implementation and gained control over community resources.

9.     The project supported women in becoming economically active and self-reliant through
entrepreneurship opportunities. Women with new skills and IGA support began running
businesses, including grocery shops, tailoring, boutiques, and handicrafts. A total of 332 women
entrepreneurs received loans through pourashava GAP funds or local microfinance linkages. Out
of them, 35 women employed 1,870 women in their business. During focus group discussions
conducted during project reviews and PCR missions (footnote 37), women beneficiaries and
community members shared that these women were recognized locally for their initiative and
were seen as motivating other women to pursue income-generating activities.

for women users in homes and public areas. Safe and separate toilets in slums, bus terminals,

and installed street and area lighting in project pourashavas. These improvements provided safer,
more accessible infrastructure for all residents, including women and girls, in slum and low-
income areas.

healthcare by strengthening municipal capacity and infrastructure. Previously, limited mobility and
low awareness meant women rarely sought care for their own health needs, especially sexual
and reproductive issues, which were often neglected. Through improved governance, awareness-
raising, and gender-responsive infrastructure, such as accessible roads, bus terminals, and
municipal buildings,5 more women and girls began visiting healthcare centers. Enhanced slum
infrastructure, including walkways and van access, further facilitated mobility. As municipal
capacity grew, some municipalities began offering primary healthcare services. As a result,

G.         Lessons and Recommendations

      •    Institutionalizing gender budgeting ensures ownership and sustainability.
           Implementing GAP and PRAP activities through pourashavas’ own revenue strengthened
           local accountability and reduced reliance on project funds. Future urban programs should
           formalize gender budgeting as part of regular municipal performance assessment.
      •    Integrating gender monitoring into existing systems improves data reliability.
           Manual reporting constrained consistency and verification. Incorporating sex-
           disaggregated GAP indicators into LGED’s digital MIS would institutionalize data collection
           and support evidence-based planning and learning.
      •    Continuity of gender outcomes depends on structured capacity building.
           Frequent turnover of elected representatives and municipal staff disrupted
           implementation. Establishing regular induction and refresher training on gender-
           responsive planning and budgeting can sustain institutional capacity and compliance.
      •    Cross-functional        coordination        enhances        implementation        quality.
           Gender results were strongest when technical, engineering, and finance divisions jointly
           monitored GAP progress. Mainstreaming gender responsibilities across departments
           promotes shared accountability for results.
      •    Women-led community mechanisms are effective models for inclusive urban
           development.
           Slum Improvement Committees chaired by women demonstrated transparency and
           responsiveness to community needs. This participatory approach can be replicated in
           other municipal service areas to ensure inclusive and locally owned outcomes.

                                     ENVIRONMENTAL SAFEGUARD

1.       Environment Categorization. The project is classified as category B for environmental
safeguards because adverse environmental impacts from construction of urban roads, drains,
street lighting, riverbank and ghat, community center, auditorium, bus terminals, improvement of
slums, markets, public toilets, water supply, and solid waste management are expected to be low
scale, localized and mitigation measures are readily available. An environmental assessment and
review framework (EARF) was prepared and disclosed in May 20141 for the project to provide
guidance in screening, categorizing, and preparing subprojects, and for impact mitigation planning
in the event of unanticipated environmental impacts arise during implementation. Five draft initial
environmental examinations (IEEs) with environmental management plans (EMPs) were
prepared and disclosed during project preparation in May 2014 for representative infrastructure
investments including water supply, solid waste management, markets, roads, and drainage in
accordance with ADB’s Safeguard Policy Statement (2009) and government laws. The
requirements from the Government of Bangladesh are outlined in the Environmental Conservation
Act 1995, and Environmental Conservation Rules 1997, which categorize the subprojects under
the project as Green, Orange A, Orange B, and Red. The Department of Environment (DOE)
issued an Environmental Clearance Certificate (ECC) applicable for the development of the
infrastructure up to Orange B in May 2016 and renewed annually. EMPs were included in the bid
document with an adequate budget for environmental management for water supply improvement
and municipal facility subprojects.

2.      Environmental Management Plan. The Local Government Engineering Department
(LGED) and the Department of Public Health Engineering (DPHE) both having extensive
experience in managing urban infrastructure subprojects financed by ADB, were the executing
agencies based in Dhaka and PIU based at the pourashava level were responsible for
implementing the EMPs. The project mobilized a designated environmental focal person in PMU
and each of the PIUs, and an environmental safeguards team consisting of one senior
environmental specialist and two regional environmental specialists was recruited under the
Management, Design, and Supervision Consultants (MDSC) for implementation, monitoring, and
reporting. However, during the construction stage, the contractors were responsible to mitigate
all environmental impacts related with the construction activities. In this context, a total of 284
IEEs and ESRs including environmental management plans were prepared and that were part of
the contract documents. ADB has given approval of 231 IEEs and ESRs that includes 131 IEEs
for urban road and drains, 54 IEEs for municipal facility and water supply and 40 ESRs for slum
improvement packages. The EMP adequately covers the (i) EMP implementation training; (ii)
management of construction work camps, hot mix plants, stockpile areas, storage areas, and
disposal areas; (iii) management of construction materials, spoils, and fuel at construction sites;
(iv) control of dust, noise, water and soil contamination; (v) protection of biodiversity; (vi)
protection of worker health and safety; (vii) management of traffic and access obstruction; (viii)
avoiding damage to community facilities; and (ix) post-construction clean-up etc. Of the 27 solid
waste and sanitation management packages, conditional clearances were provided to 6 FSTP
IEEs and 13 SWM (landfilling) packages and advised to resubmit the revised IEE incorporating
ADB’s comments for the remining 21 IEEs. PMU later submitted the revised IEEs addressing
ADB’s approval.

3.     Environment safeguard measures and environmental monitoring and reporting
during construction. PMU submitted a total of 14 environment monitoring report (EMRs) which
were disclosed on ADB’s website. However, there were lapses with safeguard implementation for

                                       ECONOMIC REEVALUATION

A.       General

1.      For economic re-evaluation, the PCR has analyzed samples of two categories of
subprojects, which represent 76% of the total investment under the project for civil work
component: (i) the upgradation of 10 roads; and ii) the upgradation/construction of seven drainage
systems. These projects are in 4 districts spread across the country. The primary assumption was
that with ADB finance (i) Improved pourashava roads would lead to lower vehicle operating costs
(VOC) and savings in terms of reduced travel time costs; and (ii) by reducing waterlogging,
improved/constructed drainage systems would save restorations costs of damaged properties
and roads, save on healthcare expenditures and lessen income loss associated with
waterlogging.

B.       Approach and Methodology

       1. Assumptions for Estimating EIRRs

2.     The economic analyses were conducted in accordance with ADB’s Guidelines for the
Economic Analysis of Projects, 20171. All prices and costs are expressed in 2022-23 terms. Costs
and benefits are expressed in domestic price numeraire. Import duties, taxes and interest during
the construction period have been excluded. The economic analysis at project completion is
based on data collected in a survey conducted during the June and July period of 2023, and from
the PMU and the pourashavas. The conducted survey covered 10 pourashava roads and 7
drainage subprojects.

3.     Subprojects from the similar towns from the original financing and additional financing
have been considered for economic reevaluation except for the Faridpur water supply package
that was considered for economic analysis during processing of the original loan but later has not
been implemented under the project.

4.      The project analysis period is 20 years after the completion of most of the projects. A
social discount rate of 9.0% has been assumed.

5.       A shadow exchange rate factor (SERF) of 1.045 has been assumed.

6.     It has been assumed that unskilled laborers are not always employed. To calculate the
opportunity cost of unskilled labor, a shadow wage rate factor (SWRF) of 0.8 has been used2.
However, in the case of skilled labor, it is assumed that their shadow wage rate doesn’t differ from
their market wage rate, hence no adjustment is necessary.

7.     Expected life of the newly built or upgraded portion of the assets is assumed to be 20
years. The residual value of the project is assumed to be 25% of the capital cost.

8.     Capital costs involve all infrastructure development costs of the project under
consideration excluding import duties, taxes and interest during the construction period (IDC).
The O&M i.e. maintenance expenses are estimated based on consultation with the experts.

  Analysis of Water Supply Projects. Manila
2 https://www.adb.org/sites/default/files/linked-documents/40540-016-efa.pdf

9.      The economic analysis presented here considered only direct and measurable benefits
associated with the road and drainage subprojects. Field-level evidence indicates that the
upgradation of earthen roads to bituminous roads and improved drainage systems induced
enhancement of trade and economic activities and land price along the upgraded roads. In
addition, non-quantifiable indirect benefits such as improved quality of life in many slums, the
benefits of women empowerment, health benefits from improved solid waste and faecal waste
management, etc. have not been accounted for in the process of EIRR estimations.

10.     Benefits for both the road and drain packages have been considered “without project” and
“with project” scenario.

11.    For calculating the EIRRs of both the road and drainage subprojects, only investment
costs for those subprojects have been considered. Project’s financing for governance
improvement and capacity development under Output 2 have not been considered since this
investment will be overarching in nature and benefits of this investment will go beyond only the
selected subprojects.

       2. Road Upgradation

12.     The main sources of benefits from the road component of the project can be summarized
as follows: (i) decreased Vehicle Operating Costs (VOCs) resulting from the improved quality of
road surfaces; ii) time saved during travel (Travel Time Savings or TTS) due to higher average
speeds made possible by improved roads; and iii) the residual value of the infrastructure created.
In the absence of the project, people were using these roads, albeit in smaller numbers. Before
upgradation, the users were incurring relatively higher VOCs. The savings in VOCs will benefit
both the pre-existing road users and the additional traffic generated due to improvement of the
roads. The generated traffic consists of increased traffic and traffic generated as a result of shift
in the modal mix from non-motorized to motorized vehicles in many cases. Moreover, traffic
volume will continue to increase over time due to overall economic growth and increased
economic activity.

13.     The EIRR estimation process used data from reports produced by the Local Government
of Engineering Department (LGED) and the Roads and Highways Department (RHD) 3 . The
reports estimated economic values of Vehicle Operating Costs (VOCs) in terms of taka per
kilometer for various vehicle types, encompassing both motorized and non-motorized vehicles,
as measured by the International Roughness Index (IRI). In addition, the same data sources were
utilized to determine the economic values of Travel Time Costs (TTCs) per vehicle hour,
categorized by vehicle type. Note that during the construction phase, both VOC and TTC
experience an increase due to road disruption. It is assumed that both VOC and TTC costs would
escalate by 25% during the construction period. In calculating VOC and TTC both normal and
generated traffic were considered. The IRI expressed in meters per kilometer, was employed as
an indicator of road roughness. For each upgraded road, both pre- and post-development values
of IRI were assigned based on their initial and post-development conditions. All figures were
adjusted for the 2022-2023 price level using GDP deflator. Expected life of the newly
built/upgraded portion of the assets is assumed to be 20 years. The residual value of a subproject
is assumed to be 25% of the capital cost.



3 LGED. 2018. Road User Cost Study for LGED Roads Final Report Phase-1 & 2. Dhaka

14.      Average annual daily traffic (AADT) has been estimated based on a post-development
field survey conducted as a part of this exercise during June-July period of 2023. The survey was
conducted to collect information on traffic volume on weekdays and weekends days by types of
traffic. Observed traffic has been categorized into 9 categories of motorized traffic and 3
categories of non-motorized traffic. Pedestrian traffic has been ignored. Traffic counting started
at 7:00 am and continued up to 9:00 pm, i.e., for 14 hours each day. The 14-hour traffic data have
been converted into 24-hour traffic volume using a multiplier of 1.20. As the survey was conducted
during the rainy season, the volume of weekly traffic was further adjusted by multiplying weekly
traffic volume by 1.1 to take seasonal variation into account. The volume of traffic was then
multiplied by 52.0 to arrive at annual traffic flows. As no baseline data exists, baseline traffic
volume has been estimated based on relevant responses received during the survey. Traffic
growth per year has been conservatively assumed at 6.0% a year but has only been considered
up to 2023 as there is little scope for continuous traffic growth without causing gridlock as the
roads are located in the urban area. Total cost of upgradation of a subproject road includes capital
cost and regular maintenance The estimated costs of regular maintenance per kilometer is based
on pourashava responses.

      3. Drain Upgradation

15.      The exercise closely followed the methodology used in the economic analysis at
appraisals. The economic benefits of drainage upgradation subprojects primarily stem from: (i)
reduced damages to properties from waterlogging, (ii) healthcare costs savings (iii) avoided
income loss due to waterlogging; and iv) reduced road maintenance cost. Waterlogging frequently
causes damage to properties located in the affected areas. The owners of the properties need to
clean up and undertake expenditures to restore the properties to their original condition. Improved
drains would lead to reduction of such expenditures. Incidences of water borne diseases such as
diarrhea, amoebic dysentery, typhoid, and intestinal worms sharply rise during the waterlogged
period. The reduction of such incidences would result in lower health care expenditure. It has
been assumed that the population of the area will keep Increasing at a conservative rate of 2.0%
per annum. Hence, with time, the avoided medical expenditure would keep increasing at 2% per
annum. Water logging also causes lower income due to absence from work, slower economic
activities in the affected areas, etc. Improved drainage systems are expected to ameliorate such
problems. The avoided loss of income is expected to rise with time as population density of the
area is expected to rise by 2% per annum and the growth of GDP per annum (5% GDP growth
rate was assumed). As expected, the survey found sharp decline in income loss due to flooding
after the completion of the drainage projects. The roads in a waterlogged areas also suffer
damages. The authority needs to bear additional expenditures to restore the roads to their original
state. Solving the problem of waterlogging would entail reduction in such expenses. The
maintenance cost per year is assumed to be 2% of the total construction costs. The life of a
subproject is assumed to be 20 years after the completion of the construction period. The residual
value of the project is assumed to be 25% of the capital cost. The costs and benefits of the
constructed/upgraded drainage subprojects are then analyzed to estimate the EIRR.

A.       Introduction

1.     The financial analyses of two water supply, two market development and one bus-truck
terminal revenue generating subprojects financed with UGIIP 3 facility were carried out in
accordance with the Financial Management and Analysis of Projects (Asian Development Bank,
2005). Financial and other required information were collected from the PMU office at LGED,
pourashavas and other relevant sources.

B.       Assumptions for Financial Internal Rate of Return Estimation

2.       The financial analysis was conducted under the following assumptions:

         (i)   For the purpose of the financial analysis, all prices and costs are expressed in 2022‒
               2023 terms. The project analysis period is 20 years once the last of the relevant
               subprojects have been implemented. However, for each individual category of the
               subprojects, the analysis period is 20 years once the last of the subprojects in that
               category have been implemented.

         (ii) The residual value has been assumed to be 25% of the original value of the capital
              expenditure for each of the sample subprojects.

         (iii) The cost stream reflects the costs incurred in delivering the estimated benefits. The
               cost stream used to determine the FIRR include capital cost, fees, taxes and duties,
               operations and maintenance (O&M) expenditure etc. The capital cost is based on the
               actual expenditures incurred for the project.

         (iv) Financial benefit stream of the project is primarily due to the payments made by the
              piped-water consumers from their volumetric water consumption. No financial stream
              for the nonrevenue water. In the case of market development subprojects, the
              revenue stream is primarily comprised of monthly rent and one-time lumpsum
              payments, locally known as selami, in the beginning. As for the bus-truck terminal,
              the pourashava collects the user fees, and parking fees from the bus operators and
              remain responsible for the operation and maintenance of the terminal. The
              poursahavas enjoy income tax exemption status, hence no tax would be deducted
              from their revenue income.

considered as loan, was lent at the nominal rate of 6.0%. The rest of the funding (60%) was
treated as equity. The government’s 20-years bond yield rate has been considered as the cost of
the equity. The WACC has been calculated at 2.12% for the subprojects as shown in Table A13.1.

12.     The financial analysis of the sample subprojects indicates that the estimated aggregate
FIRR is only 2.7% which is higher than the calculated WACC of 2.12%. Regarding water supply

revenue. Note that many revenue-generating subprojects, primarily in the bus-truck terminal and
piped-water supply category, envisaged at appraisal were never implemented.

H.       Sustainability Analysis

         1. Introduction

13.     The financial sustainability of both revenue and nonrevenue projects is contingent on the
financial capacity of the agency involved, prospects for the demand for services or products, cost
recovery mechanisms, the availability of resources for O&M of the project plus the resources
required to pay for debt financing installments. The pourashavas have a well-established system
of identifying the maintenance needs of the urban facilities whether revenue generating or not.
Pourashavs earmark a portion of their annual budget for O&M of the infrastructure facilities under
82      Appendix 13



their jurisdiction. The routine and periodic maintenance of the facilities are undertaken as
required.

14.      However, such O&M activities are not supported by the pourashavas resources alone.
Even if a pourashava has sufficient resources to finance the required O&M facility, there exists
strong incentives not to do so. First, there are competing demands for the same pool of resources
for activities such as demand for pourashavas’ infrastructural development. As pourashavas are
elected bodies, they have to respond to competing demands as well and are subject to political
influence. Second, there are other available resources with which they can meet the resource
demand of O&M. For example, LGED also undertakes the O&M of the pourashava infrastructures
using their own resources which makes the pourashavas own fund not the only source of funding
for the maintenance of the pourashava infrastructure. Another source of O&M funding is the
development partners such as ADB and WB through infrastructure upgradation projects. Through
such projects, not only the maintenance needs are covered, but the quality of the funded
infrastructures also improves.

15.      UGIIP 3 funded both revenue- and non-revenue generating projects in the pourashavas.
The revenue-generating projects are expected to generate sufficient revenues from their users to
minimize the probability of having inadequate financial resources for their O&M. On the other
hand, nonrevenue projects solely rely on pourashava revenues and government allocations for
their O&M costs which enhances the possibility of being inadequately maintained which in turn
may lead to failure in ensuring the quality of services provided. Inclusion of revenue earning
subprojects such as market development, piped-water supply, and bus-truck terminal subprojects
under UGIIP 3, enhanced holding tax collection efficiency, and an increased focus on the
installation of prepaid water-meters along with regular tariff adjustment is expected to enhance
the financial resources available to the pourashavas to undertake O&M expenditure on their own.
Note that the revenue-generating subprojects under UGIIP 3 are expected to generate substantial
amount of financial resources which would not only cover their own O&M expenditures but would
leave substantial amount of surplus that can be used to finance the O&M needs of the non-
revenue earning subprojects undertaken under UGIIP 3.2 The combined O&M cost of the non-
revenue subprojects in the pourasahvas under the program has been estimated at Tk. 513.9
million per year. The surplus generated from the revenue- generating subprojects after meeting
their O&M requirements is unlikely to meet all the O&M requirements of the nonrevenue projects
and payments for debt financing.

17.     Although a limited number of revenue and non-revenue generating projects are not
operational yet, but most of the financed projects are already operational. The already operational
projects are currently being maintained and operated by the pourashavas from their own
resources at present assuming that during the initial years after the completion of the project, not
much maintenance is required. As Table A13.10 illustrates, two of the selected pourashavas could
operate and maintain their UGIIP 3 funded revenue projects using revenues generated by those
same projects and have been enjoying operational surplus from operating those subprojects.
However, the other pourashava, i.e. Magura (see Table A13.11), may not be able to maintain the
funded revenue-generating projects with the generated revenue from the operation of those
projects. These types of pourashavas likely require monetary transfers from the central
government to financially sustain even the revenue-generating projects.

18.     The operational surplus/deficit from the revenue projects is expected to rise in the future
due to inflation and increased demand (Table A13.10 and Table A13.11). Again, the yet to be
operational revenue projects are likely to generate additional revenue and require additional O&M
expenditures as well. However, the pourashavas are unlikely to muster sufficient resources both
for servicing their debt funded by UGIIP 3 and O&M requirements of the revenue and nonrevenue
projects without government assistance.

19.     On debt servicing, the pourashavas are required to repay a debt of 40% of the total
expenditure on revenue-generating projects over the period of 20 years including a grace period
of five years. Note that the amount of debt servicing installments will vary from year to year as
loans were distributed over the years. The pourashavas are unlikely to muster sufficient resources
both for servicing their aforementioned debt and the O&M requirements of nonrevenue projects
without government assistance. The resources required to finance debt servicing will be less than
Tk424.2 million in any given year. The O&M costs of the nonrevenue subprojects financed under
UGIIP 3 has been estimated at Tk513.9 million per year which would be rising with future inflation.
This plus the maximum amount payable for debt servicing each year, estimated at Tk424.2
million, indicates that the resources required for these purposes are likely beyond the financial

to transfer resources to help enable the pourashavas to bridge the financing gap.

expenditure, yearly deficit is forecasted to shoot up from Tk-2013.08 billion to Tk-20,377.11 billion

development expenditures and its expected associated growth, the government would have
enhanced capability to meet the required financial obligations. Nonetheless, the government has

financial management system. The recent success of the government in achieving

I.     Conclusion

22.      The above analysis clearly shows that even if the pourashavas borrow the total amount
required for the O&M costs of the nonrevenue projects financed under UGIIP plus the debt
repayment installments borrowed for the revenue projects, the required amount will form a
negligible amount compared to the total revenue or expenditure of the government. The total
income of the government was Tk3,921.9 billion in 2021-22and is projected to rise to Tk4,977.8
billion by FY2025. The maximum amount that would be needed by the pourashavas would be
less than a billion taka in a year which would constitute about 0.02% of the revenue receipt of the
government in FY2025 and about 0.01% of the total expenditure of the central government in
FY2025. In terms of share of the central government’s sharply rising income and expenditure, the
pourashavas’ borrowing requirements would steadily decline in the future. On the other hand, the
fiscal impact analysis of the pourashavas implies that the pourashavas would require financial
assistance from the government for enhanced O&M expenditures and for debt servicing
purposes.
