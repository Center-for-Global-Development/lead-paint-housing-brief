 I. COUNTRY CONTEXT AND OPERATION SUMMARY


1.       Belize has the smallest population in Central America, at an estimated 430,191 people as of 2021. As a small
economy primarily reliant on tourism, the export of agricultural products, and an energy sector dependent on oil imports,
Belize is highly vulnerable to fluctuations in international commodity prices and external economic shocks. Between 1999
and 2008, real gross domestic product (GDP) growth averaged 5.6 percent, supported by expansionary monetary and
fiscal policies, before falling to 1.8 percent between 2009 and 2019, which, combined with a similar rate of population
growth, resulted in near stagnation of GDP per capita over the last decade. As a result of the country’s underperforming
economy, the poverty rate increased from 34 percent in 2002 to 52 percent in 2019, with 9 percent of the population
living in extreme poverty, a trend that contrasts sharply with poverty reductions in other parts of Latin America. Its
economy was deeply affected by the coronavirus disease 2019 (COVID-19) pandemic and has been recovering following
the reopening of the country to tourists.
2.      Belize’s disaster and climate risk profile underscores the urgent need for a coordinated approach to disaster
risk management (DRM) and climate adaptation across sectors. Ranked third in disaster risk and fifth in climate change
risk among small island developing states (SIDS), Belize faces increasing threats from hurricanes, floods, and coastal
erosion, exacerbated by rising sea levels. Since 1960, the country has seen an increase in mean annual temperatures of
approximately 0.45°C, along with a higher frequency of hot days and nights.1 Belize’s coastal areas, including its critical
coral reefs and marine ecosystems, are particularly threatened by rising sea levels, which have been increasing by around
3–5 mm per year. Approximately 57.9 percent and 36.2 percent of the population of Belize reside within 25 km of the
coast and in low elevation zones, respectively, and an additional 25 percent of the population resides on riverbanks,
exposing them to increased inland flooding. These vulnerabilities are compounded by unsustainable land development
and settlement practices.
3.      Belize has faced a diverse range of disasters over the past 15 years, significantly affecting its economy and
underscoring the need for improved fiscal and institutional resilience. These include high-frequency events such as
hurricanes, tropical storms, and floods and less frequent ones such as earthquakes, droughts, landslides, and public health
emergencies (i.e., epidemics and pandemics). According to the 2022 Climate Risk Index, Belize ranked the second most
affected country by extreme weather events in 2022, due to its exceptionally high relative economic losses, which
accounted for 3.53 percent of GDP with over 4.2 percent of its population affected. 2 It is a significant rise from its rank of
19 as of the 1993–2022 Climate Risk Index. In recent years, Belize experienced a prolonged drought in 2019; the COVID-
19 pandemic, which contracted GDP by 13.4 percent in 2020; Hurricane Nana in 2020; and Hurricane Lisa in 2022. Climate
and disaster risks pose a significant threat to Belize’s economy and public finances, estimated at US$27.2 million, which
with a one percent chance could exceed US$672 million (22 percent of 2023 nominal GDP), as highlighted by the average
annual loss from tropical cyclones, based on the Caribbean Catastrophe Risk Insurance Facility (CCRIF) Segregated Portfolio
Company model. 3

4.      The DPR Act (2000) 4 serves as the primary legal framework for emergency management, hazard mitigation, and
disaster preparedness, and has established the National Emergency Management Organization (NEMO) as the lead
agency. Despite NEMO’s legal mandate for disaster management, its effectiveness is constrained by legislative limitations.
In particular, the DPR Act has never implemented requirements for a National Disaster Preparedness Response Plan
(NDPRP), which has created gaps in the definition of operational arrangements across all phases of disaster management,
including planning and training, and the roles and responsibilities of various line departments, ministries, stakeholders
and institutions. To strengthen fund mobilization and response, Belize enacted the Finance and Audit (Reform) Act (2005)
to allow the Ministry of Finance (MoF) to issue special warrants for emergency allocations. However, these are largely
reactive rather than proactive, often resulting in budget reallocations from development programs.
5.       The proposed Belize Development Policy Credit (DPC) with Catastrophe-Deferred Drawdown Option (Cat-DDO)
aims to enhance the institutional, technical, and financial capacity of the Government of Belize (GoB) to manage risks
resulting from the occurrence of natural hazards, including the adverse effects of climate change. The operation aims to
strengthen policy and legislative reforms to improve climate and disaster resilience in construction, planning,
preparedness, and response activities. Policy reforms are organized under two pillars: Pillar 1: Strengthening National
Resilience and Disaster Preparedness and Response Capacities, focuses on strengthening Belize’s national resilience and
preparedness and response systems through the NDPRP (PA1), the National Land Use Policy (PA2), and regulations
consisting of a National Building Code (PA3). Pillar 2: Strengthening Financial and Fiscal Resilience Against Natural Hazards
and Climate Change, equips Belize with the tools and buffers needed to respond quickly to hazards, through reforms
enhancing financial preparedness and resilience [Disaster Risk Financing (DRF) Policy – (PA4)], fiscal discipline and stability
[Medium-Term Fiscal Framework (MTFF) – (PA5)], and financial protection and recovery [(Microinsurance regulations –
(PA6)]. The operation is proposed as an IDA credit in the amount of US$20 million under the Small States Exception Policy,
with its first allocation in FY26.


    II. MACROECONOMIC POLICY FRAMEWORK
6.        Belize’s macroeconomic policy framework is deemed adequate for Development Policy Financing. The country
has demonstrated robust economic growth, driven by key sectors such as tourism, agriculture, and construction, and has
made significant strides in fiscal management, reducing public debt through strategic measures. The monetary policy,
anchored by a stable exchange rate pegged to the US dollar, supports economic stability, while the financial sector shows
improvements despite existing vulnerabilities. The Government’s focus on addressing structural challenges, maintaining
fiscal discipline, and building financial reserves underpins the framework’s sustainability. The Central Bank has taken steps
to enhance financial integrity in the international financial services and banking sectors. The implementation of sound
fiscal policies and structural reforms is expected to further strengthen the currency peg and support economic stability.
Investments anticipated in the near term are expected to help address export difficulties arising from uncertainties in
global trade policies, contributing to economic resilience. However, Belize remains vulnerable to external shocks, including
global commodity price fluctuations and climate-related disruptions, necessitating continued vigilance and strategic policy
implementation to ensure long-term economic stability and growth.

    A. Recent Economic Developments
7.       In 2024, Belize’s economy was estimated to have grown by 8.2 percent, significantly surpassing the historical
trend. This growth is primarily driven by significant expansions in tourism, agriculture, construction, retail and wholesale
trade, transportation and business process outsourcing. The tourism sector has seen a robust recovery with overnight

tourist arrivals exceeding pre-pandemic levels by more than 10 percent. Despite these positive developments, Belize faces
structural challenges that could impede sustained economic growth. These include limited credit availability for the private
sector, inadequate infrastructure, skill shortages, and high crime rates. On the external front, Belize’s current account
balance improved notably, narrowing from 5.3 percent of GDP in 2022 to 1.5 percent in 2024. This improvement was
driven by a rise in the services balance due to lower shipping costs and higher tourism receipts, as well as a reduction in
the primary income deficit. The current account deficit was financed primarily through official borrowing, foreign direct
investment, and remittances. Table 1 reports selected economic and financial indicators.
8.      Belize’s monetary policy has been focused on supporting overall economic stability and growth. The Central
Bank of Belize (CBB) has maintained an adequate level of international reserves to anchor the currency peg, with gross
international reserves amounting to 3.4 months of imports at the end of 2024. Average inflation slowed from 4.4 percent
in 2023 to 3.4 percent in 2024, driven by declines in global commodity prices.
9.       The financial sector of Belize in 2024 showed some improvement but vulnerabilities remained. The CBB’s
vigilance and the authorities’ efforts to strengthen the anti-money laundering/combating the financing of terrorism
framework were positive developments. Domestic banks maintained strong regulatory capital, with a capital adequacy
ratio at 12 percent in September 2024, significantly above the legal requirement of nine percent. Non-performing loans
stood at about five percent of total loans. However, challenges persist, including pressures in specific loan portfolios,
increased corporate insolvencies, and tight liquidity in some banks, which continue to constrain real private sector credit
growth.
10.      In recent years, Belize attained and maintained a primary surplus, supported by improved expenditure control.
The overall fiscal deficit is estimated to remain stable at 1.4 percent of GDP in FY24/25, while the primary surplus is
estimated to increase from 0.7 to 0.8 percent of GDP. Revenues are estimated to decline from 22.9 percent of GDP in
FY23/24 to 22.5 percent in FY24/25, while total expenditure is estimated to decline from 24.4 to 24.2 percent of GDP.
Belize’s fiscal stability framework is underpinned by efforts to build financial reserves to support a countercyclical fiscal
approach and enhance fiscal discipline. These efforts remained largely unaffected in the lead-up to the snap election held
on March 12, 2024. However, fiscal risks remain, including the potential for higher global food and fuel prices and climate-
related disasters. Table 2 reports key fiscal indicators.
11.      Recent developments in public and external debt indicate a stabilization trend in Belize’s debt stock, with public
debt expected to stabilize at a moderate level by 2029. Public debt decreased from 103.3 percent of GDP in 2020 to 66.9
percent in 2023, with domestic debt at 24.6 percent and external debt at 42.3 percent. Public debt is estimated to have
declined further to 62.9 percent of GDP by the end of FY24/25. Debt service obligations remain manageable, with principal
payments averaging around 6.5 percent of GDP. Recent debt management efforts have focused on maintaining a favorable
debt composition and ensuring sustainable debt service obligations. External debt rollover risks are mitigated by a
significant portion of public debt owed to official creditors, which carries less rollover risk. Table 3 provides information
on public and publicly guaranteed debt stock and debt service.

  B. Macroeconomic Outlook and Debt Sustainability
12.      Economic growth is expected to slow to about 2.6 percent in 2025/2026, as the post-COVID-19 recovery
completes and the output gap closes. The Government is focused on addressing structural challenges such as limited
credit availability, inadequate infrastructure, skill shortages, and high crime rates to sustain economic growth. Key policy
priorities include improving trade integration, investing in skills and education, and tackling energy constraints and costs.
On the external front, the current account deficit is expected to remain relatively stable. The balance of payments is on a
sustainable path under current policies, supported by the CBB’s focus on maintaining adequate international reserves.
While uncertainty related to global trade policies is significant, investments projected for 2025 are expected to help
mitigate export challenges stemming from these uncertainties. Initially, this may lead to a rise in imports of capital goods
while export growth may face challenges, but recovery is expected as these investments take effect. Meanwhile,
investment growth is projected to stabilize as the economy matures. However, structural issues such as reliance on
tourism and energy imports and vulnerability to global commodity price fluctuations and climate-related disruptions pose
risks to external stability. The current account deficit is financed through private inflows and foreign aid, both of which
are expected to remain stable. Table 4 reports external financing needs and sources.

13.      Belize’s monetary and exchange rate policies are expected to continue focusing on maintaining price stability
and fostering economic growth. The authorities plan to strengthen the currency peg by increasing international reserves,
reducing public debt, implementing structural reforms, and limiting government financing by the Central Bank. These
measures are anticipated to reduce excess liquidity and help develop the domestic capital market, thereby enhancing the
credibility and sustainability of monetary policy. Inflation is expected to decline to 1.3 percent over the medium term, in
line with the anticipated fall in global commodity prices. The financial sector outlook remains stable, but the authorities
are vigilant about potential risks, including elevated global interest rates and climate-related disasters.

14.      Belize’s fiscal stance is expected to remain robust. The primary surplus is expected to remain stable in the
medium term. Public debt is projected to decline but will remain well above 50 percent of GDP due to slower nominal
GDP growth and high global interest rates. The Government is planning to introduce fiscal rules with escape clauses related
to natural disasters and has created a contingency fund that will help finance responses to emergencies, complementing
the proposed operation and other risk transfer instruments such as CCRIF. Key fiscal risks include higher global food and
fuel prices and climate-related disasters. The Government aims to address these risks by maintaining primary surpluses
supported by stable revenues and expenditures, keeping public debt on a downward trajectory, and improving the
arrangements for disaster risk financing (DRF).
15.      Belize’s public debt is assessed as sustainable with moderate risk of sovereign stress. It remained broadly stable
over the last two years, as the settlement with a foreign investor and higher interest rates on some external loans mostly
offset the effects of a primary surplus and strong nominal GDP growth. Public debt is projected to fall to 58 percent of
GDP by 2029. Gross financing needs are projected to average 7.7 percent of GDP over the medium term, supported by
primary fiscal surpluses, a favorable repayment profile for the blue loan, and manageable short-term debt rollovers by
domestic creditors. Medium- and long-term risks are contained, with debt firmly on a downward path and manageable
gross financing needs.
16.      Belize faces several macroeconomic risks, including its heavy reliance on tourism and energy imports, which
make it vulnerable to external shocks such as global recessions and fluctuations in energy prices. Global economic
uncertainty, including trade policy uncertainty, the risk of economic slowdown in the countries that are important for
Belize’s tourism sector, and volatility of global food and fuel prices, poses additional risks to inflation and growth. The
country’s susceptibility to climate-related disruptions such as flooding, wind damage, and coastal erosion further
exacerbates these vulnerabilities, with annual losses from natural disasters averaging four percent of GDP, according to
the Government’s estimates. The Government intends to mitigate these risks by implementing policies to promote
sustainable and inclusive economic growth, maintaining fiscal discipline, improving DRM and financing, and leveraging
relationships with international partners for external support.

disaster risks. It prioritizes reforms that support the development of preparedness and response systems to manage multi-

practices, establishing procedures for climate-resilient spatial and sustainable land use planning, management and

 PA3: To strengthen the resilience of newly constructed buildings, the Recipient, through its Minister responsible for
 housing, has issued regulations consisting of a National Building Code that integrates climate risk reduction measures
 in building regulations and standards. 17
38.     Rationale. The Belize Building Act was first enacted in 2003 (amended in 2020) to create a standardized approach
to building regulations and establish institutional structures, including the Central Building Authority (CBA) and local
building units, to oversee permissions and compliances. While the Building Act stipulates that any person constructing a
building must submit a permit, it only indicates that such permit may include provisions for a National Building Code. In
the absence of a National Building Code for Belize, various building codes are used based on the origins of trained
engineers, including those from the United Kingdom, Latin America, and the United Staes of America. This has led to the
construction of buildings based on codes that are not contextually comprehensive to Belize’s climate risks and has
complicated building safety inspections of the CBA. Issuing regulations consisting of a National Building Code is essential
for addressing these gaps and enhancing the country’s resilience to climate risks. PA3 aligns with global commitments to
address climate change and promote resilience, as outlined in international agreements such as the Paris Accord.
39.      Substance. The issuance of regulations consisting of a National Building Code provides a comprehensive legal
framework for integrating and administering building regulations and standards across all aspects of construction. The
National Building Code is based on the International Building Code of the International Code Council, which was adapted
to the local conditions of Belize. It establishes minimum requirements for reasonable levels of safety, health, and general
welfare through structural strength, means of egress, stability, sanitation, light and ventilation, energy conservation, and
provisions for life safety and protection from hazards. It further mandates the use of multi-hazard risk information in
building design and construction, ensuring that structures are resilient to various climate-related threats and discourages
construction of new buildings in hazard-prone areas. These legally binding regulations also address the needs of critical
infrastructure such as hospitals. The new code is intended to meet or exceed federal accessibility requirements found in
the Americans with Disabilities Act and the Fair Housing Act, ensuring all spaces, temporary or permanent, are accessible
to individuals with disabilities. The National Building Code streamlines the building approval and permit system, making it
more efficient and accessible, thereby encouraging compliance. Finally, the code promotes stakeholder engagement and
public participation in the building regulation process, ensuring that the needs and perspectives of local communities are
considered. Strengthening the roles and responsibilities of government agencies and stakeholders in enforcing building
regulations is a key component of the updated Belize Building Act.
40.     Expected results. The National Building Code will establish modern building standards and practices that reduce
vulnerability to climate risks, protect lives and property, and support sustainable development. Building practices will
become more sustainable, supporting long-term economic growth and environmental conservation. By incorporating risk
assessments and hazard mapping into building regulations, the code will help reduce the vulnerability of communities to
climate change. Enhanced coordination among government agencies and stakeholders will lead to more effective
enforcement of building standards and climate risk management. The integration of climate and disaster risk information,
energy-efficient building materials and design strategies into building regulations will not only enhance energy efficiency
and occupant comfort but also support informed decision-making and adaptive management practices while contributing
to GHG reduction. There is a one-year grace period under the regulations, whereby construction or demolition of buildings
that commenced before, and are completed within one year after the regulations entered into force, may continue under
the previously applicable standards instead of the new National Building Code. This grace period will allow for targeted
technical assistance, training, and capacity building of construction workers to ensure 100 percent compliance of approved
building plans with the National Building Code.

Pillar 2: Strengthening Financial and Fiscal Resilience Against Natural Hazards and Climate Change

41.       Pillar 2 is foundational to Belize’s DRM strategy, enabling both rapid recovery and sustained resilience against
the growing risks posed by climate change and natural hazards. Strengthening financial and fiscal resilience equips Belize
with the tools and buffers needed to respond quickly to hazards, reducing reliance on emergency aid and minimizing long-
term economic disruptions. Natural hazards and climate change pose recurring financial risks, and a robust fiscal
framework enables Belize to manage these shocks without derailing development goals or accumulating unsustainable
debt. By maintaining fiscal stability, Belize avoids excessive borrowing and the diversion of funds from critical social
services and development projects, thereby protecting public finances and supporting long-term resilience investments.
Furthermore, by increasing insurance penetration and affordability, Belize will build a stronger safety net against climate
risk for individuals, businesses, and public entities, reducing the implicit contingent liabilities of the GoB. The Cat-DDO also
provides contingent financing for rapid access after hazard events, and strong fiscal policies ensure that Belize meets
eligibility requirements to leverage these funds effectively, strengthening its response capacity. To ensure comprehensive
coverage and effectiveness across all sectors, the MoF will lead the mainstreaming of disaster and climate risk
considerations into sectoral budget planning while inter-ministerial coordination mechanisms will be further explored and
strengthened to ensure alignment of financial risk management objectives across sectors. Public disclosure of policies and
risk financing instruments will be a critical communication strategy to raise awareness across government agencies and
the public.

 PA4: To enhance financial preparedness and resilience to natural hazards, the Recipient, through its Cabinet, has
 approved a Disaster Risk Financing (DRF) Policy and Implementation Plan, which mandates activities aimed at
 strengthening the Recipients’s fiscal and financial resilience against climate risk and enabling national budgetary
 decisions based on an understanding of economic impacts of disasters and trends over time. 18
42.     Rationale. From 2016 to 2023, the average annual direct damage from disasters in Belize was approximately
US$34 million, with Hurricane Lisa alone causing US$66 million in damages and US$31 million in economic losses. Despite
existing legislation aimed at enhancing disaster preparedness and risk mitigation, along with the benefits of various
instruments such as CCRIF and CERC, the IDB/World Bank DRF assessment for Belize emphasized the need to formalize a
DRF policy within the national budgetary process to improve financial resilience. The existence of the DRF policy is
expected to reduce delays in fund mobilization and increase response efficiency. Current legislation does provide financial
instruments for emergency allocations, such as special warrants under the Finance and Audit (Reform) Act (2005), but
proactive measures to build climate risk and hazard preparedness are lacking. Additionally, existing systems lack the
guidance for operationalizing a comprehensive, risk-layered financing framework that integrates domestic reserves,
contingent credit, and risk transfer solutions to address diverse climate and disaster risks (including pandemic threats and
slow-onset climate events) with poorly defined roles and responsibilities for triggering and disbursing funds.

 IV. OTHER DESIGN AND APPRAISAL ISSUES

 A. Poverty and Social Impacts
53.     The policy reforms under Pillar 1 are expected to moderately decrease household vulnerabilities and mitigate
the effects of future climate-related disasters. In Belize, poverty is concentrated in rural areas where households rely on
agriculture, making them highly vulnerable to climate impacts such as erratic rainfall and water scarcity. These issues are
exacerbated by low education levels, limited healthcare and inadequate infrastructure, including insufficient access to
clean water, sanitation, and electricity. Establishing the National Preparedness and Response Plan (PA1) will strengthen
disaster response capabilities, providing protection to those affected by climate-related crises, including the poorest and
most vulnerable. Adoption of a National Land Use Policy (PA2) promotes sustainable agricultural practices and protects
natural resources essential for rural livelihoods, reducing vulnerability to climate shocks. Issuance of regulations consisting
of a National Building Code (PA3) is expected to improve construction standards to ensure safer housing that can
withstand extreme weather, including in poor, high-risk areas.
54.     Pillar 2 has the potential to strengthen the country’s fiscal resilience to natural disasters, with mostly indirect
implications for poverty. By enhancing Belize’s fiscal capacity to manage and respond to such events, the proposed PAs
may reduce the social and economic costs stemming from these disasters. The adoption of a DRF Policy (PA4) will provide
a structured approach to managing financial risks associated with natural hazards, which coupled with an annual Fiscal
Strategy Plan (PA5), will improve fiscal stability and enable quicker fund mobilization for disaster response and recovery.
The issuance of microinsurance regulations (PA6) should foster the development of insurance products (including
microinsurance) designed for natural disasters, helping covered households and businesses to quickly respond to and
recover from natural hazards, mitigating the impact of these shocks, which may have longer-term implications for poverty
reduction.

 B. Environmental, Forests, and other Natural Resources Aspects
55.      PAs 2 and 3 under Pillar 1 supported through this operation are anticipated to have significant positive effects
on Belize’s environment, forests, and other natural resources. The approved National Land Use Policy (PA2) emphasizes
the productive and sustainable use of land, particularly in rural and agricultural contexts. It aims to reduce land
degradation and promote sustainable land management practices that improve soil health, conserve water resources, and
protect biodiversity, thereby strengthening the resilience of both ecosystems and local communities. Additionally, the
policy is expected to support the creation of land use plans that align public infrastructure needs with environmental
preservation, promoting sustainable development. The issuance of regulations consisting of a National Building Code
(PA3), is expected to establish and standardize sustainable and climate-resilient building construction, supporting long
term economic growth and promoting environmental conservation by reducing GHG emissions, 22 improving water and
energy efficiency, and encouraging nature-based solutions, among others.

regulations support land use by regulating development and conserving natural resources. The EIA ensures that any
proposed land and infrastructure development project is screened for potential environmental impacts and designed to
minimize harm. The Department of the Environment is the main authority in Belize for environmental management and

 C. PFM, Disbursement, and Auditing aspects
57.     The Government is committed to strengthening the country’s PFM to address identified weaknesses. According
to the IMF Technical Assistance Report 2023 and the World Bank’s Public Expenditure Report 2023, the PFM challenges
include the lack of budget execution reports; a backlog in preparing government audited financial statements since
2011/12; and limited capacity in public institutions for planning, budgeting, accounting, procurement, and auditing. The
Government is implementing the PFM reforms, which are expected to enhance fiscal discipline. The reforms include
enactment of the Finance and Audit (Reform) Act (FMAA) 2021, which led to the establishment of a Contingencies Fund
to manage financial risks and aid in quick recovery post-emergency, setting up of an internal audit unit, preparation of
backlog financial statements, vetting and approval of all major contracts by the Contractor-General before awards, and
use of limited tendering procedures under emergency situations. While the FMAA provides for the streamlining and
harmonization of the procurement procedures, the country needs to update its older Handbook on Public Procurement
Procedures 2013, which is determined as rudimentary emergency procurement protocols. The country also needs to
enhance the Auditor General’s capacity to address the backlog of audits and post-disaster audits. The IMF and IDB are
supporting the Government in implementing these PFM and procurement reforms, which are included in the Plan BELIZE
Medium-Term Development Strategy 2022-26. The MoF publishes the Government’s estimates of revenues and
expenditures on its website (https://mof.gov.bz), and the finalized Auditor General reports are published on the Supreme
Audit Institution’s website (https://audit.gov.bz). Though there is a backlog in the Government’s accounts audits, the
ongoing PFM reforms are considered adequate and sufficient for the World Bank to rely on the country’s PFM systems to
process the proceeds of the Development Policy Operation.
58.      The foreign exchange (FOREX) internal control environment does not pose risks to the development objectives
of this operation. The external auditors issued an unqualified audit opinion on CBB’s financial statements for the year
ending December 31, 2023. According to the CBB Annual Report 2023, its Audit Committee monitors the compliance with
the bank’s risk management policies and procedures and the findings are reported to the Board. The audited financial
statements, available on the CBB’s website, were prepared using International Financial Reporting Standards.
59.     Based on the fiduciary assessment, the fiduciary risk is moderate. Mitigation measures include capacitating the
Contractor-General for emergency procurement, the Accountant-General’s department for addressing the backlog of
annual audit reports, implementing an emergency contingency fund, and strengthening FOREX internal controls at the
Central Bank. These measures will ensure that credit financing is received and credited to the Government’s budget for
public expenditure.

 V. SUMMARY OF RISKS AND MITIGATION

62.     Macroeconomic risks are deemed Substantial. The country’s heavy dependence on tourism and energy imports
makes it vulnerable to external shocks such as global recessions, travel restrictions, trade restrictions, and changes in
energy prices. Global economic uncertainty further threatens inflation and economic growth, potentially affecting the
operation’s implementation. The country is prone to climate-related disruptions such as flooding, wind damage, and
coastal erosion, which intensify the risks. These risks will be mitigated by sound implementation of the PAs prioritized by
the operation.
63.     Implementation capacity risks are rated Substantial, given the country’s small pool of technical experts. The
limited number of technical experts in core ministries and scarce fiscal resources may jeopardize the Cat-DDO-supported
reform program. In this context, the Government, in agreement with the World Bank, has carefully prioritized a limited
number of critical reforms to improve Belize’s implementation capacity for disaster risk and climate change management.
The World Bank will continue to provide technical assistance for implementation of reforms complementary and
harmonized with the technical and financial assistance from other international organizations, including Caribbean
Regional Technical Assistance Centre, the IMF, and the CDB.
64.     Sector strategies and policy risks are considered Substantial. The successful implementation of the PAs over the
coming years will depend on the ability of the sectors to overcome structural weaknesses, operational inefficiencies, and
potential funding limitations. To mitigate this risk, PA1, PA2, and PA4 will be complemented with implementation plans
to ensure adequate mechanisms are introduced for the intended benefits to be fully realized. The achievement of the
expected results related to PA2 will depend on subsequent actions including the promulgation of a National Planning and
Development Act, and, in accordance with that Act, establishment of a national planning and development commission
and agency, and development and approval of a national land use plan (and possibly also the appointment of related
commissions and agencies and approval of plans at certain local jurisdictions)—actions which have yet to be completed
and whose completion timeline is uncertain. National and local land use plans do not become legally binding until they
have been approved in accordance with the new Act. As mitigants for this risk, the Government has committed to taking
actions for the completion of these actions in the Letter of Development Policy. Additionally, under the Belize Blue Cities
and Beyond Project (P181064), the World Bank is supporting the development of the national plan and select local plans,
which are already underway. The microinsurance PA6 presents a specific challenge of achieving and maintaining an
increase in insured policyholders, which requires strong institutional support, effective regulatory frameworks, and
sustained public engagement. This risk is being mitigated through close coordination with ministries of targeted sectors

 PA1: To improve governing processes for managing the effects of climate hazards, the Recipient, through its Cabinet,

1.      Belize is highly vulnerable to natural hazards, and the frequency and severity of these events are expected to
increase due to climate change. A 2021 phone survey revealed that 77 percent of households in Belize consider themselves
exposed to at least one climate-related disaster risk, with tropical cyclones and hurricanes being the most prominent—52
percent of households perceived them as a direct risk (Figure 3.1). The same survey also revealed that only 64 percent of
households in Belize had access to a disaster response or emergency plan, and less than 50 percent had access to a shelter
and an evacuation route (Figure 3.2). Moreover, the impacts of natural disasters are not experienced equally. Based on a
review of existing global evidence and data, Erman et al. (2021) found that women and girls often face a disproportionate
burden due to existing inequalities in access to economic opportunities, resources, and agency. For example, women and
children accounted for 74 percent of the population affected by Tropical Storm Arthur in 2008 (Palmisano 2022). The
approval of an NDPRP is a key step toward improving climate preparedness and resilience in Belize. By providing operational
guidance throughout all phases of DRM, the NDPRP has the potential to strengthen Belize’s capacity to manage natural
disasters and their consequences.

2.       The NDPRP is expected to moderately contribute to poverty reduction by facilitating the mitigation of the
negative impacts of natural disasters, including for vulnerable communities. By strengthening coordination and
institutional capacity to respond to natural hazards, the NDPRP can enhance the Government’s ability to respond effectively
to crises and protect low-income households, which are often the most affected by disasters (Hallegatte et al. 2020). By
improving communication flows and establishing coordinated, functional, and hazard-specific plans, including around
evacuation and emergency responses, the NDPRP directly contributes to reducing gaps in household-level preparedness—
such as limited access to early warning systems and shelters. However, the plan’s effectiveness will ultimately depend on
the GoB’s ability to execute activities included in the implementation plan that accompanies the NDPRP. Without this, the
NDPRP’s impact on poverty and disaster resilience may remain limited.

 PA2: To enhance climate resilient planning, the Recipient, through its Cabinet, has approved the National Land Use
 Policy, which promotes the reduction of climate vulnerabilities through a plan-led approach for sustainable use,
 management, and development of land.
3.      Belize is experiencing rapid urbanization. Between 2000 and 2023, the population in Belize increased by
approximately 71 percent (World Bank 2025). This growth, coupled with the absence of Land Use Policy, has led to
unplanned developments in high-risk zones, including coastal areas, and in land previously used for agriculture or
conservation. As a result, along with the rising frequency and intensity of natural hazards, Belize’s vulnerability to climate-
related disasters has increased. The projected population growth over the coming decades is likely to exacerbate this
vulnerability, particularly for households in these high-risk areas. 29 A cohesive Land Use Policy that protects environmental
assets and limits the share of the population exposed to risks in these areas is essential for promoting sustainable
development and enhancing resilience to climate and disaster risks.
4.      The implementation of the National Land Use Policy is expected to have a moderately positive impact on poverty
reduction in Belize. By steering land development away from high-risk areas and promoting sustainable land management
practices, the policy has the potential to reduce the vulnerability of low-income communities to climate-related
disasters. This would help protect both livelihoods and property in the long term. Additionally, by ensuring equitable access

 PA3: To strengthen the resilience of newly constructed buildings, the Recipient, through its Minister responsible for

5.       In Belize, many low-income and vulnerable households are at significant risk from natural hazards due to
inconsistent building standards. The Belize Building Act, first enacted in 2003 and amended in 2020, aimed to standardize
building regulations, but it lacks a national building code tailored to the specific climate and disasters risks of the region.
As a result, buildings are often constructed based on various international codes, which do not adequately consider the
country’s unique disaster risks. Vulnerable communities, especially those in hazard-prone areas, are disproportionately
affected by these gaps, making them more susceptible to property damage and losses during climate-related events such
as hurricanes and floods. A 2021 survey of Belizean households found that those unable to meet basic needs were
significantly less likely to be prepared for disasters (Anglade et al. 2024). Using the possession of emergency supplies as a
proxy for preparedness, the study showed a 26-percentage point increase in preparedness among households able to meet
basic needs, after considering other household characteristics. A similar association was observed when using self-reported
preparedness, with a 25-percentage point increase linked to the ability to cover basic needs.
6.       The issuance of regulations consisting of a National Building Code is expected to have a moderately
positive impact on poverty reduction by improving resilience to climate-related disasters and promoting safer, more
sustainable construction practices. By ensuring that new buildings are designed to withstand climate risks, the regulations
will reduce the vulnerability of low-income communities, especially those in hazard-prone areas, thereby decreasing
property damage and other losses during disasters. Additionally, the focus on energy-efficient building materials and design
strategies will contribute to long-term cost savings for households, particularly for vulnerable groups. However, the impact
may be limited if enforcement of the new standards is weak or if low-income households face challenges in meeting the
updated building regulations. Moreover, while the updated building codes will provide long-term benefits, the immediate
financial burden of compliance, especially for low-income families, could create some barriers to their full adoption. While
the regulations will support long-term resilience, their effectiveness in reducing poverty will depend on the Government’s
ability to support these vulnerable groups in meeting the new requirements.

 strengthening the Recipient’s fiscal and financial resilience against climate risk and enabling national budgetary
 decisions based on an understanding of economic impacts of disasters and trends over time.

 PA5: To enhance fiscal discipline and stability, the Recipient, through its Cabinet, has approved a policy that requires
 the Ministry of Finance to annually prepare and publish on its website a Fiscal Strategy Plan including a Medium-
 Term Fiscal Framework.
7.      Belize faces significant fiscal challenges in managing the financial risks and liabilities associated with natural
hazards. On average, the GoB needs to cover losses of approximately US$29.5 million annually—about 1.7 percent of GDP—
to address contingent liabilities from hurricanes and flooding (World Bank 2018). Hurricane damage alone is projected to

8.       Together, the DRF Policy and the Fiscal Strategy Plan may have an indirect impact on poverty reduction by
enhancing Belize’s ability to manage financial risks and improve disaster response. By incorporating disaster risk
assessments and contingencies into fiscal planning, the GoB may be able to better anticipate and manage fiscal pressures,
which could help reduce socioeconomic impacts on affected populations. Additionally, aligning resource allocation with
risk reduction and resilience-building measures would improve the country’s ability to respond to natural hazards.
However, the success of both policies will depend on effective enforcement and implementation and the extent to which
fiscal resilience contributes to policies and programs that benefit the poorest and most vulnerable.

 PA6: To enhance financial protection and recovery from climate-induced hazards, the Recipient, through the
 Minister for insurance, has issued microinsurance regulations, which enhance industry oversight and enable the
 expansion of affordable risk insurance to vulnerable populations.
9.       In Belize, the availability and penetration of private catastrophe insurance remain low, leaving households and
businesses vulnerable to the financial impacts of natural disasters. In 2023, the penetration rate for non-life insurance in
Belize stood at just 2.7 percent, far below the average for many SIDS in Latin America. This low coverage rate limits the
ability of Belizeans to shield themselves from the risks of frequent climate-related events such as hurricanes, floods, and
droughts. Despite the existence of some insurance products, the protection gap for low-income households and small
businesses remains large. Recent steps, such as the passing of the New Insurance Act of 2023, aim to address this gap by
enabling the introduction of microinsurance—a more affordable and accessible insurance option for vulnerable groups.
However, the successful implementation of microinsurance requires comprehensive regulations to guide the industry,
ensuring that these products are designed to be inclusive, reliable, and financially sustainable.
10.     The approval of the microinsurance regulations can be part of a strategy to provide low-income populations with
financial protection against natural hazards, enabling them to recover more quickly after disasters. Evidence from other
contexts suggests that the demand for microinsurance among the poor tends to be low (Platteau et al. 2017). However,
microinsurance can act as a tool for resilience (Jensen and Barrett 2016) if information gaps that hinder access are
addressed. For example, Janzen and Carter (2019) indicate that “there is growing evidence that insurance encourages
investment in higher risk activities with higher expected profits”. While noting that “the findings regarding microinsurance’s
impact on consumption smoothing are mixed”, the authors find that insured households in Kenya are less likely to sell
productive assets in response to shocks after receiving insurance payouts—preserving their long-term economic prospects.
By reducing financial vulnerability and enhancing transparency and accountability in the insurance sector, the new
regulations may promote wider adoption and foster economic stability for Belize’s most at-risk populations. Ultimately, the
success of these regulations will depend on the Government's ability to enforce quality standards and stimulate demand
by addressing issues of affordability and awareness and other market barriers.
