 I. STRATEGIC CONTEXT
 A. Project Strategic Context
 1.     Armenia has recently experienced notable economic shifts, compounded by a significant influx of refugees that
 has placed additional pressure on the already strained country’s public services and social assistance programs. The
 Government’s response aims to address both the immediate humanitarian needs and the broader structural issues within
 the housing sector. However, persistent challenges in the private housing market, such as limited affordability, an
 underdeveloped mortgage landscape, and potentially inadequate housing supply, have highlighted the need for a
 coordinated and innovative national response.
 2.      Within this context, the Armenia Housing Response and Mortgage Market Enhancement (HOME) project seeks
 to provide refugees with targeted support in acquiring housing solutions, while also bridging critical gaps in national
 housing and mortgage markets. By focusing on supporting both institutional development and robust housing finance
 mechanisms, the project aims to promote the longer-term social inclusion of refugees and strengthen their economic
 stability as well as disaster and climate resilience to increasing floods, extreme heat, and drought in alignment with
 Armenia’s National Adaptation Plan1 that highlights human settlements as a key priority area. With this, the project aims
 to foster resilience in the face of climate risks and disaster vulnerabilities. It incorporates lessons learned from past
 interventions aligned with Armenia’s broader development agenda, prioritizing the needs of vulnerable populations,
 particularly refugees. Considering refugees’ lower socio-economic status and reduced capability to cope with disaster
 and climate risks,2 the long-term goal is to ensure that strengthened institutional frameworks for housing sector
 development and access to affordable and resilient housing, as well as improved access to long-term funding for the
 mortgage market, are central to the country’s path forward.
 B. Sectoral and Institutional Context
 3.      In late September 2023, following the renewed conflict between Armenia and Azerbaijan, Armenia experienced
 an influx of approximately 115,000 refugees,3 constituting about a 4 percent increase in the country's total population.
 The refugees crossed into Armenia over the course of one week, with arrivals peaking at about 15,000 per day. These
 refugees included a high share of vulnerable people—about 31 percent children, 18 percent older persons, and 16
 percent people with disabilities—immediately straining Armenia’s housing, social protection, education, and health
 systems.4 Having left behind their main capital assets, the majority of refugees arrived in Armenia with few belongings
 and required urgent assistance. Early assessments indicated acute near-term needs, including food and water, basic
 necessities, shelter, medical services, and other support for well-being, alongside structural pressures on housing and
 municipal services. Broadly, many refugees5 were—and remain—located in Yerevan and nearby towns and villages, with
 the rest spread across the country.
 4.       The Government of Armenia (GoA), in coordination with international partners, acted swiftly and introduced
 several measures to address urgent needs of the refugees and to support their economic and social integration. To
 cover critical short-term financial needs, the GoA provided all refugees with emergency cash transfers of AMD 100,000
 (US$263) per person during September and October 2023. From October 2023, the GoA also introduced streamlined
 assistance to all refugees in the form of monthly cash support of AMD 40,000 (US$105) for rent and AMD 10,000 (US$26)
 for utilities (Decision N 1763-L dated October 12, 2023),6 hereafter referred to as the Social Assistance Program, or SAP.7

 5.      This rapid arrival of refugees increased pressure on Armenia’s public services and financial resources. The fiscal
 deficit is expected to rise sharply in 2025 to 5.1 percent of Gross Domestic Product (GDP) from 3.5 percent in 2024, in
 part due to the immediate assistance programs supporting refugees.10 Nevertheless, despite the short-term strain on
 fiscal capacities and resources, the GoA has remained fully committed to integrating refugees into Armenian society, with
 a focus on helping them overcome potential disparities in housing conditions and ensuring their access to social and
 employment services. This approach has been highly impactful in preventing potentially problematic temporary solutions
 and inadequate conditions (for example, tent camps and overcrowded units), while empowering refugees with the
 agency and freedom to move around the country in response to their needs, family ties, and jobs.
 6.      To provide eligible refugee households with the opportunity to obtain permanent housing, the GoA
 subsequently introduced the state housing support program in May 2024 (Decree N 710-Լ dated May 16, 2024),11
 hereinafter, the Housing Support Program or HSP. The objective of the HSP is to provide financial assistance to refugee
 households to help them to acquire housing solutions. The program targets refugee households that do not own real
 estate in Armenia,12 did not receive government housing support in 2020, do not have two or more working-age family
 members living abroad, and have obtained Armenian citizenship.13 Refugees in Armenia can own property regardless of
 citizenship status, and all refugees can obtain or own housing and other property in Armenia. The precondition to acquire
 Armenian citizenship applies only to those receiving permanent housing that is fully repaid by the GoA through the HSP.
 HSP-eligible refugee households are provided with Housing Purchase Certificates (HPCs) to obtain housing solutions of
 their choice and location. They can get 10-year mortgages at market rates from Participating Financial Institutions (PFIs)
 that are fully repaid over time by the GoA. Based on the World Bank estimates, up to around 13,000 refugee households
 are expected to qualify for support under the HSP (see annex 3 for details). Since the launch of the HSP, about 3,020 HPCs
 have been approved (of which approximately 455, or 15 percent of the total HPCs, are for female-headed households),
 and about 1,050 mortgages (of which 169, or 16 percent, are for female-headed households) have been originated to
 date.
 7.      Addressing the housing needs of eligible refugees has revealed several shortcomings within existing national
 housing initiatives. There is a preexisting backlog of about 20,000 Armenian households living in temporary housing of
 various types that has accumulated over the past decades and in need of housing support.14 Previously executed housing
 programs have been fragmented and primarily tailored to support housing ownership solutions for specific population
 groups. These programs15 relied on significant subsidies for particular household groups to acquire units, with only a few
 directly targeting households from various vulnerable groups (for example, families with children or disabilities, and other
 displaced families), while many exclusively targeted specific professional affiliations (for example, athletes, artists, and
 civil servants). As a result, the existing programs have omitted significant segments of underhoused populations and
 vulnerable groups and would require considerable improvements to their targeting and inclusion to respond effectively
 to the refugee influx. In addition, the high cost of subsidies per beneficiary and the ad-hoc availability of financial

 8.     Moreover, there is currently no dedicated institution solely responsible for all national housing policy
 formulation and coordination. While the Ministry of Labor and Social Affairs (MLSA) has some experience in
 implementing housing-related programs targeted at vulnerable groups, it does not have a formal mandate to elaborate
 and implement housing policy. Shifting from ad-hoc support to a more comprehensive, integrated national system of
 targeted, harmonized housing programs, guided by an overarching national housing framework and roadmap, would
 help ensure adequate housing options not only for refugees, but also for other vulnerable groups that require tailored
 support and housing solutions. In addition, providing a range of equitable housing choices would help promote access
 and fairness across all segments of the Armenian population. This will require establishing a clear and cohesive regulatory

 9.      The full implementation of the HSP entails substantial fiscal costs and poses liquidity challenges for the banking
 sector, requiring coordinated policy and institutional support to ensure effective implementation. At an estimated total
 cost of US$670 million (or 3 percent of GDP), financing of the HSP upfront would have breached the GoA’s fiscal rule by
 raising the debt-to-GDP ratio and fiscal deficit. Partnering with commercial banks to implement the HSP enhances the
 program’s security and efficiency, and experience with similar housing programs shows that most beneficiaries tend to
 borrow within program limits. Nevertheless, the estimated mortgages for up to 13,000 households that could participate
 in the program would constitute over 17 percent of the banking sector’s outstanding mortgages (in Armenian drams),
 and almost 2.3 percent of its assets. Hence, HSP implementation necessitates that commercial banks mobilize significant
 long-term local currency liquidity beyond their current capacity to originate mortgages, thereby increasing the need for
 the National Mortgage Company (NMC) to be involved and act as a refinancing entity to meet liquidity needs.
 10.     The NMC, established in 2009, provides long-term funding to support housing finance. As of September 2024,
 the NMC´s capital and loan from the German Development Bank (Kreditanstalt für Wiederaufbau, KfW) accounted for 70
 percent of total funding, while local bonds and other international finance institutions (IFIs) financing (mainly from the
 French Development Agency “Agence Française de Développement”, AFD) accounted for 21 percent and 9 percent,
 respectively. Since 2022, the NMC has participated in the post-2021 refugee housing support program, with nearly all
 refinanced loans receiving 100 percent support from the GoA. Social programs traditionally account for 10 percent of the
 NMC's refinancing portfolio, but this increased to 35 percent in 2023 and 45 percent in 2024, placing significant pressure
 on the NMC's balance sheet. The NMC also sustained below-market interest rates on those loans, gradually eroding its
 retained earnings reserves. As a result, the NMC's return on equity (ROE) decreased from 6.4 percent in 2022 to a
 projected 5.8 percent for 2024. Although the NMC remains profitable, its current balance sheet is small relative to the
 HSP’s size, which is five times larger than the 2022 program, with a total estimated cost ranging from US$500 million to
 US$670 million. Continuing to participate in subsidized mortgage programs would render the NMC unsustainable,16 and
 there is a need to improve the NMC’s capital and governance arrangements. Meeting even 50 percent of the HSP’s
 liquidity demand would require the NMC to double its current balance sheet, including securing funds from external
 sources and domestic investors.

 437,000 private houses,17 with approximately 60 percent of these units in urban areas and 30 percent in Yerevan, where
 infrastructure, employment, and social services are more accessible. Many apartment units were constructed before

 12.     Armenia's mortgage and construction sectors have seen double-digit growth over the last four years, driven by
 state incentives, including tax credits for mortgage interest payments (with the program to be phased out by 2027) and
 a large inflow of foreign citizens in 2022 (see annex 2). The mortgage-to-GDP ratio has risen from 4 to 15 percent between
 2016 and 2024, which is slightly above Europe and Central Asia (ECA) peers.22 Mortgage interest rates have remained
 about 12.5–14 percent, with nonperforming loans (NPLs) have remained below 2 percent over the last five years. The
 price index growth rate accelerated in the Armenian real estate market amid high economic activity, raising affordability
 concerns. With a homeownership rate exceeding 90 percent in Armenia and most families already owning homes without
 mortgages, the demand for housing finance is primarily influenced by affordability, new household formation, and the
 desire to upgrade living conditions.
 13.      Ensuring equitable access to safe and adequate housing is also complicated by Armenia’s high exposure to
 natural disasters and its increasing vulnerability to climate variability and climate change. The country faces projected
 average temperature increase of 2.8°C by 2050 and a significant increase in the number of very hot days. Armenia also
 regularly experiences high maximum temperatures. It is expected that the annual probability of heatwaves will increase
 from 3 percent currently to 5–18 percent by the end of the century, with higher average temperatures in the built-up
 and populated cores projected to continue rising.23 Other major climate-induced hazards include floods, wildfires,
 droughts, hail, and landslides,24 which occur very frequently and, over the past 25 years, have caused more than US$1.5
 billion in assessed damages and losses (equivalent to average annual damage and losses of more than US$76.5 million).
 Less frequent but high-impact geological events, such as earthquakes, can cause immense damage and losses, especially
 in the residential housing sector. Human settlements are highly exposed to these climate risks, and this is a key priority
 for Armenia’s National Adaptation Plan. Subsequently, the impacts of climate change and geophysical hazards will likely
 compound pre-existing fragility and accelerate internal displacement, disproportionately affecting the poorest and most
 vulnerable refugee households without sufficient means or resources for adaptation.

 15.     Theory of Change. The HOME project is designed with three mutually reinforcing elements: (a) delivering targeted
 housing subsidies—including rental assistance, down payments, and subsidies for mortgage installments — to eligible
 refugee households, alongside development of the GoA’s institutional and technical capacities for housing sector policy
 formulation and implementation; (b) providing liquidity support to the NMC to enable the commercial banks to originate
 mortgages for HSP beneficiaries; and (c) contributing to effective implementation of the HSP through upgrading of the
 HSP’s Management Information System (MIS), project management, communications, and citizen engagement. In the
 short term, these activities are expected to provide eligible beneficiaries with housing solutions; strengthen the GoA’s
 institutional and technical capacities for housing policy and program delivery; improve efficiency, functionality and
 reliability of the MIS; and catalyze private sector participation in the capital market by channeling institutional investor
 financing toward the NMC, thereby enhancing its capacity to refinance mortgages and extend long-term funding.
 Ultimately, these changes are intended to provide beneficiary households with improved access to housing solutions and
 to deepen Armenia’s mortgage market. In the long term, these outcomes will help strengthen institutional frameworks
 for housing sector development and broader access to housing, as well as improve access to long term funding for the
 mortgage market. Achieving these outcomes critically depends on several assumptions, including the sustained
 commitment of the GoA to the HSP, effective coordination and collaboration among all stakeholders throughout
 implementation, strong leadership by the MLSA to achieve long-term housing policy reforms, and strengthening of the
 NMC’s governance framework.
 C. Project Beneficiaries
 16.     The project is expected to reach an estimated total of 51,200 refugee beneficiaries.26 It is expected to support
 approximately 44,508 refugees with cash assistance for rental, utilities, and other consumption needs. The project is also
 expected to support about 6,500 refugee households with down payments and mortgage installments. By improving
 refugees’ access to housing, the project aims to enhance their resilience to climate-related risks—including droughts,
 extreme heat, and floods—given that refugees are often among the most vulnerable to their impacts. Support for
 refugees is also expected to produce positive spillover effects in the host communities by stimulating local economic
 activity through increased spending on goods and services. The GoA and its agencies, directly or indirectly involved in the
 housing sector, will also benefit from institutional development activities.
 17.     The project will strengthen the country’s financial sector by deepening the mortgage market and improving its
 performance through the funding to the NMC, while creating incentives to expand long-term local currency financing
 in the system. This is critical for mortgage affordability, since longer loan terms lower monthly payments, as commercial
 banks need to align the maturity of their assets and liabilities.

 F. Lessons Learned and Reflected in the Project Design
 37.    The project draws on a wealth of experience and global knowledge in aiding refugees, including through cash
 assistance, which serves as an effective support mechanism in emergencies or crisis situations. Experience from crises,
 including the World Bank-financed projects in Bangladesh, Colombia, Chad, Jordan, and Peru, among others, shows that
 cash transfers are preferable to other forms of support (for example, in kind). Government-led cash assistance and
 subsidies have proven to be operationally easier to channel and cost-effective, give beneficiaries flexibility, and maximize
 their agencies. They also empower beneficiaries to allocate the funds in the best ways to meet housing, utilities, and
 consumption needs. Predictable cash support also allows refugees to build resilience, prevent worsening living
 conditions, and avoid negative coping strategies.46 Cash assistance is also likely to improve socioeconomic outcomes,47
 increase adaptive capacities, promote climate resilience, and produce positive spillover effects in host communities by
 stimulating local economic activity through increased spending on goods and services. This higher demand for goods and
 services directly benefits local businesses and host communities through additional tax revenues and income multiplier
 effects.48

 IV.PROJECT APPRAISAL SUMMARY
A. Technical, Economic, and Financial Analysis
57.     The World Bank has provided analytical and just-in-time advisory support to the GoA and NMC during the
preparation and rollout of the HSP. That included financial modeling and cost analysis to optimize and reduce the fiscal
risks to the GoA, including simulations of program size, potential beneficiaries, and costs; analysis of approaches for
providing durable housing solutions, including ownership and formalizing rental solutions; and analysis of 14 existing
housing programs administered by the GoA to identify guiding principles to inform the design of the HSP.
58.    The HSP is grounded on solid program design principles: (a) being demand-driven and beneficiary led, with
beneficiaries having the ability/choice to locate anywhere in the country (except central Yerevan where demand is very
high); (b) capping the maximum benefit; (c) applying a minimum size for units, as measured in square meters per capita,

 Economic and Financial Analysis
 63.    Data analysis and simulations undertaken by the World Bank indicate strong economic justification for the
 project. Analysis of the net present value (NPV) and economic internal rate of return (EIRR) indicates positive quantitative
 benefits from the project activities. The economic analysis was conducted as a traditional incremental cost-benefit
 analysis over a 15-year horizon. It considered two scenarios: (a) a baseline ‘without-project’ case that reflects the status
 quo whereby refugee households continue to receive the existing per-person rental allowances through 2026, without
 access to long-term housing finance mechanisms, and (b) a ‘with-project’ case systematically identifying all relevant
 benefit and cost streams that arise exclusively as a result of the project.
 64.     The model has been developed using costs comprising the subsidies for down payments, mortgage repayments,
 and cash assistance, and the following main benefits: (a) consumption impulse from subsidies freeing up household
 budgets for essentials and generating secondary effects for landlords; (b) construction multiplier from housing purchases
 that stimulate both resale and new construction, with each dollar invested yielding more than one dollar of GDP; (c)
 impact of housing price appreciation/depreciation; and (d) imputed rent as homeowners gain housing services they
 would otherwise rent, valued at market rates.
 65.     On this basis, the EIRR is calculated on US$250 million against cash flows from (a) construction stimulus,
 reflecting multiplier effects of housing-related spending across the broader economy; (b) the real appreciation or
 depreciation of the housing stock, net of inflation and maintenance costs; and (c) the annual value of imputed rent by
 estimating the annual rental equivalent value of owner-occupied housing, representing the benefit of housing
 consumption by program beneficiaries. A 9 percent discount rate is applied, as defined for similar public infrastructure
 investments in the Government Decree on Public Investment Management.59 The analysis assumes 6,500 households
 (half of the total estimated 13,000 households that could be supported at the maximum subsidy level), which would
 require US$670 million. Although early uptake may be lower, the base case assumes gradual scale-up, accounting for the
 administrative and outreach constraints in the HSP’s initial years. That assumes that 30 percent of the total units will be
 newly constructed.
 66.     Calculations yield an EIRR of 18.6 percent, an NPV of US$25.35 million, and a cost-benefit ratio (CBR) of 1.08,
 supporting the project’s value creation. Sensitivity analyses were conducted on two key variables affecting the project’s
 EIRR and NPV: (a) housing price appreciation/depreciation and (b) the timing of beneficiary uptake. Results show that
 uptake distribution —whether more front-loaded or larger overall- does not have a negative impact on the NPV. Housing
 prices, however, have an effect on the EIRR. Positive results occur with annual appreciation of about 6 percent (±0.5
 percent) or higher, while the break-even level is about 4 percent. Below this threshold, outcomes become less favorable.
 67.   Other key factors affecting project resilience include housing price trends and interest rate fluctuations.
 Armenia’s inflation has averaged about 4 percent over the past 15 years, making a sharp decline in housing prices unlikely;
 however, to mitigate downside risk and address concerns about an overheating real estate market, the project includes
 a 30 percent down payment to provide financial institutions with a buffer against potential value drops. Interest rate
 changes also influence outcomes, but sensitivity analysis indicates that even with a ±2 percent variation from the 14
 percent base case, the project remains financially sound.
 68.      The project is also expected to provide significant wider benefits that have not been quantified in the project’s
 economic analysis. These include improving access to permanent housing for 6,500 refugee households which helps
 reduce potential negative human and welfare impacts from falling into poverty; enhancing the sustainability outlook of
 the mortgage market; helping reduce the fiscal burden from the HSP; providing predictable funding that offers the GoA
 flexibility in balancing between fiscal efficiency and management of government debt; and supporting institutional
 reforms in the housing sector such that it can more effectively cater to all groups, including the poor and vulnerable
 groups among the broader Armenian population.

                          ANNEX 2: MACROECONOMIC AND FINANCIAL SECTOR OVERVIEW
1.      Macroeconomic environment. After exceptional growth in 2022-2023 at 10.5 percent on average, Armenia’s
growth eased in 2024 to 5.9 percent. The growth in 2024 has been primarily driven by private consumption and
investment, buoyed by a 33 percent (yoy) average increase in consumer and mortgage loans, and a 6 percent increase in
real wages. Real GDP growth in H1 2025, driven mainly by private consumption and investment, reached 5.8 percent
(yoy). Inflation, which surged post-COVID-19 and peaked in 2022, has subsided, decreasing from 8.6 percent in 2022 to
3.4 percent (yoy) in July 2025. The CBA’s monetary policy facilitated a reduction in the inflation rate in Armenia and
improved its capacity to absorb shocks. The local currency (Armenian dram) experienced turbulence in the post-conflict
period in 2022, mitigated by the CBA’s episodic interventions in the exchange market.
2.     Although Armenia's debt-to-GDP ratio had been declining in recent years, it rose to 48 percent in 2024 from 46.7
percent in 2022 and will continue to increase due to fiscal pressures. The consolidated budget fiscal deficit increased
from 1.9 percent of GDP in 2023 to 3.5 percent in 2024 and is expected to reach 5.1 percent in 2025, partly from a nominal
increase in social allowances and pensions, including refugee support, which began in September 2023 and continued in
2024. Enhanced revenue mobilization will be necessary to finance the 2021–2026 Government Program, which includes
ambitious reforms in healthcare, wages, education, social protection, and infrastructure.
3.      Financial sector overview. By the end of 2024, Armenia’s financial system showed robust growth and soundness.
The assets-to-GDP and loans-to-GDP ratios reached 108 percent and 62.7 percent, respectively, with banking sector
assets rising 20 percent yoy to AMD 11,035 billion (US$29 billion). Commercial banks remained dominant, holding 83.2
percent of total assets, while pension funds grew to 7.2 percent amid easing inflation and stable global markets. Foreign
capital inflows led to 50 percent of deposits in foreign currency, boosting FX liquidity, though 68 percent of loans were
in AMD. Financial soundness indicators reflected strong capitalization—regulatory capital to risk-weighted assets at 20.2
percent (Tier 1 at 18.3 percent)—and profitability, with ROE jumping to 27 percent in 2024. The NPL ratio dropped to
1.24 percent.
4.      Armenia's mortgage and construction sectors have grown in double digits over the last four years, driven by state
incentives, including tax credits for mortgage interest payments (with the program to be phased out by 2027) and a large
inflow of foreign citizens in 2022. The generous mortgage interest tax credit, along with the Civil Code’s regulations on
how property developers may use funds held in escrow accounts have been the primary drivers of the housing market’s
overheating for the past years. The Government has already limited developers’ ability to draw from the escrow account,
while the planned phaseout of the tax credit exception by 2027 (2025 for the Yerevan area) is expected to help moderate
the pace of new mortgage lending. The mortgage-to-GDP ratio has risen from 4 percent in 2016 to 15 percent by the end
of 2024, which is comparable to its ECA peers. Mortgage interest rates remained about 12.5–14 percent, with NPLs have
remained below 2 percent over the last five years. The price index growth rate accelerated in the Armenian real estate
market amid high economic activity, raising affordability concerns. With a homeownership rate exceeding 90 percent in
Armenia and most families already owning homes without mortgages, the demand for housing finance is primarily
influenced by affordability, new household formation, and the desire to upgrade living conditions. The behavior of the
real estate market (including the mortgage market) in Armenia poses some risks to financial stability, with some signs of
overheating in the housing market. The CBA has implemented some macroprudential measures regarding mortgage
loans, including, in 2021, a maximum loan-to-value (LTV) ratio of 90 percent for local-currency (AMD) loans secured by
real estate and 70 percent for foreign-currency loans secured by real estate. In 2023, to reduce dollarization, the CBA
introduced a prohibition of FX mortgage loans to residents. The CBA also raised the countercyclical capital buffer to 1.5
percent in August 2023.

                                                     ANNEX 3: DETAILED DESCRIPTION OF COMPONENT 1
 1.      Implementation of the SAP. The SAP is administered by the MLSA. Beneficiaries receive cash assistance for the
 previous month directly into their registered bank accounts within 10–15 days of each month following eligibility
 verification by the MLSA. The list of eligible beneficiaries is verified by the MLSA through its NORK Center (Social Services
 Technology and Awareness Center) at the beginning of each month using information from the Migration and Citizenship
 Services to ensure physical presence in Armenia. Beneficiaries can withdraw cash using their existing commercial bank
 cards and make digital payments, including purchases at point-of-sale (POS) machines.
 2.       Implementation of the HSP. The HSP is administered by the MLSA, using the 49 USS territorial centers at the local
 level, and in partnership with PFIs. The HSP is demand-driven and beneficiary-led. Eligible refugee households apply
 online before the current deadline of December 31, 2028, and are issued a unique digital HPC following an automated
 eligibility check and document verification. The housing solutions are acquired on a willing buyer-willing seller basis, and
 the HPC, valid for one year and renewable, is used at participating commercial banks to obtain a 10-year mortgage loan
 once the household has identified a suitable housing solution. Commercial banks verify program eligibility and conduct
 the necessary due diligence on each property. Beneficiary households are not required to pay any transaction fees or
 taxes during the mortgage origination process, but are responsible for subsequent annual local taxes and property
 maintenance costs. Full ownership is transferred to the household after 10 years, once the mortgage loan has been fully
 repaid and the household has maintained good standing with respect to the HSP requirements.
 3.      Geographic eligibility. As the HSP operates nationally, its beneficiaries can reside anywhere in Armenia, except
 the core central areas of Yerevan (Zones 1 and 2). The GoA has excluded these two zones due to the elevated housing
 prices and overall high demand for real estate in these areas stemming from their historical significance, cultural and
 social attractiveness, and accessibility within central Yerevan.
 4.     Estimation of beneficiary households. According to Decree N 710-L, household composition is flexible and may
 include single-parent households, single member household units, and any other household configurations. There are no
 income requirements, and both salaried and non-salaried households can apply. Out of the 30,000 refugee households
 that arrived in Armenia, about 13,000 households are estimated to be eligible given (a) 3,000 households already
 benefited from the previous housing support program of 2020 (Decree N 169-L), (b) 7,000 households are expected to
 have at least one family member already owning an apartment or residential house in Armenia,68 (c) 3,000 households
 have an adult not residing in Armenia, and (d) 3,000 households (10 percent of the refugee household population) may
 opt out of applying due to various other reasons.
 5.     Maximum HPC values and related requirements. The total value of HPCs is determined by the number of family
 members, the property’s location, and whether the assistance will be for housing acquisition or to support an existing
 mortgage. The HSP also has maximum property values and defined parameters to ensure refugee households can attain
 basic minimum housing standards with secure titles and access to basic amenities and services. The level of support
 ranges from AMD 2 million (about US$5,256) to AMD 5 million (about US$13,140) per household member. Any positive
 balances (that is, if the housing solution price is below the maximum HPC value) can be used for housing renovations and
 the purchase of approved furniture and modern, energy efficient household appliances, subject to prescribed limits.
 6.     Controls and supervision of HSP. The HSP incorporates a framework of procedures, roles, and responsibilities,
 and oversight mechanisms to ensure adequate controls, FM, and supervision. To offer mortgage loans under the HSP,
 PFIs are required to enter into contractual agreements with the MLSA that include the terms of the program, monitoring
 and reporting procedures, E&S requirements, recourse mechanisms, among others.
