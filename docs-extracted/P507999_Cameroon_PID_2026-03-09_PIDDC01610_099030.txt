 B. Introduction and Context

 Country Context
Cameroon’s growth and job creation are constrained by limited access to finance, particularly for micro, small, and
medium enterprises (MSMEs). Macroeconomic pressures—including high public debt, inflation, and shallow financial
markets—have dampened private investment and credit expansion. The government’s National Strategy for the
Development of the Financial Sector (SNDSF) prioritizes expanding access to finance, strengthening credit infrastructure,
and developing the mortgage market.




 Sectoral and Institutional Context

MSME lending is hindered by high risk perception, collateral requirements, limited long-term funding in local currency,
and gaps in credit information and secured transactions systems. Public financial institutions require reform to better
support private sector finance. The operation supports: (i) de-risking MSME lending through a new PCG Facility; (ii)
provision of wholesale long-term local-currency finance via a reformed BC-PME; and (iii) upgrades to credit
infrastructure, including the credit reporting system and movable collateral registry. The project is aligned with the
Country Partnership Framework and complements macro-financial reforms supported by the World Bank and the IMF. It
is consistent with CCDR priorities to foster a greener, more resilient private sector.


 Relationship to CPF
The operation contributes to CPF objectives on private sector development, jobs, and financial sector deepening, with a
focus on inclusion (including women-owned MSMEs) and longer-term financing.

 D. Concept Description
The proposed US$200 million, seven-year operation aims to increase access to finance—particularly long-term finance—
for micro, small, and medium enterprises (MSMEs) in Cameroon to support investment, productivity, and job creation.
The project addresses binding constraints to MSME finance through risk-sharing instruments, institutional reforms,
long-term local-currency financing, and targeted capacity building.

The project will establish and capitalize a Partial Credit Guarantee (PCG) Facility to reduce credit risk for participating
financial institutions (PFIs) lending to creditworthy MSMEs that lack sufficient collateral. It will also support the
transformation of the Cameroon Bank for Small and Medium-Sized Enterprises (BC-PME) into a financially sustainable,
second-tier development finance institution and provide it with a long-term local-currency line of credit for on-lending
to MSMEs through eligible PFIs. IDA proceeds will be passed on by the Government of Cameroon, in local currency, to
the PCG Facility and BC-PME under agreed terms, with the Government retaining foreign-exchange risk. The project is
designed to mobilize private capital at scale.

A “jobs lens” will be embedded across the PCG Facility and the line of credit through a transparent targeting scorecard
prioritizing firms with high employment impact, women- and youth-led MSMEs, climate-resilient and low-carbon
investments, and selected value chains. The operation will be implemented as an Investment Project Financing (IPF) with
Performance-Based Conditions (PBCs) linked to institutional readiness, capitalization, and deployment of financing.

Project Components
    1. PCG Facility: Technical assistance and capitalization of a permanent partial credit guarantee scheme, including
        capacity building for PFIs to originate, manage, and monitor guaranteed MSME loans.
    2. BC-PME Transformation and Line of Credit: Institutional reform of BC-PME, long-term financing for MSMEs
        through PFIs, and technical assistance to BC-PME and PFIs on risk management, long-term lending, and
        environmental and social systems.
    3. Mortgage Finance and Credit Infrastructure: Support to mortgage finance reforms, and strengthening of select
        credit infrastructure.
    4. Project Implementation: Project management, fiduciary oversight, monitoring and evaluation, and outreach,
        including capacity building for MSMEs to improve bankability.

 Summary of Screening of Environmental and Social Risks and Impacts



At the concept stage, the overall environmental and social risks of the Cameroon Development Finance Project are
assessed as Moderate.

Environmental risks : Environmental risks are rated Moderate, as the Project has a low likelihood of causing significant
adverse impacts on human health or the environment. The Project will not directly finance physical works or large‑scale
industrial activities. Potential environmental risks are indirect and downstream, arising from micro, small, and medium
enterprise (MSME) activities financed through participating financial intermediaries (PFIs). These risks may include
inefficient use of natural resources, poor waste management practices, small‑scale pollution, generation of electronic
waste, and environmental degradation associated with agribusiness activities. While environmental risks at the level of
individual MSMEs are expected to be limited in scale and severity, cumulative impacts may occur in areas where MSME
activities are geographically concentrated. These risks are considered manageable through the application of robust
environmental and social risk management systems. In line with the Environmental and Social Framework,
Environmental and Social Standard 9 (Financial Intermediaries) applies, requiring financial intermediaries to identify,
assess, and manage environmental and social risks associated with their portfolios and subprojects through
appropriately designed Environmental and Social Management Systems (ESMS).

Social risk: Social risks are also rated Moderate. The Project is not expected to result in land acquisition, physical or
economic displacement, or loss of livelihoods. Key social risks relate to potential exclusion of certain groups from access
to finance, particularly informal enterprises, women‑owned MSMEs, youth‑led businesses, and persons with disabilities.
Additional risks include weak labor practices within MSMEs, occupational health and safety concerns, and limited
awareness or access to grievance redress mechanisms. These risks are expected to be mitigated through inclusive
eligibility criteria, targeted stakeholder engagement, and the strengthening and effective implementation of ESMS by
financial intermediaries. The Project’s design emphasizes proportional risk management, transparency, and accessibility,
which are expected to reduce the likelihood and severity of adverse social impacts.
