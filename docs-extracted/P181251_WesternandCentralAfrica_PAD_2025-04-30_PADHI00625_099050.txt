        I.   STRATEGIC CONTEXT

      1. Achieving the vision of ending extreme poverty and boosting shared prosperity in West and Central Africa is
         predicated on greater financial inclusion, that is ensuring that individuals and businesses have access to useful
         and affordable financial products and services that meet their needs delivered in a responsible and sustainable
         way. By providing financial services, individuals and businesses in the region can improve their income-generating
         activities, manage risks, and reduce their vulnerability to shocks, thereby helping to alleviate poverty. Access to
         financial services empowers individuals, particularly women and marginalized groups, by giving them the tools to
         manage their finances, invest in education and health, and improve their overall quality of life. For small and young
         firms, access to finance enables innovation, job creation, and growth; and this is particularly important for firms
         facing greater constraints. Additionally, by providing access to affordable housing finance, the region can improve
         access to affordable housing which is vital for reducing inequality, preventing overcrowding and informal
         settlements, and fostering sustainable urban growth. Affordable housing development can stimulate economies
         through creating jobs in contribution and related industries, and as part of integrated housing development, can
         reduce strains on urban infrastructure and promote more sustainable water, sanitation and transport services.
      2. The proposed US$205.0 million West Africa Economic and Monetary Union (WAEMU) Financial Inclusion for
         Micro, Small and Medium Enterprises (MSMEs) and Households Project is an innovative World Bank
         engagement with the West Africa Development Bank (Banque Ouest Africaine de Développement, BOAD) to
         address the very low levels of financial inclusion—for both households and MSMEs—in the WAEMU region,
         which is home to 140 million people in eight countries 1. It will promote access to finance for MSMEs, and to long
         term housing finance for lower income households in the subregion. This proposed World Bank engagement will
         work in synergy with the Multilateral Investment Guarantee Agency (MIGA)’s recently approved guarantee for
         private loans to BOAD, and International Finance Corporation (IFC)’s ongoing and future cooperation with BOAD.
         The Project is expected to generate approximately US$190 million in private capital mobilization (PCM), and to
         directly benefit approximately 8,000 households through affordable housing (estimated 40,000 household
         members) and 4,375 businesses and to create approximately 37,550 direct jobs. This does not include the indirect
         impact on jobs creation (such as through the MSME suppliers) as well as the impact in terms of added liquidity for
         housing and MSME loans. The Project will support firm resilience in the face of climate change, promote green
         transition, and support household resilience by financing green investments for MSME lending and green building
         standards for housing finance. The Project will help reduce the gender gap in access to finance for MSME and
         housing loans. The Project will promote regional integration through further developing the single WAEMU capital
         market, making longer term capital available for private investments, working with commercial pan-African banks
         present in multiple WAEMU countries, and promoting intra-regional learning and demonstration through sharing
         and dissemination of successful models.

    A. Project Strategic Context

      3. The WAEMU is a customs union and a currency union, consisting of eight member countries, which aims to
         promote economic integration beyond the existing monetary integration. WAEMU countries implement
         independent ﬁscal policies but have a common Central Bank of West African States (Banque Centrale des Etats de
         l’Afrique de l’Ouest, BCEAO) and a single monetary policy, which makes a strong argument for ﬁscal-monetary
         policy coordination. Recent geopolitical events, rising inﬂation, alongside tightening monetary policy and
         continued large public ﬁnancing needs, have accentuated the macro- stability risks. Inﬂation had historically been

         accumulated external debt arrears and signiﬁcant ﬁnancing needs in many countries.

    B. Sectoral and Institutional Context

WAEMU Financial System Overview
      5. The WAEMU ﬁnancial system is dominated by banks, which had total assets of FCFA 64 trillion (about US$100
         billion)—or 58 percent of regional GDP—at end-2022. The banking sector accounts for around 72 percent of the
         ﬁnancial system (based on 2022 data), while other ﬁnancial institutions (microﬁnance, insurance, and pension
         funds, as well as securities custodians) account for around 28 percent of the ﬁnancial system’s assets. Of the 155
         credit institutions operating in the WAEMUatend-2022, 132 were banks 5. The geographic concentration of their
         assets is broadly in line with that of economic activity, with over half held by credit institutions in the WAEMU two
         largest economies, Côte d’Ivoire and Senegal (34 percent and 19 percent, respectively). Regional banking groups
         (34) have emerged as key players, holding nearly 85 percent of banking sector assets. The 13 banks which are
         majority publicly owned (with public ownership over 50 percent of total capital), hold 10 percent of banking assets
         and 13 percent of banking capital in the WAEMU.

    7. WAEMU’s small and bank-centered investor base continues to be one of the key bottlenecks for capital markets
       development. The institutional investor base largely consists of banks. Non-bank institutional investors remain
       small and their participation in the regional capital markets is limited. WAEMU’s regional capital market remains
       fragmented due to a limited degree of legal, regulatory and tax harmonization. As a result, settlement cycles,
       documentation and transaction processes and costs vary from one country to another. The insurance sector still
       plays a marginal role in WAEMU’s ﬁnancial sector. The sector has seen some consolidation since the increase of
       the capital requirements in 2019; however, it remains very fragmented with more than 100 insurance companies
       operating in the WAEMU zone. Their portfolios remain concentrated in bank deposits and real estate. Pension
       fund assets in the zone represent about 4 percent of GDP. The investment portfolio of pension funds in WAEMU
       is not diversiﬁed and is mostly concentrated in government and banking sector securities. Despite good progress
       in improving the attractiveness of the corporate bond market and introducing new asset classes, WAEMU’s non
       sovereign securities markets remain nascent. The dependence on banks as the main institutional investors in the
       capital markets has created challenges beyond the capital markets, crowding out bank lending to the private
       sector. Banks’ sovereign exposures were about 38 percent of domestic assets in 2023, up from 30 percent at end-
       2019. Further, given the liquidity challenges of the domestic banks, liquidity in the capital markets has become
       heavily inﬂuenced by the availability of BCEAO reﬁnancing. The joint World Bank-IFC Joint Capital Markets
       Program (JCAP) Phase one has been supporting the development of foundations of capital markets, and a second
       phase is under preparation.
Financial System Stability
    8. The ﬁnancial system has remained broadly stable with slowly improving systemwide soundness indicators;
       however, performance across countries and banks is not uniform and overall, risks have increased due to the
       rising exposure of some banks to sovereigns and reliance on reﬁnancing from the Central Bank. The WAEMU
       banking system also remains exposed to signiﬁcant concentration risks on both the borrower side and the deposit
       side of bank balance sheets, posing a signiﬁcant vulnerability for both asset quality and continued liquidity.
       Speciﬁcally, the credit concentration of the ﬁve largest borrowers to capital, as an average for WAEMU banks, has
       risen since the COVID-19 pandemic. On the liquidity front, bank funding comes primarily from customers’ deposits,
       mainly short-term (“sight”) deposits also with signiﬁcant concentration. In addition to the inherent risk of deposit
       concentration, the declining liquidity buﬀers in the banking sector in the context of an underdeveloped interbank
       market and tightening monetary policy have heightened liquidity risks. Banks have been increasingly relying on
       BCEAO reﬁnancing and several banks persistently depended on this form of funding. The illiquidity of the
       secondary markets for government securities and the high concentration of deposits hampers banks’ ability to
       mitigate liquidity shocks and exacerbates liquidity risks and shortage in some banks. On the other hand, banks’

       9. The WAEMU zone faces significant liquidity pressures due to a shallow interbank market and macro-economic
          challenges. Liquidity risks in the banking sector in the region are intensifying due to shrinking liquidity buffers and
          an underdeveloped interbank market. In recent years, banks have seen a steady decline in their liquid assets. By
          June 2024, liquid assets represented only about 22.8 percent of total assets, with significant variation across the
          region (ranging from 13.3 percent in Benin to 27.9 percent in Mali) 8. This ratio remains relatively low compared
          to other Sub-Saharan African (SSA) countries, with comparator countries in the region having ratios from 30
          percent to a high of 50 percent. While banks hold reserves at the BCEAO, much of their assets either lack or have
          limited market liquidity. As a result, banks have increasingly relied on BCEAO refinancing. Moreover, recent
          monetary policy adjustments by the BCEAO to combat inflation have further tightened liquidity in the process.
       10. The surge in public debt in the aftermath of the COVID-19 pandemic has deepened the sovereign-bank nexus in
           the region and further reduced already scarce liquidity for the private sector. The public debt-to-GDP ratio rose
           from 44.8 percent in 2019 to 61 percent in 2023. A signiﬁcant share of public debt was bought by regional banks.
           WAEMU banks’ exposures to public debt instruments, including securities and direct loans, have signiﬁcantly
           increased since the pandemic, reaching 38 percent of total banks’ assets at end-2022 (from 30 percent at end-
           2019 9), well above the average for the SSA region. In the absence of stable demand for securities from institutional
           investors that would have long-term liabilities, banks buy securities with relatively long maturities and generally
           hold them to maturity. This increased ﬁnancing of the sovereign in the local banking sector results in a liquidity
           squeeze and a reduction in credit available for the private sector, which is experiencing diﬃculties in accessing
           ﬁnance.
       11. The WAEMU zone is highly exposed to climate risks. Increasing temperatures and shifting rainfall patterns are
           already aﬀecting livelihoods, food security, and economic and governance stability. The region’s long coastline,
           home to densely populated cities and economic hubs, is experiencing sea level rise and severe coastal erosion,
           Projected to increase with signiﬁcant impacts to coastal populations and urban economic centers. Crops and
           livestock, a base for about 60 percent of livelihoods and 35 percent of GDP regionally, face increasing heat stress
           and variability in rainfall. Transnational dimensions of climate impacts include food and water quality and
           availability, health conditions related to air quality, disruption of transportation networks, and migration. The
           WAEMU region is prone to a range of recurrent natural disasters such as drought, heat waves, ﬂoods, locust
           invasions, and wildﬁre. According to the EM-DAT 10 and Desinventar 11 databases, between 1970 and 2020, the
           region was aﬀected by more than 300 natural disasters. The most damaging events in economic terms in recent
           decades have been droughts and ﬂoods. And when combined together or with other external shocks, these
           phenomena are even more disastrous, especially for vulnerable populations and sectors.

Access to Finance for MSMEs
     13. Access to ﬁnancing continues to be a signiﬁcant hurdle for private sector development in WAEMU, with the
         ﬁnancial sector failing to adequately support businesses, in particular micro, small and medium enterprises
         (MSMEs) which represent between 80 percent and 95 percent of companies identiﬁed in the region. Bank credit
         growth to the private sector was robust in recent years but slowed to 10.3 percent in 2023, down from 14.2
         percent in 2022 13. This lending growth outpaced economic growth, reﬂecting a need for ﬁnancial deepening.
         However, the rapid increase in lending poses potential credit risks due to weak economic diversiﬁcation,
         information asymmetries regarding borrowers, and structural challenges in the business environment. Only 24.4
         percent of companies in the region have a loan or a line of credit, with formal borrowing rates notably low across
         countries, ranging from 10 percent in Burkina Faso to 2.8 percent in Côte d'Ivoire. Although banks are the principal
         source of ﬁnance for MSMEs, only 16 percent of bank lending goes to small enterprises, compared to 35 percent
         in Morocco for instance. 14 This situation is compounded by high rates of informal borrowing, underscoring a
         reliance on informal ﬁnancial networks that is higher than in the East African and non-WAEMU West African
         region. According to the MSMEs Finance Gap Report 15, the MSME ﬁnance gap in WAEMU countries and its
         proportion to GDP stands as follows; Benin (US$1.1 billion, 9 percent of GDP), Burkina Faso (US$2.8 billion, 19

           in the availability of financing for long-term investments to sustain economic growth. Many businesses, especially
           SMEs, require longer loan terms to finance capital-intensive Projects such as equipment purchase and expansion
           plans, but the supply of such financing remains limited. The dominance of short-term loans can be attributed to
           several factors, including high perceived risk by lenders, limited access to long-term funding sources, and a lack of
           well-developed capital markets. As a result, businesses are often forced to rely on frequent refinancing, which can
           be costly and restrictive for long-term growth. Addressing this mismatch between loan maturity and business
           needs is crucial to fostering economic development in the region and unlocking the potential for sustained
           economic growth.

           on the private sector, which has grown from 22.8 percent of GDP before the pandemic to 24.7 percent as of 2022 19.
           However, the rapid growth of banks' claims on the public sector and the greater exposure to sovereign debt could
           crowd out private sector credit in the future. Furthermore, the liquidity pressures facing middle-sized banks that

Aﬀordable Housing
     21. The housing deficit in the WAEMU region is a significant challenge, exacerbated by rapid urbanization and
         population growth. The region faces a shortage of approximately 3.5 million housing units. To address this deficit,
         an estimated 800,000 new housing units need to be constructed annually just to cover the current needs 22.
         However, the current supply falls drastically short, with WAEMU banks issuing only about 15,000 new mortgages
         each year. This shortfall is particularly acute for low- and middle-income households, which struggle to access
         affordable housing due to high costs and limited financing options. The situation is further complicated by the
         high growth rates of urbanization, which exceed 3.5 percent annually, leading to increased land prices and the
         proliferation of informal settlements.

F. Lessons Learned and Reflected in the Project Design

   45. Prior Projects, including the recently closed WAEMU Affordable Housing Finance Project (P161658) as well as
       discussions with stakeholders and a review of existing reports have provided valuable lessons that have been
       reflected in the proposed Project design. These lessons include the following:
         a. Design targeting principles to ensure that financing is used for productive purposes and enhance
            additionality. Projects should also consider adding more specialized criteria to liquidity support to reach
            excluded segments of the population. For example, it is advised to use specific target or incentives to reach
            women, or climate resilience outcomes.
         b. Ensure that the Project reaches previously excluded households and bridges a financing gap. The proposed
            Project includes explicit objectives and activities to help financial institutions further reach underserved
            households in the region. The combination of housing refinancing support coupled with the guarantee
            product would help in this direction as will the support to incremental housing finance.
         c. Have a well-designed and well-funded M&E system to track impact. The proposed Project has a specific
            M&E and impact evaluation build into its design. It will allow the BOAD and the World Bank to follow
            progress and assess impact through surveys and impact evaluation of beneficiaries and a control group.
         d. Ensure that there is a link between the proposed operation and the broader financial sector reform
            agenda. The Project builds on Technical Assistance (TA) to support financial sector reform in the WAEMU
            region including financial inclusion, climate and gender finance.

  57. The Project is structured to ensure profitability for participating banks while fostering economic growth. For
      economic modelling purposes, it is assumed, based on market conditions, that the participating banks would
      provide loans at a cost of 7.2 percent, which would include a 1.1 percent margin for the participating banks.
      Mortgage loans are priced at 8.0 percent, with a 2.0 percent margin for the banks. This pricing structure, aligned
      with market conditions, ensures that banks can cover their costs and earn a reasonable profit, thereby

         approximately US$7 million for the banks, which will be subject to a corporate tax rate of 27 percent.
     58. In addition to the financial benefits for banks, the Project is expected to have a significant impact on job

         40,000 people, estimating 5 members/household. Spillover benefits would include, beyond affordability, access
         to decent and adequate housing, which meets local safety and sanitary standards. In conclusion, the Project is
         expected to deliver substantial economic benefits to the region. By providing liquidity for mortgage and MSME

B. Fiduciary

   (i) Financial Management (FM)
  61. The proposed FM arrangements, including planning, budgeting, accounting, internal controls, funds flow,
      financial reporting and external auditing are in accordance with the fiduciary requirements of the World Bank
      Operational Policies and Principles for Investment Project Financing (IPF). BOAD will be responsible for the FM
      coordination and monitoring of Project activities for Components 1 and 3, while the FM responsibilities will be
      under CRRH-UEMOA for Component 2. Both BOAD and CRRH-UEMOA will maintain FM arrangements under their
      respective activities, including (i) ensuring compliance with the Financing Agreement; (ii) obtaining funds from the
      World Bank and managing such funds in an efficient, effective, and transparent manner; (iii) providing financial
      reports and Project audit reports to the World Bank; (iv) ensuring overall management of payments and
      accounting functions of the Project; (v) managing internal and external audits and following up on audit issues;
      and (vi) responding to any other World Bank requests related to FM matters.
  62. An FM assessment was carried out by the World Bank team prior to appraisal, which reviewed the existing FM
      arrangements at BOAD and CRRH-UEMOA and covered elements of budgeting, accounting, financial reporting,
      funds flow, internal controls, and external auditing. The assessment concluded that both BOAD and CRRH-
      UEMOA have experience in managing the World Bank financed Project. For instance, CRRH-UEMOA has
      successfully hosted WAEMU Affordable Housing Finance (P161658) which closed in June 2024 while BOAD has
      been managing the Regional Off-Grid Electricity Access Project (P160708). Fiduciary compliance differs from one
      Project to another. With regard to the Regional Off-Grid Electricity Access Project, BOAD submitted the unaudited
      IFRs on time and they were found to be acceptable to the World Bank, except for the revised IFR for the semester
      ending on June 30, 2024, which was due on August 29, 2024, and was received at the World Bank with more than
      5-month delay. Although the external auditors have issued an unqualified opinion, the first audit report was
      received with a 9-month delay. The FM performance and FM risk were rated as moderately satisfactory (MS) and
      substantial respectively, following the last supervision mission carried out in November 2024. With regard to the
      WAEMU Affordable Housing Finance P161658, CRRH-UEMOA submitted the last IFR for the semester ending June
      30, 2024, which was due on August 14th, 2024, with a 2-month delay, although the IFR was found to be acceptable.
      The audit reports for the Fiscal Year ending December 31, 2023, were submitted to the World Bank on time with
      an unqualified opinion. The FM performance and FM risk were rated as moderately satisfactory and substantial

V. KEY RISKS

  72. The overall risk for the operation is currently assessed as Substantial. Political and Governance risk is assessed
      as Substantial due to the FCS status of four of the WAEMU countries (Burkina Faso, Guinea Bissau, Mali, Niger),
      the recent military coups d’etat in three of the eight WAEMU countries (Mali, Burkina Faso, Niger), the still ongoing
      transitions to civilian rule, and in a context marred by continued fragility. The exit of the three countries from
      ECOWAS heightens those risks. Additionally, three WAEMU member countries (Côte d’Ivoire, Burkina Faso, and
      Mali) are on the Financial Action Task Force (FATF) “grey list” denoting signiﬁcant weaknesses in their (AML-CFT)
      Anti Money Laundering/Counter Terrorist Financing regimes and non-compliance with the global standards. Niger
      is being actively monitored by FATF for a possible grey listing. Exit from this list depends on the completion of a
      speciﬁc action plan agreed with FATF. All three countries have such action plans under implementation. The
      approval in 2023 by the WAEMU Council of Ministers of a new AML/CFT law is also an important step, which, once
      adopted, will improve the governing framework for AML/CFT.
  73. Macro-economic risk is assessed as Substantial. As described earlier, the region’s public debt surge, coupled with
      monetary policy tightening due to inﬂationary pressures and largely limited access to international markets, means
      macro-economic risk remains substantial. Performance across countries is varied and recent indications are that
      inﬂationary pressures have come under control with countries such as CIV and Benin once again accessing
      international ﬁnancial markets, though the outlook remains uncertain with signiﬁcant downside potential. The
      recent Central Bank more accommodative policy, ﬁscal consolidation, and decline in inﬂation are mitigating this
      risk.
  74. Technical design risk is assessed as Substantial. The Project includes several activities with two key implementing
      agencies but also engaging with PFIs. While the LoC component is not a new activity for the BOAD, there is a need
      to ensure suﬃcient pipeline development, in particular, to achieve the 25 percent target on Climate Investments.
      This will be mitigated by the fact that BOAD has experience in climate ﬁnance Projects.. Furthermore, the risk of
      achieving within the Project timeframe the goal of 30 percent green mortgages to be refinanced by the CRRH-
      UEMOA is substantial given that this is a new activity in the region. This risk is offset by the fact that countries
      such as Côte d'Ivoire and Senegal have plans to build large-scale affordable green housing, as well as by the
      appetite of financial institutions for financing green assets. The Project will also schedule significant capacity
      building in this area. The regional housing guarantee product was launched in May 2024 and PFIs will require
      signiﬁcant awareness raising on its features and usage.

                                               ANNEX 2: Financial Intermediary Assessment
I.      Background Information
     1. The Project aims to promote ﬁnancial inclusion for households and MSMEs in WAEMU region by facilitating
        access to credit for households and MSMEs. To that end, the Project’s intervention will be a total US$205 million
        IDA credit (US$200 million IDA SUW and US$5 million IDA RW) for three components. The ﬁrst would be for access
        to ﬁnance to MSMEs through a BOAD administered US$140 million long-term Line of Credit (LoC), which will be
        on-lent to eligible Participating Financial Institutions (PFI) operating in WAEMU countries, which will in turn
        finance viable MSMEs. The second component would be US$60 million ﬁnancing to (1) increase the CRRH-
        UEMOA refinancing capacity of banks and MFIs to offer long term affordable housing loans and (2) provide credit
        guarantees for low-income households and households with an irregular income. While the third component of
        US$5 million (from RW) would be for Project implementation support, monitoring and evaluation.
     2. The Financial Intermediary Assessment FIF assessment applies to the BOAD for its role as a ﬁnancial
        intermediary under Component 1, the line of credit to MSMEs as well as the CRRH-UEMOA as ﬁnancial
        intermediary under Component 2. For Component 1: The line of credit will be lent to the BOAD to be on-lent to
        eligible Participating Financial Institutions (PFI) operating in WAEMU countries, which will in turn finance viable
        MSMEs, to fund their diverse financing needs, primarily for investment and networking capital. Twenty-five
        percent of the LoC will be allocated to MSMEs operating in green sectors and/or investments by MSMEs for
        adaptation and mitigation purposes. To that end, the Project will adopt IFC’s Climate Assessment for Financial
        Institutions (CAFI) green taxonomy tool 36. This component will mobilize private capital from PFIs as it requires
        that for each extended MSME loan, PFIs draw up 80 percent from the LoC and contribute 20 percent from their
        balance sheets. This co-financing feature (i) ensures an alignment of interest with PFIs; and (ii) increases the
        amount of funding to MSMEs, further increasing the impact and additionality of the Project. For Component 2:
        the line of credit will be lent to the BOAD as well and then be on-lent to the CRRH-UEMOA who will then provide
        concessional mortgage portfolio refinancing to eligible PFI in WAEMU countries. By the end of the Project 25
        percent of the LoC will be allocated for refinancing of green mortgages and 30 percent for mortgages provided to
        women. This component will mobilize private capital from the capital market by supporting CRRH-UEMOA bond
        issuances, which will then blend the bonds proceeds with the IDA credit.
     3. The BOAD will be the Project implementing entity (PIE) for Component 1. The BOAD would communicate the
        features of the Loan to PFIs and seek their interest in its utilization. It would also monitor compliance with the
        eligibility criteria. A successful eligibility assessment of the Financial Institutions and beneficiary MSMEs will be a
        precondition for the BOAD to respectively enter into a loan agreement with the eligible PFIs, and to operate the
        disbursement of funds out of the Borrower's Designated Account to the eligible PFIs wishing to benefit from the
        line of credit. The BOAD would also provide the Project operational and financial reporting as set out in the loan
        agreement.

  5. The World Bank will lend euros to BOAD on IDA SUW terms. These are the same as IBRD terms for middle-
     income countries. For Component 1 the BOAD will lend the funds to PFIs in euros, CFA francs, or other local
     currencies at interest rates sufficient to cover funding costs, administrative costs, currency and interest rate
     hedging costs, and an adequate return on capital. The PFIs will in turn provide loans in euros or the local currency
     to the beneficiary MSMEs. The PFIs will set interest rates and other terms depending on credit quality and degree
     of risk mitigation. There will be no fixed funding quota by country. Funding channeled to each country will depend
     on the demand for debt financing and readiness of commercial lenders operating in each country. Interest rate
     and currency risks will be hedged by BOAD or the PFIs depending on their relative capability, to achieve maximum
     cost-effectiveness. For Component 2, the BOAD will provide the funds to the CRRH-UEMOA in euros who will then
     lend to PFIs in CFA francs at interest rates sufficient to cover funding costs, administrative costs and currency and
     interest rate hedging costs. PFIs will determine the interest rate to MSMEs based on market conditions.
II.      Sectoral Context and Market Failure37
      6. The WAEMU region banking system has demonstrated resilience to recent global and regional challenges. Since
         2018, there has been a gradual alignment of prudential regulations with Basel II/III standards, alongside
         enhancements in banking supervision and macroprudential oversight, which have strengthened the banking
         system. However, while cyclical vulnerabilities have been managed, bank credit portfolios are still heavily
         concentrated, and there is a notable increase in exposure to sovereign risks and liquidity risks in recent years.
      7. The Banking sector dominates the financial system, with total assets of CFAF 64 trillion, representing 58 percent
         of the region's GDP by the end of 2022. The banking sector accounts for about 72 percent of the financial system,
         while other financial institutions make up around 28 percent. Out of 155 credit institutions, 132 are banks, with
         asset concentration primarily in Côte d’Ivoire and Senegal, holding 34 percent and 19 percent of assets,
         respectively. Regional banking groups control nearly 85 percent of banking sector assets, while smaller
         unaffiliated private and public banks represent the remainder. Thirteen banks with majority public ownership
         account for 10 percent of banking assets and 13 percent of banking capital in the region.
      8. Credit risk: Bank’s asset quality in the WAEMU has improved significantly in recent years but remains high.
         Bank credit growth to the private sector was robust in recent years but slowed to 10.3 percent in 2023, down

 9. Solvency risk: The region’s average bank capital adequacy ratios have improved in recent years. The average CAR
    for the WAEMU is among the lowest in Sub-Saharan Africa, standing at around 13 percent at the end of 2022.
    However, it exceeds the regulatory minimum of 11.25 percent and has trended upward since 2018. This overall
    trend masks significant disparities across countries. Profitability also lags that of peers with a return on assets
    (ROA) persistently around 1.2–1.5.
10. The WAEMU region faces significant liquidity pressures due to recent monetary policy adjustments and
    economic challenges. The BCEAO has implemented several rate hikes to combat inflation, which has tightened
    liquidity in the process. Additionally, in 2023, the BCEAO withdrew CFAF 85 billion from the market as part of its
    liquidity management strategy. As of Q4 2024, WAEMU countries aim to raise CFAF 1.38 trillion (US$2.35 billion)
    through public securities to support budgetary needs and infrastructure Projects,
11. Liquidity risk: Declining liquidity buffers in the banking sector, coupled with an underdeveloped interbank market,
    are increasing liquidity risks. By the end of 2022, liquid assets made up about 24 percent of total assets. The lack
    of liquidity in secondary markets for government securities and the high concentration of deposits hinder banks'
    capacity to manage liquidity shocks, potentially worsening liquidity risks. To counterbalance these liquidity
    constraints, the central bank utilized its marginal lending facility to inject liquidity into the system, ensuring that
    banks could meet their short-term obligations. These measures underscore the persistent liquidity tensions in the
    banking sector, driven by an increasing reliance on short-term funding and causing banks to increasingly rely on
    BCEAO financing.
12. Interest rate risk: In WAEMU, banks are increasingly exposed to interest rate risk due to a growing share of
    government securities on their balance sheets, which typically have longer maturities than the banks' funding.
    However, potential valuation losses from rising yields on sovereign securities are limited, as WAEMU banks
    generally hold these securities until maturity.
13. The rising sovereign – bank nexus and liquidity vulnerabilities will further constrain private sector credit. At the
    end of 2023, banks’ sovereign exposure amounted to 38 percent of domestic assets, up from 30 percent at the
    end of 2019. The year over year growth of bank credit to the private sector slowed from 14.2 percent in 2022 to
    10.3 percent in 2023, on tightening liquidity conditions. The rapid growth of banks' claims on the public sector
    and the greater exposure to sovereign debt is crowding out private sector credit 38.

14.      Interest Rates: as of the end of Q2-2024, the prevailing Policy rate is 5.50 percent. It gradually rose over the

15.      Directed Credit: The Project will target viable MSMEs in the WAEMU region in all economic sectors except the
standard WBG exclusion list. MSMEs are one of the strongest drivers of economic development, innovation and
employment. Access to finance is identified as a critical barrier to growth for MSMEs is critically constrained. Creating
opportunities for MMSMEs in the region is key to advancing economic development and reducing poverty.

16.      BOAD will be the sole recipient of the IDA SUW Loan and act as the apex Financial Insitution for the Financial
Inclusion and MSME Finance in the WAEMU Project. The Project Team has carried out initial due diligence on BOAD
and found that BOAD satisfies all the requirements of Investment Project Financing Policy and Directive.
17.      The risks associated with lending to BOAD are relatively low. BOAD has a strong financial position to be the
recipient of the IDA SUF credit. It never had any default since its creation in 1973. As of 2024, BOAD is rated BBB by
Fitch and Baa1 by Moody’s—both investment grade ratings. which is much higher than any individual sovereign in
WAEMU (Senegal and Côte d’Ivoire are respectively rated Ba3 and Ba2 by Moody’s and B+ and BB- by S&P). The
rating is underpinned by its strong liquidity, access to the refinancing window of BCEAO, the Central Bank of the
WAEMU region, and the strength of member shareholders’ support. BCEAO owns 47 percent of BOAD’s capital and
the eight WAEMU countries another 47 percent, while non-regional shareholders led by France hold 6 percent. In the
event of a default, the shareholders of BOAD would be liable. There is a robust mechanism to ensure that capital
payments from shareholders remain timely: BCEAO collects a tax on financial transactions on behalf of member
countries, which is placed in their accounts and is used to make payments to BOAD in priority before the country can
even access these funds. According to Moody’s, the amount of tax collected by BCEAO every year more than covers
the yearly capital payments to BOAD. BOAD has a professional management team and strong governance. 40
18.      Regarding the CRRH-UEMOA, the Bloomfield rating agency renewed in 2024 the financial rating of CRRH-
UEMOA, which retained its AA+ rating for the 7th consecutive year. This rating is the best obtained to date by an
issuing institution on the regional financial market. It attests to the quality of the signature of CRRH-UEMOA, which
is one of the highest-rated financial institutions in WAEMU. In addition, it placed CRRH-UEMOA under positive
outlook, leaving the possibility of an upgrade of its financial rating to AAA, Bloomfield's best rating.
19.     BOAD and CRRH-UEMOA have a positive prior track record of involvement in World Bank-financed Projects.
The World Bank-financed Project Affordable Housing Finance for WAEMU (P161658) approved by the Board in
September 2017 was financed by an IDA SUF credit of EUR 116 million (US$130 million equivalent) to BOAD. Similarly,
the Regional Off-Grid Electrification Project (P160708) approved by the Board in April 2019 was also financed by an
IDA SUF credit of EUR 122 million (US$140 million equivalent) to BOAD.

submitted to the World Bank within 45 days after the end of the semiannual period. Internal auditors will also be
