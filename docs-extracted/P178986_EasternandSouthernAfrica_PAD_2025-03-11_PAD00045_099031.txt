 I. STRATEGIC CONTEXT

1. Housing is a foundational socio-economic element for individuals and communities and is consistently proven to
drive economic growth. 1 The sector is recognized as having a significant potential impact on economies through job
creation, enhanced productivity, and economic inclusion. This, in turn, can elevate living standards and contribute to
poverty alleviation. Private sector involvement is crucial in harnessing the sector’s potential to deliver positive multiplier
effects through forward linkages to the financial markets and significant backward linkages to small and medium
enterprises, as well as employment generation using local labor in the provision of building materials, technologies, and
related services. Housing is also a sector deeply affected by climate-fueled disasters. 2 Poor site choices, design, and
construction materials often fail to offer sufficient protection to vulnerable populations. Also, the building and
construction sector accounts for 37 percent of global energy-related carbon dioxide (CO2) emissions. 3 This confluence
demands a strong call to action to embed climate change mitigation and adaptation measures in the sector.

2. The Africa Green, Resilient and Inclusive Housing De-risking (GRIHD) Facility is an innovative “mobilizer” of
climate finance to drive this change in the housing value chain of Eastern and Southern Africa (AFE) region. The Facility
promotes greater climate resilience and resource efficiency outcomes via scalable and replicable financial instruments
designed to meet private sector concerns. Its interventions in the housing value chain are a transversal feature of four of
the six Global Challenge Programs (GCP) by: (i) incentivizing flood risk and disaster risk reduction; (ii) scaling up energy
efficiency and increasing renewable energy integration; (iii) encouraging the use of technology to enable climate-friendly
solutions such as smart building sensors to reduce energy output; and, (iv) taking an inclusive approach to support private
sector-led investments with a focus on small businesses, particularly those led/owned by women.

3. The first Series of Project (SOP) will make a substantial contribution to private capital mobilization by stretching
the IDA financing envelope to crowd-in US$440 million through de-risking products that spark co-financing
partnerships with the private and financial sector. It will help generate nearly 55,150 employment opportunities, both
direct and indirect, demonstrating the powerful correlation between jobs and the housing industry. To maximize climate
results and co-benefits to the beneficiaries, it will condition access to its de-risking products to value chain activities that
directly target green and/or climate resilient outcomes. The GRIHD Facility further exemplifies the vision of the World
Bank Evolution. It promotes collaboration and partnership among all parts of the World Bank, including IDA, IFC, and
MIGA, in order to achieve faster and more impactful results. It is also creating opportunities for strategic partners such
as Shelter Afrique Development Bank (ShafDB) and ZEP-RE (PTA Reinsurance Company) who have expressed interest to
partner on the GRIHD platform. Rather than having a displacing effect, the Facility will be opening markets for and
working alongside such key industry players toward impact.

 A. Regional Context

4. Growth in the AFE region is being stifled by a series of homegrown factors, including monetary and fiscal policy
tightness in response to high inflation and fiscal unsustainability, high risk of debt distress and rising conflict and violence.
This is aggravated by a string of global shocks, a slowdown in the global economy, and tight global financial conditions.
While a recovery in global output and trade, lower global inflation, and gradual easing of global financial conditions may

5. AFE is experiencing widespread impacts from climate change, which is posing threats to not only its natural

6. Across countries in AFE, the supply of housing fails to meet the increasing demands, limiting opportunities for
homeownership and decent rental options, perpetuating inequality, and social exclusion of vulnerable populations
(Table 1). Demand is soaring for housing that can (i) provide a basic quality of life with access to infrastructure and services,

from worsening impacts of climate change and other environmental threats.

7. While the region has contributed marginally to global energy-related CO2 emissions, addressing such extensive
housing needs via ambitious national housing programs – like Kenya's 250,000 units annually, Zambia's 130,000 affordable
homes in Lusaka and Mozambique’s 5 year “Habita Moçambique” program – will escalate demand for materials, increasing
embedded carbon emissions and negative environmental impacts, as well as energy demand for cooling and water. The
emerging innovations along with the rising political commitment and general public awareness on climate risks offer
unprecedented opportunities to collaborate and coordinate efforts at the sub-national, national, and regional levels, and
among public and private sector stakeholders as well as donor communities.

B. Sectoral and Institutional Context

8. The housing sector confronts a myriad of supply- and demand-side challenges, and a lack of integration and
coordination. By dissecting its value chain, per Figure 1, the relationship between the public and private sector becomes
clearer. Certain upstream ingredients of the housing delivery process remain in the authority of governments and are
difficult to devolve e.g. development of planning, zoning, and building standards and regulations; and unlocking public
land (tenure systems, title and lien registration, institutional arrangements for land administration, etc.).

Commercial banks are averse to financing longer-term productive loans due to macroeconomic instabilities, asset–liability
mismatch and transactional uncertainties, while capital markets remain underdeveloped. Institutional investors that hold
the necessary local currency resources at scale (such as pension funds, sovereign wealth funds, and insurance companies)
are traditionally risk-averse and require appropriate credit enhancements. These obstacles dramatically limit lending for
housing in most AFE countries. For example, while Kenya’s banking sector had total net domestic credit of US$36 billion
in 2023, only US$2.7 billion (7.5 percent) is allocated to the real estate sector. 5

11. Moreover, housing projects in AFE are often perceived as high risk and conventional investors or lenders are
reluctant to invest. This is especially true for programs aiming to incorporate green and climate- or disaster-resilient
features through new technologies and materials, which are seen as introducing additional layers of risk to already
"unbankable" transactions. Investment propositions often fail to close due to unbalanced risk-return profiles, creating
viability gaps that need to be filled by instruments that reduce, transfer and/or compensate for risk (i.e., “de-risk”).

12. Market enabling actions by governments are critical to nurturing innovation and creating a conducive environment
for private investment in greening the sector. Policies and regulations on green, resilient building materials (e.g., tax
incentives and import tariffs) impact their availability and prices and determine standards for use. Government decisions
on infrastructure investment through land use planning, priority setting, and budget allocations are also key in determining
the availability of serviced land for housing development, as well as the cost of services. Adopting a regional approach in
coordinating these efforts can be more effective in establishing the value proposition via scale.

13. Without addressing these challenges and creating business-friendly environments, private capital will continue
retreating and the housing market will not be able to drive large-scale job creation for the semi- and low-skilled youth
in AFE. Extending beyond the direct number of jobs generated through housing construction (about 5 new jobs per unit),
a focus on the value chain will deliver multiplier effects across ancillary and other mainstream industries such as forestry,
aggregates, building materials, ceramics, household goods, etc. Creating a vibrant housing and construction sector will
enable local industry to scale up and produce materials which are currently imported, reducing transportation costs, and
generating environmental benefits. The job impact can be further enhanced in future series of the project through national
initiatives for training local workers in construction skills to develop a modern efficient housing delivery sector and
improve the quality of jobs created. Experience from numerous countries points to construction sector having among the
highest multipliers for direct and indirect jobs, as well as for overall economic stimulus.

14. In gender terms, women are unequally represented in the demand for housing, are largely excluded in the housing
supply market, face limited financial inclusion, and are disproportionally hard hit by climate change. Women in Africa
are 14 times more likely than men to die during a climate disaster and constitute 80 percent of those displaced. 6 The
gender gap in the sector is evident across the value chain from access to land ownership or title (only 19 percent of women
in Zambia hold land title, while it is only 6 percent in Kenya), to home-ownership where only 13 percent of women in
Kenya are sole homeowners, compared to 39 percent of men, although the proportion of female-headed households is
23 percent. 7 In Kenya, women make up 3 percent of the construction workforce with only 15 percent registered as
contractors whereas in Mozambique and Tanzania, 0.1 percent women participate in the construction industry. These
disparities are a result partly of gaps in access to finance which constrains women’s ability to be productive.

sensitivity, is needed. The GRIHD Facility heeds this call by adopting a value chain approach that encompasses reducing

C. Relevance to Higher Level Objectives
16. The design of the GRIHD Facility SOP reflects the evolution of the World Bank to deliver solutions and impact at
scale. It integrates key elements of the new vision expressed in the World Bank’s Evolution: (i) mobilization of private
capital and climate financing through innovative instruments and strategic deployment of concessional financing; and (ii)
fully leveraging the potential of World Bank institutions while harnessing partnerships and recognizing that no single
institution can meet the financing needs of the dual housing and climate crises. The Facility will form an important delivery
tool for four of the six GCPs i.e. (i) Fast-track Water Security and Climate Adaptation; (ii) Energy Transition Efficiency and
Access; (iii) Accelerating Digitalization; and (iv) Forests for Development, Climate, and Biodiversity. It is also strongly

17. Drawing on the private sector as an engine of integration is a key action to achieving these goals. The Facility will

(JET) through labor-intensive programs that generate more direct and indirect jobs in the housing industry; and addressing
gender gaps in the housing value chain by identifying opportunities for supplier and contractor diversity as well as
promoting inclusion in construction and end-user finance.

Enabling the One World Bank approach
20. The GRIHD Facility advocates for the aggregation of the collective muscle of the One World Bank approach, as well
as other strategic partnerships, to mobilize co-financing from the financial sector (Box 1). The project recognizes that no
single institution can meet the financing needs of the dual housing and climate crises. Additionally, provision of inclusive
housing requires long-term commitment, support, and championing by governments to foster a conducive environment
for private sector investment and delivery. The GRIHD SOP design incorporates this market-enabling role of the public
sector by targeting policy dialogue, legal/regulatory reforms, and future capitalization of the Facility through government
contributions to improve the overall housing ecosystem. The Facility will in this regard create opportunities and open
markets for the private sector, IFC and MIGA included.

                                               Box 1: Why a “One World Bank” Approach?
    Coordination and synergies: No single institution can meet the complex technical challenges and financing needs of the

    Partnership with the IFC: The Facility is informed by substantive and frequent technical discussions with the IFC, and is

B. Project Components

Vision and Approach

24. The GRIHD Facility will serve as a catalyst to mobilizing private investments in climate solutions through a highly
efficient use of limited public resources. An integrated regional approach is proposed to capture the positive externalities
that will result from the growth of green, resilient, and inclusive housing markets in AFE. Through a regional platform,
markets can: augment economies of scale, effectively pool and share risks, and promote efficiency gains through
specialization, cross-border trade, and knowledge sharing (Box 2). Policy harmonization and improved regulatory
environments as well as accompanying capacity enhancement on climate intelligence by the ecosystem stakeholders,
including the public sector and home buyers or renters, will help ensure that results are sustainable.


                                                   Box 2: Why a regional approach?
    Financial market integration: While demand for climate-smart housing is growing, the pipeline of viable deals is limited
     and within small and fragmented financial (and capital) markets to attract long-term financing. The scale at which the
     proposed Facility can foster a collective pipeline will provide cross-benefits and reduce operational costs, particularly for
     sub-projects that otherwise would be too small. Financial market integration will happen through adopting a pooled
     portfolio approach with harmonized policies, standardized procedures, investment instruments and replicable
     arrangements. Standardization across these elements also enables small investors/markets to engage at lower costs
     and large investors to achieve the scale necessary to make the engagement worthwhile.
    Cross-border trade and knowledge exchange: Considering the mass housing construction plans in the region, the
     Facility seeks to stimulate the value chain through supply of eco-friendly building materials to accelerate trade among
     the pilot jurisdictions in AFE. By supporting the creation of regional markets for manufacturers and suppliers of climate-
     smart technologies, materials and construction inputs, the proposed operation will boost intra-AFE trade and market
     integration while promoting regional innovation and exchanges of knowledge. A true African Continental Free Trade
     Area (AfCFTA) effort in action that will promote economic transformation by trade in goods and services across borders
     in the AFE region.
    Management of cross-border climate risk by supporting the creation of enabling legislation and establishment of
     industry and market standards for a green and resilient housing market that local financial institutions and borrowers
     can easily use.

27. In recognition of the need for the Facility to take a measured approach in rolling out its operations, a phased
implementation of the value chain windows, and the financial de-risking products is recommended. Based on a detailed
pipeline assessment informed heavily by consultations with prospective Primary Financial Institutions (PFIs) and value
chain actors, Windows 1 and 3 of the Facility will be launched first. The rationale and plan for the remaining windows is
as follows:

     a) Window 2: While the provision of housing infrastructure and services is traditionally seen as a government
        responsibility, the scale and complexity of the current housing needs make private sector participation,
        partnership, and investment essential. However, engagement with governments is necessary to effectively
        support private sector participation in the provision of serviced land. In this regard, SOP 1 will focus its efforts on
        initiating and deepening public-private dialogue to strengthen the policy, legal, regulatory, and institutional
        framework and to assess the quality of potential pipelines with the private sector for implementation in SOP 2.

     b) Window 4: This Window is crucial as it reflects the actual absorption of housing stock into the market, indicating
        the successful matching of supply with the effective demand from homebuyers or renters. The need to phase the
        activation of this window reflects lessons learned from other World Bank interventions in the AFE region (such as
        the mortgage refinancing companies in Kenya and Tanzania), where building an effective pipeline for home loans
        implies a corresponding development of housing supply.

28. SOP 2 activities will build upon the foundations of SOP 1 i.e., its regional platform and evidence and learnings
gathered from its investments and partnerships to increase its reach. Options include: (i) expanding the Facility to cater

the value chain coverage; (iii) attracting additional co-investors to support a scale-up approach, particularly from
sovereigns who will also help to address enabling environment concerns as well as unlocking of unencumbered land which

 39.      There remains high liquidity in the financial markets of the potential pilot countries in the form of deposits,
 treasury bills, and bonds. 19 Informed by market intel, the three main risks holding back the release of this liquidity in the
 sector are: i) Credit Risk - the risk that a lender may not receive the owed principal and/or interest, in accordance with
 the agreed terms, which results in an interruption of cash flows, default and increased costs for collection; ii) Foreign
 Exchange (FX) Risk - the risk that an investment's value may decrease due to unfavorable changes in the relative value of
 the involved currencies e.g. foreign currency debt to finance projects whose earnings are mostly in local currency; and
 iii) Political Risk - the risk that an investment's returns could suffer as a result of adverse political decisions, events, or
 conditions in a country.

 40.      The Facility will improve the creditworthiness of underlying green and/or resilient investments by covering the
 Credit and FX risks only under SOP 1 (Table 7), thereby improving their financial profile and stimulating capital release.

 E. Rationale for Bank Involvement and Role of Partners

49.      World Bank interventions are most effective when aiming to generate greater scalability (e.g., via investment
platforms, regional cooperation, adopting pooled portfolio approaches to aggregate projects and diversify risk),
standardization (e.g., common procedures and instruments), and test innovative approaches for replication by the
market. The proposed Facility embodies these principles in its ambitions to mobilize climate finance. While this kind of
transformative initiative is a necessity to incentive change, and has a high development impact, it also has the highest risk
of loss. With World Bank support and market credibility, DFIs such as TDB gain courage and comfort in adopting this new
model to drive forward such a long-term initiative and deliver sustainable development outcomes.

F. Lessons Learned and Reflected in the Project Design

51. Several practical and valuable lessons have been acquired, including directly from the AFE subregion, that have
been incorporated into the design process of the Facility:
     a) Value chain approach: Success in the housing industry depends on a wide array of activities and sectors that
        sometimes extend beyond the housing sector’s reach or mandate. Together they comprise the housing value
        chain; a chain that is as strong as its weakest link. Assessing and integrating interventions along this chain must
        be more strategic and systematic. For the AFE region, a renewed focus on the supply side to unlock structural
        bottlenecks for the private and financial sector is fundamental.
     b) Economies of Scale through a regional financing and technical platform: Effective support to the 26 AFE countries
        through individual housing finance projects is challenging. The pipeline of viable climate-smart housing
        transactions is limited and within small and fragmented financial (and capital) markets that are not suitable to
        meet long-term finance needs. Scale is key to attract interest from the private sector and financial institutions. A
        regional perspective and approach will foster collaboration, knowledge-sharing between geographies, and build
        markets and industries.
     c) De-Risking Needs: Mobilizing private capital is underpinned by risk/reward tradeoffs. The Facility would
        strategically help mitigate perceived investment losses in the housing value chain, a key factor that has held back
        private capital - thereby redefining the risk-return equation for financial institutions and investors.

52. The GRIHD Facility complements and learns from ongoing World Bank initiatives related to green, resilient, and
inclusive housing in specific AFE countries such as the Kenya Affordable Housing Finance Project (P165034), Kenya
Upscaling and De-Risking Private Investment in Affordable Green Housing Supply (TF073813), Mozambique Mais
Oportunidades Project (P178658), and Tanzania Cities Transforming Infrastructure & Competitiveness Project (P171189).

C. Sustainability
61. The project benefits from strong ownership and commitment from TDB, who has progressively built a robust
sustainability framework guiding all of its operations including a Climate Finance Strategy of 2023. On financial
sustainability, the proposed Facility is designed to operate on a commercial basis, generating appropriate returns on
investment that will enable funds to be recycled. A number of cost-recovery strategies and mechanisms have been
considered: (i) adopting a revolving structure (plough back net profits); (ii) being cost-efficient by leveraging internal TDB
back-office support resources (i.e. Treasury, Finance, E&S, Human Resource, IT, Procurement, etc.); and, (iii) drawing
revenue from key sources such as net interest income from de-risking operations, investment income from
placements/deposits as well as fees from the unfunded products. Additionally, the Facility will drive sustainability through
a multifaceted risk-sensitive implementation approach. For the economic and financial assessments, rigorous scenario
modelling has informed planning for changing conditions with contingency plans in place to ensure structured resilience.

issuance.

D. Environmental and Social
84. The environmental risk is rated High. The Project is designed to support green and resilient housing value chain
solutions, which is not only complex on its own, but is also at embryonic stage in AFE region in which Facility is planned to
be implemented. At this stage, the location of the sub projects and the details of the PFIs TDB will finance are unknown.
Under SOP 1, TDB through PFIs will finance the supply, manufaturing and assembly of small, medium to large scale green

construction materials and renewable energy and water technologies, as well as housing development (construction or
renovation) projects. Construction activities associated with the subprojects, and the operation of manufacturing facilities,
usually generates E&S risks and impacts with respect to: environmental pollution; occupational health & safety (OHS);
efficient use of resources such as water and energy; the degradation of natural resources especially water bodies and soil
from mining of raw materials for building material; community health and safety, road safety, emergency preparedness;
biodiversity loss from land conversion from sourcing of raw material; existing environmental damage at construction sites
and off-site- soil and water contamination, etc.; transport related EHS risks; potential EHS issues with house rehabilitation,
related to environmental liabilities (such as asbestos, lead based paint, on-site wastewater disposal, etc.) and potential
financing of housing in areas that don't have the appropriate support infrastructure (thus indirect EHS impacts/risks) such
as water supply, wastewater collection and disposal, roads, energy supply, etc. Some of the construction phase impacts
especially those related to OHS, environmental pollution, waste management, resource use, road safety, emergency
preparedness, etc. will be witnessed during the operational phase of Windows 1 and 3.

85. The social risk is rated High. This is because the location of activities, details of the PFIs and nature and size of financing
are still unknown and because of the potential social impacts of proposed activities. Under SOP 1, the focus is expected
to be on the supply, manufaturing and assembly of medium to large scale green construction materials and renewable
energy and water technologies, as well as housing development (construction or renovation) projects that are likely to
result in involuntary resettlement impacts, complex labor management issues, and may be in areas where IPs/SSAHUTLCs
are present, areas with a high prevalence of Gender Based Violence and Sexual Exploitation (GBV/SEA) which can be
exacerbated by the project, areas affected by conflict or violence, or areas hosting refugees. Some of the other risks
include inadequate stakeholder engagement, child and forced labor, and elite capture.

86. TDB has established ESG structures and ESMS systems that will support Facility implementation. TDB's E&S Policies
are well-crafted, reflecting institutional commitment to integrate E&S sustainability across operations, complete with an
exclusion list. The policy aligns with IFC/WB Performance Standards. The existing ESMS includes E&S policy, categorization
procedures, screening, due diligence, monitoring, and communication mechanisms. TDB subjects its ESMS to periodic
reviews performed by external consultants to ensure it continues to be in line with international best practice. The last
ESMS review was completed in 2024, where TDB made changes in its ESMS to integrate step-by step guidelines to assist
in implementing the ESMS, bench-marking E&S policy to international practice, standardization of tools and templates;
clarification of roles and responsibility, provision of grievance mechanism; considering the new responsible finance trends
and tools, and E&S requirements for FIs. Through the Bank financed RIFF project, TDB further enhanced its E&S, GBV,
labor and climate management capacity. It has also developed a Guidance Note on E&S risk management in its lending
through FIs.

E. Gender
87. The GRIHD SOP program is committed to employing interventions to empower women to make meaningful
contributions to the housing value chain in AFE. The gender gap in access to finance is cross-cutting, affecting both land
ownership and title, participation in the supply chain and construction workforce, and homeownership. Although gaps
remain in terms of the sex-disaggregation of financial inclusion data specifically in the housing sector, there is still progress
to be made overall in narrowing the gender gap in access to finance, particularly when it comes to usage of financial
products. Under subcomponent 1.2 of SOP 1, the Facility will incorporate a gender-responsive approach to increase access
to finance for women-owned SMEs to improve their productivity in the manufacturing, assembly and supply of
construction materials and technologies, and in the housing construction and development industry. This would include
facilitating the collection and monitoring of sex-disaggregated data to help identify any sector specific gender disparities
in access, usage, and outcomes of financial products, better understand the impact and effectiveness of the de-risking
products, which would enable PFIs make informed decisions. The Facility will utilize the collected data to design/tailor a

will support stakeholder engagements to build capacity for gender integration in the housing value chain of TDB member
states, including the development of a targeted learning curriculum. This involves targeted training to enhance active
participation of women in green and resilient housing initiatives. Further details are provided in Annex 6 on Gender

F. Citizen Engagement
88. Design and implementation modalities of the housing delivery process inherently rely on extensive citizen

intervention’s effectiveness and sustainability.

                                ANNEX 1: Implementation Arrangements and Support Plan

Strategy and Approach for Implementation Arrangements.

1. The GRIHD Facility will be established as a ring-fenced SPV within TDB as an international body corporate enjoying
privileges and immunities as other TDB Group international entities. As a separate self-governing legal entity, the Facility
will have its own constitutive documents. Like TDB’s other international entities, the entity will be self-regulating. Given
its off-balance sheet nature, the Facility’s risks will be isolated from the TDB Group, ensuring its independent decision-
making and clear traceability of its activities.

2. The governance and operational framework is designed to ensure that sufficient oversight is provided over the
management of the Facility and that the interests of all shareholders are adequately represented. The proposed structure
is sufficiently robust to accommodate new investors who may be onboarded in the future. Figure 6 outlines the Facility’s
governance structure:

investment process for each transaction at the apex level, a one-off PFI vetting process will be carried out – aligned with

                                  ANNEX 2: Financial Intermediary Financing (FIF) Review

1. Introduction. The GRIHD Facility project has been designed to meet all FIF requirements and incorporate international
good practices, with a private sector-led approach to transform the housing sector and redefine the financial sector’s risk
concerns for such lending. Financial institutions in target countries are holding high liquidity and seeking new investment
opportunities. Market consultations found that de-risking through junior debt and guarantees would be highly attractive
for green, resilient, and inclusive housing lending. The project in SOP 1 will only support private sector-led investments.

2. This operation represents the World Bank’s largest effort on green, resilient, and inclusive housing in the AFE region
and will require coordination between the TDB SPV and private financial institutions, as well as donors and regulators. The
implementing agency is the Eastern and Southern African Trade and Development Bank (TDB), a partner of the WB in the
Regional Infrastructure Financing Facility (P171967) and the Accelerating Sustainable and Clean Energy Access
Transformation in Eastern and Southern Africa (ASCENT) (P180547) supporting distributed renewable energy in the region.

3. Macroeconomic and Fiscal environment. TDB, via the SPV, will select investment opportunities using the country
prioritization filter, which assesses a country’s macroeconomic situation. Investments will be in countries where the
macroeconomic environment supports the viability of the eligible sub-projects. In countries where fiscal issues such as
high debt distress are evident, the project will avoid any public sector investments that could worsen the situation. In
countries with hyperinflation (where annual inflation rate is over 25 percent for at least 18 months), the investments may
be postponed until more favorable macroeconomic conditions are in place. Since SOP 1 will only support eligible sub-
projects by private commercial enterprises, there is no effect on a country’s debt level or contingent liabilities.

4. Financial sector. As part of the country prioritization filter, the TDB SPV will ensure that the national financial sector is
supportive of medium to long term lending. This review will include interest rate policies (such as caps), Treasury bill and
bond offers, any “crowding out” of productive lending by such government fiscal requirements, and any subsidized
housing programs. A review of financial sectors in Kenya, Mozambique, Tanzania, and Zambia found a pool of sound
financial institutions that are interested in SPV support through market priced junior debt and fee-based guarantees for
GRIHD housing finance.

5. Implementation arrangements. TDB will operationalize a regional platform managed by a commercially oriented special
purpose vehicle (SPV) for private sector-led interventions in the green, resilient, and inclusive housing value chains of
select countries in the region. It will offer back-office support services for a fee and training services per an MOU with the
legally established SPV.

6. Component 1 of the project includes (i) establishing, capitalizing, and operationalizing the Facility (Component 1.1 of
US$25 million, using PBCs against verified targets) and (ii) providing de-risking products for green, resilient, and inclusive
housing value chains (subcomponent 1.2 of US$70 million). Component 2 provides technical assistance for TDB,
participating financial institutions (PFIs) and public sector stakeholders (US$5 million). Given the size of TDB (US$8.6 billion
in assets in June 2023), and its proven ability to provide innovative lending in the subregion, there is negligible risk that
TDB will encounter difficulties managing current and planned commitments. These new activities fit well into the existing
TDB mission and its objective of increasing project finance in innovative fields.

7. The SPV will be the apex institution, providing de-risking products (junior debt, fee-based guarantees) to boost PCM
from PFIs accredited through a stringent due diligence process using CAMELS and international good practice standards

10. TDB risk management and supervision. As a supranational financial institution, TDB does not come under the
jurisdiction of any national supervisory body. TDB has diversified exposure concentration risks by financing activities to 22
sovereigns and many non-sovereign clients. TDB’s active risk management approach covers portfolio risk, currency risk,
maturity mismatch risk, environmental and social risk, and concentration of exposure risk. TDB has taken four actions to
ensure sound governance – (i) a detailed credit risk management system with project level analysis of financial,
environmental, and social risks, (ii) use of Basel international standards, (iii) annual external audits, and (iv) outsourced
internal audit function (Price Waterhouse Coopers, reporting to the Board) and external audit is conducted by Deloitte.

11. Financial Products. The following pricing and credit risk principles will apply: (i) a commercial orientation (for interest
rates and fees), (ii) a transparent approach to blended finance, (iii) clear assignment of credit risk (shared with PFIs through
de-risking products), and (iv) active portfolio monitoring, loan contract enforcement, and early treatment of issues.
Foreign exchange risk is the greatest challenge to sustainable project finance in the region. It is far too expensive to use
traditional hedging products. Although they may be useful for junior debt lending, the contingent nature of guarantees
makes traditional hedging ineffective. The foreign exchange risk will be addressed initially by a project-support dedicated
reserve account, and subsequently with increases in the reserve account based on income generated from junior debt
interest income and guarantee fee income. Guarantees will cover up to 50 percent of transaction value, with fees for
administrative costs. It will be a first loss facility. This may be adjusted per market demand and operational experience.
Local currency bonds will be considered as a secondary means of mobilizing local currency financing in markets in targeted
countries in the late stage of SOP1 or for SOP2. This will be patterned after successful local currency bond issuances for
infrastructure by InfraCredit in West Africa, and for mortgages by KMRC in Kenya. This may be used in markets with strict
foreign exchange control policies.

12. Interest rates and maturities. The interest rate for junior debt from the SPV to PFIs will cover the weighted average
cost of funds, operational expenses, loan loss provision and a small profit margin. Any cost savings from blended finance
will be passed on to end borrowers. PFIs will be able to price the loans to sub-borrowers per national market costs and
risks but will be required to share both the nominal and effective interest rate with sub-borrowers and identify any
fees. TDB’s operational expenses as a share of revenues was 17 percent for trade finance (mostly with PFIs) and 8 percent
for project finance (mostly with sovereigns). Using blended finance and equity partnerships (such as with Shelter Afrique
and ZEP-RE), the SPV costs for tailored products should be lower than any market alternatives for green, resilient housing
investments.

13. In those exceptional cases when the SPV lends directly to firms, the interest rate will cover the SPV weighted average
cost of funds, operational expenses, loan loss provision and a profit margin. The interest rate will be based on a formula,
linking the base rate to a reference rate set by TDB, and reflecting the SOFR rate plus a mark-up. (SOFR is the secured
overnight financing rate, which replaced LIBOR in mid-2023). It is based on transactions on the United States Treasury
repurchase market and is used internationally for dollar-denominated loans. Currently, for direct lending, TDB prices loans
at 3-month SOFR plus a mark-up of 6.5 to 8 percent. The mark-up is based on the borrower’s characteristics and the nature
of the41rojectt, as well as the maturity and collateral provided. The rate to PFIs for SPV’s apex lending for the products is
expected to be in this range as well, using a positive yield curve.

14. The maturity will be determined by the scale, cashflow, and other characteristics of the investment. There is often a
grace period of one to two years to allow for the generation of sufficient cashflow to cover operating expenses as well as
loan repayments. The specific grace period in each case will be determined by the PFI, since it takes the credit risk and
must enforce the loan contract.

15. Indicators and Monitoring. The indicators will include level of financial activity (number of PFIs, number of investments
per window), the quality of the portfolio (PAR90 or NPLs), the number of SPV investors and private capital
mobilized. Indicators will include portfolio quality (NPLs), the volume of lending, the amount of PCM verified, and the
number of PFIs using junior debt or guarantees from the SPV.

16. Investment due diligence. The SPV will use the existing TDB five-step due diligence process for investments (involving
risk and compliance, operations, credit risk, legal and transaction advisors). Given the credit review process already in
place for project finance, the GRIHD project meets the requirement for “specific purpose lending” under ESS9, and a
transaction-based portfolio under Paris alignment for financial intermediation. The PFIs will use their existing due diligence
systems, which will be reviewed by TDB as part of the PFI due diligence process. The World Bank will have a “no objection”
to PFI selection.

17. Coordination with the IFC and MIGA. The project has been designed in consultation with both the IFC and MIGA. It is
expected that commercial financial institutions with relationships with the IFC will be interested in access to SPV lending
and de-risking products for the green housing sector. Those institutions will be subject to the same due diligence review
as other financial institutions. The IFC is providing EDGE and BRI tools to support the quality of outputs supported under
the project. MIGA has expressed indicative interest in active participation with its menu of insurance and de-risking
products.

                                         ANNEX 4: Economic and Financial Analysis

Economic Analysis
The economic viability of the proposed project was assessed using a standard cost-benefit methodology. Economic costs
were estimated based on the IDA funding that is deployed by the Facility and the private capital mobilized due to de-
risking instruments. The following are the assumptions and costs.

Financing and deployment assumptions
• The IDA financing to be deployed is estimated at US$95 million with a drawdown based on the market demand from
    the projected pipeline. The model assumes 25 percent of the funds are deployed on funded products and the balance
    towards unfunded instruments.
• Based on industry practices, the funded products are estimated to crowd in private capital of 1.85 times and 5.63
    times for fee-based unfunded products.

Assumptions for calculating economic benefits.
• The cumulative funding i.e. IDA and private financing will lead to increased economic activity in the housing value
   chain in the form of direct and indirect employment. As per published research by the IMF, manufacturing generates
   11 direct jobs, and housing development 100 direct jobs for every US$1 million invested. The resulting indirect jobs
   would be about 3.5 times the direct jobs. A wage of US$1,685 has been considered for each job created based on the
   weighted average GDP per capita for the potential pilot countries, and deployment of products.
• The IDA funds and equity investment in the Facility are incremental funds, an economic value of 30 percent has been
   considered as value add due to this funding. The assumption is based on value added to the economy trends in Sub-
   Saharan Africa. Similarly, an economic value add has been considered on the net profit earned by the GRIHD facility
   from sustainable operations post the deployment of IDA funding.
• Funding will deliver climate resilient housing that provides a cost to benefit ratio of 1:4 for investing in resilience which
   covers aspects such as lives saved, avoided cost of displacement, and finding alternative housing, disruption to
   economic activity.
• The housing units constructed will be energy efficient, water efficient and will provide economic benefits through
   annual cost savings in building operations costs.
• The project is also expected to have several additional benefits which are either uncertain or difficult to quantify. As
   such, the results of the economic analysis can be considered a conservative estimation of the total economic benefits
   for society.
• The economic assessment assumes only one cycle of deployment of funding from the Facility, however funding will
   be recycled at least three times during the tenure of the IDA loan. Further, GRIHD SPV is expected to raise capital from
   the markets in the form of debt and equity for increasing its activities after the completion of SOP1. These upside
   benefits have not been considered in the economic estimation for conservatism.

Summary: The economic benefits calculations indicate a baseline NPV of US$37 million (at discount rate equal to 5
percent) with an economic cost to benefit ratio of 1.41 times.

                                         ANNEX 5: Gender Analysis and Actions

Gender Analysis
The gender gap in the housing value chain multifaceted; however, the gap in access to finance is a cross-cutting issue
which constrains women’s ability to invest in housing assets.

While the gender disparity in financial inclusion is narrowing in some AFE countries, there is still some progress to be
made, especially in the actual usage of financial accounts. For instance, even though Kenya ranks highly in financial
inclusion of women (81.7 percent), a housing survey revealed that though demand is high for financial products
(specifically to facilitate homeownership), the ability to take advantage of these opportunities are only available to a
small and exclusive group of women (around 20 percent of women belonging to high-income groups bought houses via
mortgages, banks, or loans from their employers). In Mozambique, around 8 percent of the female population has
access to credit via banks, 4 percent via informal sources and 3 percent via family and friends, compared to Tanzania
where 1.5 percent of the female population has access to credit via banks, 8 percent via non-bank formal sources, 28
percent via informal sources and 26 percent via family and friends. By intentionally addressing these barriers that
women face and ensuring equal access to finance, women are able to enhance their productivity and economic
opportunities.

Gender Action
Given the low levels of financial inclusion in the housing industry, the GRIHD Facility SOP 1 project provides an
opportunity for the AFE region to integrate targeted approaches to narrow gaps in access to finance for female owned
MSMEs and employ interventions to empower women to make meaningful contributions to the housing value chain.

The project will achieve this under subcomponent 1.2: De-Risking Products for Green, Resilient and Inclusive Housing
Value Chains, and Component 2: Technical Assistance through:

         o   Developing a framework for the collection and monitoring of sex-disaggregated data by PFIs, which will
             enable the design of any evidence-based product interventions to close the gender gap.
         o   Designing a de-risking product that takes into consideration the unique circumstances and assets that
             women may possess and offer an incentive-based system in its deployment to enhance the effectiveness
             and usage of the product. The objective with this product is to increase the number of loans disbursed by
             PFIs to female-owned value chain actors.
        o    Development of a targeted learning curriculum for female actors across the value chain (accounting for
             diverse learning needs and preferences) and customized modular courses and training programs, with an
             additional focus on the development of “soft skills’ such as confidence, personal development, and
             leadership skills.
