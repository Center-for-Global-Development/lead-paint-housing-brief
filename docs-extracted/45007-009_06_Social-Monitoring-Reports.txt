                                       EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

1.      The Government of Mongolia aims to improve living conditions in Ulaanbaatar by
developing ger areas with the implementation of the Ulaanbaatar Urban Services and Ger Areas
Development Investment Program (the Program) with the support of Asian Development Bank
(ADB) and European Investment Bank (EIB). The Program is divided into three tranches to be
implemented over a period of 13 years (2013–2026). 1 Tranche 1 and 2 support the development
of Selbe and Bayankhoshuu subcenters. In addition to this, Tranche 2 supports also other priority
subcenters such as Dambadarjaa, Denjiin Myanga; while Tranche 3 supports the development of
Tolgoit, Sharkhad, and Bayankhoshuu subcenters.

2.      Land acquisition and resettlement (LAR) for infrastructure facilities and urban
development works within subcenters under the three tranches of the Program, have been
internally and externally monitored semiannually. Program management office (PMO) is
responsible for the internal routine monitoring and external monitoring is carried out by an
independent external entity. The Urban Future Planning Consultancy Limited Liability Company
(LLC) is taking the responsibility to carry out external monitoring and evaluation for the period
starting from May 2021 and the service contract was extended on 1 July 2024.

3.     This is the semiannual external monitoring for the LAR activities conducted in the first half
of 2025, particularly under Tranches 2 and 3. Therefore, main objectives of this external
monitoring are to (i) assess the effectiveness, impact, and sustainability of resettlement measures
implemented within Tranches 2 and 3, during the period covered in the monitoring; (ii) determine
whether safeguard compliance has been met; and (iii) learn strategic lessons for future policy
formulation and planning. For the monitoring, both quantitative and qualitative (sample survey)
research methods were used and research findings have been disaggregated by gender and
vulnerability, where applicable.

4.       As of the end of June 2025, a total of 256 land parcels (of which, 207 land parcels were
fully and 49 land parcels were partially affected) with 105,558 m2 area and 352 households and
entities were affected by LAR activities carried out within Tranche 2 subcenters. 349 affected
households (AHs) were compensated, and the total amount of compensation is equal to MNT20.2
billion ($5,615,600.0). The progress of the LAR is 99.1%.

5.      For Tranche 3, a total of 379 (89.0% of total land parcels that planned to be affected) land
parcels (of which, 305 fully and 74 partially affected land parcels) and 532 (97.1% of total
households planned to be affected) households and entities were affected by the LAR activities
carried out in Tolgoit and Bayankhoshuu subcenters. Of these, 506 were households that had
negotiated. By the end of June 2025, in total 506 AHs concluded the land acquisition and
compensation contract with the project implementors and 503 of them were fully compensated.
Total compensation amount equals to MNT34.48 billion ($9,585,440.0). The progress of the LAR
is 94.5%.

6.      The results of the household sample survey, which was conducted on 148 AHs confirm
that the most of the affected peoples’ wellbeing and livelihoods were not adversely affected by
land acquisition. AHs are very satisfied with the improvement of their living environment, housing
condition and access to basic services. Beneficiaries of the livelihood program are most satisfied
about the program effectiveness. The program implementers have worked very effectively with

1.1      Introduction

9.      With the implementation of the Ulaanbaatar Urban Services and Ger Areas Development
Investment Program (the Program), the Government of Mongolia (GOM) aims to improve living
conditions in Ulaanbaatar (program impact) by developing a network of livable, competitive, and
inclusive subcenters in Ulaanbaatar’s ger areas providing economic opportunities and urban
services, leading to a healthier urban environment (project outcome).

10.    Approximately, 400,000 citizens, living in ger areas will directly benefit and the overall
population of Ulaanbaatar will indirectly benefit from the Program. 2 The total cost of the Program
is about $344.20 million 3 equivalent and is cofinanced by the Asian Development Bank (ADB)
($163.70 million or 47.6%), the GOM ($120.20 million or 34.9%) and the European Investment
Bank (EIB) ($60.3 million or 17.5%). The executing and implementing agency for the Program is
the Municipality of Ulaanbaatar (MUB).

11.      The Program is divided into three projects (three tranches) to be implemented over a
period of 13 years (2013-2026) and has four outputs: (i) roads and urban services are expanded
within priority subcenters, and connectivity between them is improved; (ii) economic and public
services in targeted areas are increased; (iii) service providers become more efficient; and (iv)
institutional strengthening and capacity building. 4

12.     Tranche 1 of the Program supports the city master plan in developing the Selbe and
Bayankhoshuu subcenters and Tranche 2 assists the GOM in expanding the coverage of similar
investments within Selbe and Bayankhoshuu subcenters and in other priority subcenters
(Dambadarjaa and Denjiin Myanga) mainly located in the northern part of the mid-ger areas. 5
Then, Tranche 3 is in line with the Program objectives to create a network of livable, competitive,
and inclusive subcenters in Ulaanbaatar’s ger areas, with similar outputs to two previous tranches.
The two additional areas covered under Tranche 3 are Tolgoit located in the west part of
Ulaanbaatar and Sharkhad located in the east part of Ulaanbaatar. Moreover, from 2022, the
Program has started to support some developments in Bayankhoshuu subcenter under Tranche

1.2     Monitoring Objectives, Methodology, and Scope

Objectives

15.     Main objectives of this external monitoring are to (i) assess the effectiveness, impact, and
sustainability of resettlement measures implemented particularly within Tranches 2 and 3 in the
first half of 2025; (ii) determine whether safeguard compliance has been met; and (iii) learn
strategic lessons for future policy formulation and planning. 11

Methodology

16.      Desk review is considered as one of the most relevant research methods in development
projects/work. It helps to understand and summarize the progress and current state of the project
with the help of analysis of documents related to that project. Therefore, ADB and GOM
documents related to the Program, the Program operational and other reports including LAR
activities carried out mostly within Tranches 2 and 3 in the first half of 2025, were reviewed in the
second half of July 2025. As a result, the monitoring team become able to summarize the Program
LAR implementation and the outputs achieved by the end of June 2025 and priority was given to
clarify the information to be collected through further research. Moreover, since the provision of
social housing for Tranche 1 non-titled AHs is still ongoing, the progress information of this activity
has also been reviewed during this external monitoring.

17.     Next method used in this external monitoring was the sample survey method. Specifically,
with the help of simple random sampling technique whether the objectives of mitigating potential
negative impacts on the affected persons (APs), specifically AHs have been met, and whether
livelihood and living standards have been restored at least to pre-project level or enhanced,
including those of non-titled displaced persons, were determined.

18.     This time, sampling units were selected from AHs in Tranche 3 with the intention to select
as many households as possible that had not been previously sampled and surveyed. According
to the renewed appendix of extended consultancy service contract, 12 which was established
between the PMO and the Urban Future Planning Consultancy LLC, on 1 July 2024, the
households and entities affected by LAR activities that were conducted under Tranches 2 and 3,
and negotiated and contracted with the project implementors is equal to 500 (See Appendix 2).
50 of them belong to Tranche 2 and they were surveyed during the previous external monitorings.
The remaining 450 AHs belong to Tranche 3, and they were considered as population for this
sample survey. If the population size is small, random sampling provides the best results since
all population units have equal chance of being selected, and according to the terms of
reference 13 (TOR) for the external monitoring and evaluation team, it was advised to randomly
sample at least 20% of total APs in LAR activities. Consequently, 148 households were sampled,
which is 32% of the population.

19.     Therefore, with a sample of 148 AHs (there were 53 (35.8%) male and 95 (64.2%) female
respondents), each sampled household represents 3 APs in the population. Since, we have
employed a simple random sampling technique, this sample weight is equal for all units of the
sample. If all the weights are equal, then the weighted mean of interested character of population
is the same as the simple arithmetic mean or sample mean. Therefore, all estimations were done
without taking sample weights into account (see Chapter 4 and Appendix 4 for household survey
results).

20.      Structured interview questionnaire was used in data collection for sample survey. The
following key indicators were used for the development of questionnaire: (i) economic status of
APs (specifically AHs), including employment, income and household assets; (ii) status of
vulnerable persons, in terms of food security, household demographics, livelihoods and health;
(iii) impact of land acquisition on women, children, elderly, the poor and other vulnerable groups;
(iv) degree of support for and post land acquisition status of affected vulnerable households; (v)
perceptions of affected entities regarding the implementation of the land acquisition process; (vi)
participation and involvement of affected entities in LARP implementation; (vii) effectiveness and
fairness of valuation, compensation, assessment and disbursement measures; (viii)
implementation and effectiveness of income restoration measures; (ix) effectiveness and fairness
of Grievance Redress Mechanism (GRM); (x) level of satisfaction among affected entities in the
post-resettlement period; (xi) adequacy of resettlement funds and results of financial audits (see
Appendix 8 for household sample survey questionnaire).

was initially formulated and approved in 2013, and updated in 2016 and in 2020, respectively.

1.3     Monitoring Process

24.      In order to accomplish the above-mentioned objectives, the external monitoring and
evaluation team of the Company performed the following activities mentioned in the TOR for its
consultancy service: (i) review and verification of the internal monitoring reports of the PMO; (ii)
identification and selection of impact indicators; (iii) impact assessment through quantitative and
qualitative surveys; (iv) consultation with local stakeholders; (v) assessment of compliance with
local laws, the ADB Safeguard Policy Statement (SPS) (2009), resettlement framework and the
LARPs; (vi) recommendation of land acquisition process modification and adaptation measures;
(vii) lessons learned for future land acquisition and resettlement policy formulation and planning.

25.     The external monitoring and evaluation team implemented above activities in three
phases. In the first phase, the activities numbered one and two above were carried out.
Specifically, ADB, GOM and PMO documents and reports (internal monitoring reports, LARPs
and due diligence reports [DDRs]) were reviewed and analyzed in the second half of July 2025.
As a result, the activities implemented by the Program regarding the LAR have been summarized,
and the information to be clarified further has become clear. At the same time, the monitoring
methodology, the scope and size of the sample were determined in detail, the tools used for
quantitative and qualitative research were developed, and the monitoring process was planned.

26.     The next phase of the external monitoring involved data collection from primary sources
through quantitative and qualitative research (the activities numbered three and four above were
carried out). The work was completed at this phase, when the information/data collected was
sorted, checked, and prepared for analysis. The activities of this phase were carried out in August
2025.

110. This is the semiannual external monitoring for the land acquisition and resettlement
activities conducted in the first half of 2025, mainly under Tranches 2 and 3. Main objectives of
this external monitoring were to (i) assess the effectiveness, impact, and sustainability of
resettlement measures implemented within Tranches 2 and 3 during the period covered in the
monitoring; (ii) determine whether Safeguard compliance has been met; and (iii) learn strategic
lessons for future policy formulation and planning. Since, the provision of social housing to
Tranche 1 non-titled AHs is still ongoing, its progress was also monitored during this external
monitoring.

111. The land acquisition works carried out in these tranches were carried out in line with the
provisions of the resettlement documents (LARPs, DDRs) which have been approved on the basis
of the LARF, that was initially formulated and approved in 2013, and updated in 2016 and in 2020,
respectively. Moreoever, land acquisition activities were in accordance with the requirements of
the ADB safequard policy and the laws and regulations of Mongolia. The PMO officers and other
parties involved in the planning and implementation of LAR activities have been actively working
to provide the necessary support to the APs and to prevent the violation of the rights of the APs
in advance.

112. As of the end of June 2025, in total 256 land parcels (of which, 207 land parcels were fully
and 49 land parcels were partially affected) with 105,558 m2 area and 352 households and entities
were affected by LAR activities carried out within Tranche 2 subcenters. 349 AHs were
compensated, and the total amount of compensation is equal to MNT20.2 billion ($5,615,600.0).
The progress of the LAR is 99.1%. There was 1 state enterprise (operates a water well) and its
land parcel was vacated without negotiation, contract and compensation. 1 household with land
parcel number HSSC-C08 does not live on its affected land parcel and could not be contacted to
negotiate about the LAR, therefore was not compensated. For another 1 household (DDRI-32),
although this household has signed the land acquisition contract, the compensation paying is still
pending because of its family dispute in court.

113. For Tranche 3, in total 379 (89.0% of total land parcels that planned to be affected) land
parcels (of which, 305 fully and 74 partially affected land parcels) and 532 (97.1% of total
households planned to be affected) households and entities were affected by the LAR activities
carried out in Tolgoit and Bayankhoshuu subcenters. Of these, 506 were households that had
negotiated. By the end of June 2025, in total 506 AHs concluded the land acquisition and
compensation contract with the project implementors and 503 of them were fully compensated.
Total compensation amount equals to MNT34.48 billion ($9,585,440.0). The progress of the LAR
is 94.5%. For a land parcel numbered as TFP-2 was possessed by a state-owned enterprise was
acquired without negotiation and an owner of another 1 land parcel has not been found yet.
Moreover, for 19 land parcels’ negotiations will be done during construction work and due to the
incomplete documentation, negotiation and contracting processes for 2 AHs, were delayed. For
the remaining 3 households, since the land rights were granted after the announcement of project
cut-off date, the LMA decided not to approve contracts and provide compensations. These 3
households were without land rights and were not living on affected land parcels.

114. The results of the household sample survey, which was conducted on 148 AHs confirm
that the most of the affected peoples’ wellbeing and livelihoods were not adversely affected by
land acquisition. AHs are very satisfied with the improvement of their living environment, housing
condition and access to basic services. Beneficiaries of the livelihood program are most satisfied
about the program effectiveness. The program implementers have worked very effectively with

relevant parties to provide rent-to-own housing for both eligible and ineligible non-titled AHs. The
demand for the program is relatively high, and it has been agreed to provide rent-to-own housing

rights have fully exercised their rights as specified in resettlement documents and received full

115. In general, land acquisition activities under the Program are approaching to its completion,

Background

1.       Ulaanbaatar city was home to more than 1.4 million people as of 2015. The city’s core is
largely comprised of apartment blocks served by comprehensive utility services, including district
heating, piped water supply, and wastewater collection and treatment. Surrounding the core is a
vast and low-density peri-urban area (so-called ger areas) with poor infrastructure that
accommodates about 60% of the city’s population (about 840,000 inhabitants). 50 Ger areas have
spread and expanded through successive waves of migration. 51 These low-and medium-income
house hold settlements are characterized by loosely aligned plots, creating irregular path ways
that remain unpaved. 52 The majority of households have land tenure. A lack of basic urban
services affects the ger area residents' health and living conditions through (i) severe air pollution
generated by unimproved individual coal stoves used by the majority of residents during
winter; 53 (ii) poor sanitation from almost exclusive reliance on open pit latrines; (iii) poor solid
waste collection; (iv) limited access to portable water (supplied by water kiosks); 54 and (v) traffic
congestion caused by unmanaged road network. Finally, ger areas have a critical lack of public
space, public facilities, commercial areas, and economic opportunities.

2.       Investment program. On 12 December 2013, the Asian Development Bank (ADB)
approved the Ulaanbaatar Urban Services and Ger Areas Development Investment Program
(GADIP) using the multitranche financing facility (MFF) modality. The program's framework
financing agreement was signed on 9 December 2013 and ratified by the Mongolian Parliament
on 23 May 2014. The program supports the Ulaanbaatar city master plan in upgrading priority
services and developing economic hubs (subcenters) in ger areas. The implementation period is
9 years (December 2013–December 2022) and comprises of three tranches. The program is
geographically targeted with multisector interventions. Responding to the urgent demand for basic
urban services and the ongoing densification, it proposes an integrated solution to initiate a
redevelopment process in ger areas and develop urban subcenters as catalysts for growth.
Improving infrastructure within the ger area subcenters is critical for the city’s inclusive
development. Better urban planning combined with infrastructure along priority roads will initiate
the structural change of subcenters. This will (i) improve residents’ access to basic urban services,
public space, and socioeconomic facilities; (ii) support local economic development; (iii) allow
residents and businesses to take advantage of urban economies; and (iv) provide better housing
options. The changes in land use and urban density will improve efficiency and financial
sustainability of water, sanitation, and heating services delivery. The first tranche is financing
priority infrastructure, facilities, and capacity building to develop the Selbe and Bayankhoshuu
subcenters. The second and third tranche will finance additional investments in subcenters under
Tranche 1, and extend investments in priority infrastructure, public facilities, and capacity building
to develop Denjiin Myanga and Dambadarjaa subcenters (Tranche 2), and Tolgoit and Sharkhad
subcenters (Tranche 3), respectively. For more information about the project, including information
on poverty, social and gender dimensions, environment and resettlement, please refer to the ADB

3.       Project implementation arrangements. Municipality of Ulaanbaatar (MUB), the
executing agency of GADIP, has Planning and Strategic Department that is responsible for
coordinating the program implementation. Program management office (PMO) has been
established under the first deputy vice mayor in charge of development policy. A program
steering committee, chaired by the mayor of Ulaanbaatar and comprised of government
officials, has been established to provide strategic and policy guidance. Under the PMO, two
consultancy services were hired to support the implementation of tranche 2, (i) T2-CS01:
Consultancy services for detailed engineering design and supervision; and (ii) T2-CS02:
Consultancy services for institutional strengthening and capacity building.

4.       The construction of roads and infrastructures, and public facilities in Tranche 2 and
Tranche 3 subcenters of GADIP, Dambadarjaa and DenjiinMyanga (tranche 2), and Tolgoit and
Sharkhad (tranche 3) are to start from 2021 and the land acquisition and resettlement (LAR)
activities are starting from 2020. It is vital for GADIP to commence the independent external
monitoring and evaluation of LAR activities to ensure the compliance of LAR activities with ADB
Safeguard Policy Statement (SPS), 2009, especially its requirements on Involuntary
Resettlement, to protect the rights and interests of the people affected by land acquisition and
resettlement. This terms of reference (TOR) is prepared for selection and recruitment of
consultancy services for LAR external monitoring and evaluation. The details of the consultancy
services are provided in the sections below.

5.      Purpose. External monitoring and evaluation (EME) will be conducted to (i) assess the
effectiveness, impact and sustainability of land acquisition and resettlement activities; (ii)
determine whether safeguard compliance has been met; and (iii) learn strategic lessons for future
policy formulation and planning related to land acquisition and resettlement (LAR).

6.       Methodology. Monitoring and evaluation methodologies used, but were not limited to, the
following: desk review and field visits; use of structured questionnaires; meetings with
stakeholders such as departments and organizations of Municipality of Ulaanbaatar (MUB), local
officials, and affected households, and other relevant stakeholders; focus group discussions; and
key informant interviews. Separate meetings have been held with representatives of women and
vulnerable households. Monitoring indicators and findings have been disaggregated by gender,
when applicable. Households who moved outside the ger areas or those who are in temporary
housing within or outside the ger areas have been covered in the external monitoring.

7.       External monitoring has been carried out for all household (HH) and entities affected by
public facilities and as well as randomly selected households and entities affected by roads and
infrastructures. Random sampling has been done at least on 20 percent of all affected households
(AH) and entities affected by roads and infrastructure. Moreover, the consultant team consulted
with stakeholders involved in LAR implementation such Resettlement Working group (RWG),
Program Management Office (PMO), Land Management Agency of Ulaanbaatar (LAM) , khoroo
and kheseg leaders and Capacity building for Sustainable Livelihood Support in Ger Areas
Transaction Technical Assistance (TRTA), which is providing livelihood restoration support to
eligible affected households.

8.    Scope of work. LAR external monitoring shall be carried out for all subprojects being
implemented under Tranche 1 and for all packages considered in Tranche 2 and Tranche 3.

Purpose of interview: To discuss the implementation, results, sustainability, and alignment with

3 of the USGADIP, as well as the participation of stakeholders in the project.

Questions and Answers

Q 1: Please provide a brief overview of the land acquisition and resettlement activities carried out
under the project during the first half of 2025.
A: Land acquisition activities under our project have been largely completed. Under Tranche 3,
land was acquired from a small number of households in Tolgoit affected by engineering utility
works. Construction works under Tranche 3 are in progress. The construction of the Tolgoit
Business Incubator building has been completed and is now in the process of being handed over
to the commissioning committee.

Q 2: We would like to discuss whether the activities were consistent with the ADB Safeguard
Policy.
A: Throughout the entire implementation period, we have complied with the requirements of the
ADB Safeguard Policy, preparing and implementing the necessary land acquisition
documentation accordingly. In December 2023, the “Law on Reducing Traffic Congestion in the
Capital City and Redeveloping Ger Areas into Apartment Housing” was enacted. In connection
with the implementation of this law, relevant regulations for its enforcement have been issued.
The PMO, based on our previous experience, submitted recommendations to incorporate certain
provisions of the ADB Safeguard Policy into the said regulation, which were accepted. These
include not applying depreciation to affected assets; valuing land at market rates depending on
location; and, prior to land acquisition, not only issuing a “Notice” but also establishing a “Cut-off
Date” for eligibility to receive assistance, which we consider a significant improvement. In addition,
we recommended addressing the issue of adjacent households, ensuring social protection
measures for vulnerable groups, and providing either land allocation for fully affected households
or inclusion in rent-to-own housing projects.

Q 3: Were there any complaints or grievances related to land acquisition this year?
A: There was one case in which the Selbe Sergelt Project was mistakenly perceived as a
continuation of our Ger Area Project, and a complaint was submitted to the ADB. The Selbe
Sergelt Project is a separate project aimed at constructing housing for a total of 12,000
households, and it has its own PIU. However, some NGOs filed complaints incorrectly, suggesting
that information had been withheld and linking it to our project. Under this project, over 2,200


                                                 80
households in Selbe were relocated within a short period. Currently, around 200 households have
not yet relocated, and we understand that some are requesting significant compensation.
The Land Acquisition Department of the Land management Agency conducted this large-scale
relocation in accordance with the “Law on Reducing Traffic Congestion in the Capital City and
Redeveloping Ger Areas into Apartment Housing.” Going forward, all land acquisition activities in
the capital will follow this law. Regarding the Dambadarjaa subcenter road project, several
complaints were received claiming that the road encroached too closely on household fences.
Upon investigation, it was found that these households had extended their fences by 3–5 meters
into the space of neighboring households who had already relocated, thereby moving closer to
the road. Since their neighbors had already vacated their land, extending their own fences
constitutes a failure to fulfill their contractual obligations.

Q 4: Have all AHs moved into the new rent-to own-apartment?
A: According to the General Financing Agreement between the Government of Mongolia and the
Asian Development Bank (ADB), and based on consultations between the ADB and the Capital
City Governor’s Office (CCGO), the “General Documentation on Land Acquisition and
Resettlement” for the “New SubCenter” project specifies that households are provided with
apartments either for rent at government-subsidized rates or through a rent-to-own scheme at
government-subsidized rates. In accordance with this requirement, 126 apartments were
constructed in the 6th Khoroo of Songino Khairkhan District using ADB loan funds. Based on the
record of the 15th meeting of the Capital City Governor’s Council in 2024, the Capital City
Governor’s Office issued Order No. A/1532 in 2024, granting approval to allocate apartments to
a total of 75 residents (AHs).

As of now, out of the 75 residents eligible for the rent-to-own scheme, 57 have signed agreements
and moved into their apartments. Ten residents declined participation due to reasons such as
having existing mortgage loans, dissatisfaction with the apartment location, or high monthly
payments. Eight residents expressed interest but did not meet the requirements—for example,
their debt-to-income ratio was insufficient, they were listed on the Mongol Bank’s blacklist, they
had large outstanding loans from banks, or they lacked a co-borrower—and were therefore
excluded. Other reasons also led to some exclusions.

Regarding the eight residents who did not meet the eligibility requirements for these apartments,
as well as eight residents who filed complaints through the ADB-OSPF via the Zurgan Buudal
NGO, the ADB Resident Mission recommended that those who did not meet the payment
requirements be accommodated in the apartments under a rental scheme. Following this
recommendation, the issue was presented, and a decision was made at the 2025 PSC meeting.

A: No comments.
