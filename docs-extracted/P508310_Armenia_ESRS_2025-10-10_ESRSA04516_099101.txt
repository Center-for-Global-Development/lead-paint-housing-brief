                     C. Summary Description of Proposed Project Activities
                     The Housing Response and Mortgage Market Enhancement (HOME) Project seeks to improve refugees' access to
                     housing solutions and enable deepening of the housing and mortgage market. The project instrument is Investment
                     Project Financing (IPF) with Performance-Based Conditions (PBCs), organized around three components as follows: (i)
                     Housing subsidies and support for institutional development, (ii) Housing finance market development, and (iii) Project
                     management, communications, monitoring, and evaluation. Component 1 will provide housing solutions to eligible
                     beneficiaries, which will simultaneously increase their resilience towards increasing climate risks such as extreme heat

                     and floods. The component will also seek to enhance the institutional capacities of the Government of Armenia (GoA)
                     and its relevant agencies for designing, establishing, implementing, and managing future national housing sector policy
                     and programs. Subcomponent 1.1 will finance the provision of cash assistance (CA) to eligible beneficiaries supported by
                     the ongoing Social Assistance Program (SAP). Subcomponent 1.2 will finance direct subsidies to eligible refugee
                     households (HHs) under the Government Housing Support Program (HSP) for the acquisition of housing solutions
                     through participating financial institutions (PFIs). This subcomponent will finance the provision of: (i) up-front
                     downpayment assistance of up to 30 percent of the housing purchase price, and (ii) assistance for full monthly
                     mortgage payments for up to the first five years of 10-year mortgage loans. Subcomponent 1.3 will finance
                     procurement of goods, consulting, and non-consulting services for technical assistance (TA), advisory, and capacity
                     building to support institutional strengthening and policy reforms at the national level in the housing sector as well as
                     improvements to the HSP’s management information system managed by the Ministry of Labor and Social Affairs
                     (MLSA). Component 2 will support the National Mortgage Company (NMC) operations by enhancing long-term liquidity
                     for mortgage-originating financial institutions. This component aims to expand a sustainable housing finance system by
                     providing longer-term local currency funding, essential for affordable mortgage markets. A line of credit (LOC) extended
                     by the project to the NMC will increase access to mortgage loans by providing refinancing for PFIs, i.e., commercial
                     banks with signed agreements with the GoA to provide mortgage loans under the HSP. Component 3 will finance the
                     procurement of consulting and non-consulting services, goods, training, and overall operating costs to support the
                     project implementing team (PIT) under MLSA in project management, communications, and implementation activities,
                     including for, but not limited to, monitoring and evaluation, reporting, audits, independent verification of PBCs,
                     procurement, financial management, environmental and social management, feedback and grievance redress
                     mechanism (FGRM), citizen engagement activities including beneficiary surveys, and project communication outreach

                     D. Environmental and Social Overview
                     D.1 Overview of Environmental and Social Project Settings
                     Project activities will be implemented nationwide, targeting refugees who arrived in Armenia after 2020 and in
                     September 2023, estimated at 13,000 - 15,000 HHs eligible for refugee HSP, while the broader refugee population are
                     benefiting from temporary CA for rent, utilities, and consumption. The refugee HSP is demand-driven and beneficiary-
                     led, allowing to choose and settle anywhere in the country except central Yerevan, where demand is very high.

                     Armenia’s aging housing stock is primarily privately owned and, as of 2024, includes over 460,000 apartment units and
                     437,000 private houses, most of which built before 1988 under outdated Soviet building codes and with relatively old
                     infrastructure. Approximately 60% of the housing is in urban, with 30% concentrated in Yerevan, where infrastructure,
                     jobs and social services are more accessible, while rural and near-border areas often face limitations. Nationwide,
                     according to estimations, about 55,000-60,000 units are vacant, indicating average vacancy rate of around 6%, primary
                     outside of Yerevan with some regions reporting vacancy rates of 30-40%. In some rural locations, given the nationwide
                     scope, private houses may be situated near natural protected zones or within buffer zones. The aging housing stock may
                     also include buildings of historical significance. However, the construction of individual houses and reliance on existing
                     housing are expected to use current infrastructure and take place in developed settings, thereby minimizing proximity
                     to natural or cultural protected zones and excluding greenfield development, as well as developer-financed housing
                     investments. None of the activities will be implemented in conflict affected/disputed territories.

                     Currently, most refugees are concentrated in Yerevan and nearby towns and villages, with the vast majority residing in
                     rented housing or accommodated by relatives. No refugee tent camps or other problematic temporary solutions have
                     been established, allowing refugees to maintain their mobility across the country and settle where family ties,
                     employment, or other opportunities are available. The HSP defines a minimum standard apartment size of 9 m² per
                     capita, and incentivizes housing in border regions, where communities are more vulnerable and face limited job
                     opportunities/infrastructure. Because the maximum benefit under the housing program is capped, many refugees may
                     only be able to afford housing in rural areas or in less adequate housing stock in peri-urban settlements surrounding
                     Yerevan. These locations are generally less densely populated, often offering lower housing costs, but they also tend to
                     have fewer services and amenities. At the same time, they are still connected to Yerevan, which remains the main hub
                     for employment and access to social services. Incentives for settlement in border areas—while aligned with government
                     regional development priorities—carry additional risks linked to proximity to fragile borders and more limited access to
                     markets, jobs, and services.

                     Both the provision of CA and the HPS are fully digitalized procedures, designed to minimize administrative burden and
                     the need for in-person interactions. CA is provided to beneficiaries based on the Migration and Citizenship Services
                     (MCS) database and beneficiary lists maintained by the MLSA. Beneficiaries were registered upon arrival in the country
                     and beneficiary databases are managed by Unified Social Service (USS) of the MLSA. Transfers are made directly by the
                     MLSA to beneficiaries’ registered bank accounts and all transactions are processed digitally, without requiring physical
                     contact with ministry or USS staff. Under Government HSP, eligible refugee HHs apply online at MLSA website and are
                     issued digital Housing Purchase Certificates (HPC) after an automated eligibility check and document verification
                     process. Afterwards beneficiaries apply to PFIs that verify their eligibility, conduct due diligence on the property, and

                     D.2 Overview of Borrower’s Institutional Capacity for Managing Environmental and Social Risks and Impacts
                     The Project will have two loan agreements: one with the MLSA, serving as the main counterpart for Component 1 and
                     3, and with NMC responsible for Component 2.

                     To implement Component 1 and 3 activities, the MLSA will establish a PIT, drawing on the project management,
                     monitoring, and evaluation support provided under Component 3. The PIT will include a dedicated E&S specialist and a
                     Stakeholder Engagement (SE) and Communications specialist, to be hired or assigned within the MLSA, to support
                     project implementation. The Project will also involve the USS of the MLSA, with its 49 territorial centers, which are
                     responsible for the HSP/CA. These centers engage directly with beneficiaries at the local level and will contribute to the
                     Project through beneficiary outreach and HSP communications. The existing territorial centers network of the USS,
                     together with the MLSA’s national 114-hotline, will also serve as the primary entry points for project-related grievances.
                     The capacities of the USS and the 114-hotline need to be further strengthened and enhanced to handle the additional
                     caseloads related to the HSP, to manage sensitive grievances related to Sexual Exploitation and Abuse/Sexual
                     Harassment (SEA/SH), and to ensure systematic data disaggregation, case tracking, and reporting.

                     Armenia’ experience of developing and operating Environmental and Social Management Systems (ESMSs) is limited.
                     While government agencies such as the MLSA has prior experience with WB-financed projects under the Safeguard
                     Policies and, more recently, the Environmental and Social Framework (ESF), this experience has primarily focused on
                     project-level safeguards compliance rather than the systematic development of ESMS. In particular, MLSA’s capacity to
                     support financial intermediaries (FIs) and to implement ESMS remains limited.

                     While stand-alone Environmental and Social Commitment Plans (ESCPs) will be prepared for each loan agreement, with

                     B.3 Other Salient Features

                       Use of Borrower Framework                                                                                         In Part
                       The Borrower’s framework will be partially used for the Project to assess and ensure that the management of E&S
                       risks and impacts of the Project, specifically labor management and working conditions of Project workers, waste and
                       OHS management measures, community health and safety, including SEA/SH, cultural heritage risks and impacts, FIs,
                       OHS requirement and grievance redress mechanisms are consistent with the requirements of ESSs 2, 3, 4, 8, 9, and 10,
                       with the Borrower’s E&S and labor framework, which includes, inter alia, the country´s relevant policy, legal and
                       institutional framework.

                       Additional gap-filling measures, based on the findings and recommendations of the Analysis of Gaps between National
                       Legislation of Armenia and the WB ESF (2023), to maintain alignment with the ESSs will include: (i) for ESS1, an E&S
                       risk screening and exclusion criteria procedures have been developed to exclude activities that might be classified as
                       substantial or high risk, and to identify risks and impacts requiring management and monitoring under the ESMS; (ii)
                       for ESS2, CoCs for Project workers will be adopted and implemented, and workers grievance mechanism will be
                       established; (iii) for ESS3, the E&S risk screening and exclusion criteria will also screen out activities with potential
                       exposure to, or requiring management of, hazardous materials; (iv) for ESS4, the E&S risk screening and exclusion
                       criteria will screen out activities that might impose risks and impacts to the communities, including traffic and road
                       safety, and SEA/SH preventive actions will be implemented, proportionate to the risks and impacts and (v) for ESS9,
                       the MLSA will develop an ESMS based on the E&S risk screening tool and proportionate to the level of E&S risks and

                       Use of Common Approach                                                                                                 No
                       The Bank will partner with the Asian Development Bank (ADB) to jointly support the Project. The Bank intends to use
                       the IPF instrument, while the ADB will apply its Program-Based Lending (PBL) instrument, which is broadly equivalent
                       to the Bank’s Program-for-Results (PforR). Under the ADB-financed Program, safeguard arrangements will be guided
                       by a Program Safeguards System Assessment (PSSA). Given that the two institutions are applying different financing
                       instruments, a common approach will not be pursued. However, the Bank will closely coordinate with ADB to ensure
                       alignment where possible, avoid duplication of efforts, particularly in areas such as SE, monitoring, and reporting, and
                       maintain consistency of objectives across the two operations.
